## Summary

### Top 3 Most Impactful New Findings

1. **Finding 1 (Adams & Yellen 1976 + Schmalensee 1984)**: Bundling works by reducing heterogeneity in customers' willingness-to-pay. When customers value individual items differently, a single bundle price can extract more total surplus than item-by-item pricing — the mathematical foundation that explains why bundles capture revenue that itemized pricing leaves on the table.
2. **Finding 2 (Stremersch & Tellis 2002)**: The strategic distinction between *price bundling* (combining items at a discount) and *product bundling* (combining items functionally) matters — each serves different strategic goals, and conflating them leads to suboptimal outcomes. Product bundling with complementary items is the highest-value e-commerce implementation.
3. **Finding 3 (Janiszewski & Cunha 2004)**: How bundle discounts are *presented* — as overall savings vs. item-level discounts — significantly changes perceived value. Aggregate savings displays outperform itemized savings for complementary bundles, but itemized savings outperform for disparate item sets.

## Findings

### Finding 1: The Economics of Bundle Pricing — Consumer Surplus Extraction
- **Source**: Adams, W.J., & Yellen, J.L. (1976). "Commodity Bundling and the Burden of Monopoly." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 90(3), 475–498. <https://doi.org/10.2307/1886045> — Extended by: Schmalensee, R. (1984). "Gaussian Demand and Commodity Bundling." *Journal of Business*, 57(1), S211–S230. <https://doi.org/10.1086/296229>
- **Methodology**: Adams & Yellen: Theoretical economics analysis using utility functions and reservation price distributions to show conditions under which bundling outperforms separate selling. Mathematical proof with illustrative examples. Schmalensee: Extended the Adams-Yellen framework to show that bundling becomes most profitable when customers have *negatively correlated* valuations across items — a key condition for e-commerce practitioners.
- **Key Finding**: Bundling is profitable when customers have heterogeneous (varied) willingness-to-pay for individual items AND when the bundle can serve as a "price fence" that allows a single price to capture customers across the valuation distribution. Schmalensee's critical insight: bundling is most valuable when different customers value different items highly — the customer who values A but not B, and the customer who values B but not A, both end up buying the bundle when the bundle price is set optimally. Negatively correlated item valuations = ideal bundling conditions. Positively correlated item valuations = bundling adds little.
- **E-Commerce Application**: The best candidates for bundling are products where different customer segments value them differently. Classic examples: camera + camera bag (photographers value camera highly, casual buyers value bag; values are negatively correlated). Phone case + screen protector (case buyers may not care about screen protectors, and vice versa). Avoid bundling items that ALL customers value equally — there's no surplus to extract. Use customer segment analysis to identify which product combinations have diverse valuation distributions.
- **Replication Status**: Foundational economic theory with 1,000+ citations. Extended by numerous economists and marketing researchers. The conditions for profitable bundling are well-established in theory; empirical validation in specific retail contexts varies.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The model assumes firms have market power sufficient to set bundle prices. In highly competitive markets with easy unbundling alternatives, bundle pricing power diminishes. If customers can easily buy substitutes individually at lower prices, the bundle falls apart. Digital goods are strong bundling candidates because customers can't easily source substitutes, and marginal cost of adding items is near zero.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (foundational peer-reviewed economics; extensively cited and built upon)

### Finding 2: Price Bundling vs. Product Bundling — Strategic Distinction
- **Source**: Stremersch, S., & Tellis, G.J. (2002). "Strategic Bundling of Products and Prices: A New Synthesis for Marketing." *Journal of Marketing*, 66(1), 55–72. <https://doi.org/10.1509/jmkg.66.1.55.18455>
- **Methodology**: Conceptual synthesis paper reviewing 40+ years of bundling research across economics, operations research, and marketing. Developed a typology of bundling forms with empirical examples from multiple industries. Not a primary experiment — a framework paper synthesizing the existing literature into actionable strategic guidance.
- **Key Finding**: Distinguishes two fundamentally different strategies: (1) **Price bundling** — selling two or more separate products at a combined discount (e.g., "buy both for $79, save $21"); (2) **Product bundling** — integrating products so they become a new combined entity (e.g., a "starter kit" that functions as a unit). Price bundling is primarily a pricing tool; product bundling is a product strategy. Mixed bundling (offering the bundle AND the items individually) outperforms pure bundling for most e-commerce contexts because it captures both bundle-preferring and single-item-preferring customers. Stremersch & Tellis find that mixed price bundling consistently outperforms pure bundling in revenue generation.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Most e-commerce "bundles" are price bundles — discount for buying together. This is strategically appropriate but leaves value on the table vs. true product bundling (e.g., a curated skincare routine packaged as a "Complete Morning Routine"). True product bundles command price premiums, reduce comparison shopping, and create barriers to substitution. Invest in product bundling (curated sets, starter kits) for high-margin product lines; use price bundling for accessory cross-sell. Always offer mixed bundling (individual products still available) unless the product bundle genuinely cannot be decomposed.
- **Replication Status**: Framework paper with 2,000+ Google Scholar citations. Widely adopted in marketing strategy literature. The mixed > pure bundling finding is supported by multiple empirical studies.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Product bundling requires genuine functional integration or strong curation value — consumers resist bundles where the integration feels arbitrary. Price bundling is only effective when the discount is perceptible (minimum 15%, ideally 20–25%; see CRO Finding 14). Pure bundling works for services with high switchover costs (software suites, telecom packages) but typically backfires in e-commerce where customers can order individual items from competitors.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (high-impact synthesis paper; mixed bundling > pure bundling finding has empirical support; primary framework is conceptual)

### Finding 3: Bundle Discount Presentation — Aggregate vs. Itemized Savings
- **Source**: Janiszewski, C., & Cunha, M., Jr. (2004). "The Influence of Price Discount Framing on the Evaluation of a Product Bundle." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 30(4), 534–546. <https://doi.org/10.1086/380287>
- **Methodology**: Six studies (grouped into four primary experiments in the article body) testing how the framing of bundle discounts affects perceived value. Compared aggregate savings presentation ("Save $20 on the bundle") vs. itemized savings presentation ("Save $8 on Item A, $12 on Item B") across complementary and non-complementary product sets. Sample: undergraduate students; N = 80–140 per experiment. Controlled for actual discount magnitude.
- **Key Finding**: For **complementary product bundles** (items that go together naturally), aggregate savings framing ("Bundle saves $20") yields higher perceived value and purchase intent than itemized savings framing. Customers perceive the complementary bundle as a unified entity — breaking it into parts reduces the gestalt value. For **non-complementary bundles** (disparate items that don't obviously belong together), itemized savings framing outperforms aggregate, because customers evaluate each item individually and need to see savings on items they actually want. The framing effect is driven by how consumers mentally represent the bundle — as a unit vs. as separate items.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For thematically coherent bundles (skincare routines, camera kits, outfit coordinates), use aggregate savings: "Complete Kit — $79 (Save $21!)". Do NOT show item-level discounts. For cross-category promotions or bundles combining unrelated items (e.g., a sale promotion combining a shirt + kitchen gadget), show per-item savings so customers can evaluate what they're getting on each piece they actually want. Test which framing applies to your specific bundle composition.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated within the paper across six studies (structured as four primary experiments). Cited 300+ times. Consistent with gestalt processing and mental accounting theory (Thaler, 1999). No recent large-scale direct replication found, but the theoretical mechanism is robust.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires clear product complementarity perception — if customers don't perceive the items as going together, the "treat as a unit" processing doesn't activate for aggregate framing. Category knowledge moderates: experts may process bundles more analytically regardless of framing. Effect size is meaningful in lab settings; real-world magnitude may be smaller.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JCR, four experiments, controlled for discount magnitude)

### Finding 4: BYOB (Build-Your-Own Bundle) and Customization Value
<!-- Audit Note (2026-04-22): Hildebrand et al. 2013 removed — confirmed miscitation (paper is about community feedback on self-designed products, not BYOB complexity). - **Source**: Dellaert, B.G.C., & Stremersch, S. (2005). "Marketing Mass-Customized Products: Striking a Balance Between Utility and Complexity." *Journal of Marketing Research*, 42(2), 219–227. <https://doi.org/10.1509/jmkr.42.2.219.62293> — Complementary support: Iyengar, S.S., & Lepper, M.R. (2000). "When Choice Is Demotivating: Can One Desire Too Much of a Good Thing?" *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 79(6), 995–1006. <https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.79.6.995>
- **Methodology**: Dellaert & Stremersch: Empirical study of consumer responses to product configuration complexity. Experiment testing utility vs. complexity tradeoff in build-to-order settings (mass-customized PC configurations). N = 159 consumer panel. Measured purchase intent, satisfaction, and decision difficulty across different levels of configuration complexity. Iyengar & Lepper: Series of experiments demonstrating that presenting too many options (24 vs. 6 jam varieties) reduces engagement and purchase; foundational choice-overload evidence.
- **Key Finding**: Build-your-own bundle (BYOB) systems — "Pick any 3 for $X" — generate higher engagement and customization-driven satisfaction, but only up to a moderate level of complexity. Dellaert & Stremersch find that as the number of configuration options increases, decision difficulty rises faster than perceived utility in mass-customization product configuration. The widely-cited 3–5 slot optimum for BYOB bundles is a **practitioner extrapolation** from this PC-configuration mass-customization data (not a directly measured BYOB-bundle result); the underlying principle — that complexity rises faster than utility beyond a moderate option count — is well-supported. Iyengar & Lepper's choice overload work corroborates the direction. The key tradeoff: BYOB maximizes perceived autonomy and satisfaction with choice, but requires careful constraint of the option set.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Implement BYOB bundles with 3–5 curated slots from a pre-screened product pool, not open-ended product search. Step-by-step selection UX (choose item 1 → choose item 2 → choose item 3) reduces cognitive load vs. a grid of all possible items. Show running savings total updating in real-time ("Your bundle saves $18 so far"). Cap at 5 items max. Present BYOB as a constrained curation exercise ("Pick 3 bestsellers") rather than open customization. Works best for replenishment categories (supplements, coffee, skincare) where customers know what they want. Test your specific slot count — the 3–5 range is a starting hypothesis, not a universal law.
- **Replication Status**: BYOB complexity-utility tradeoff is consistent with the choice overload literature (Iyengar & Lepper, 2000). Dellaert & Stremersch finding replicated directionally in mass customization research. Specific BYOB bundle implementation effects (as distinct from product-configuration complexity) are not formally studied as of 2026.
- **Boundary Conditions**: BYOB systems add UX complexity and development cost. They work best when: (a) products are reasonably interchangeable (all the same category), (b) customers have prior category knowledge, (c) decision process is enjoyable not stressful. BYOB fails when: products require complex comparison (high-consideration goods), option set is poorly curated, or the UX is buggy/confusing.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (Dellaert & Stremersch is peer-reviewed JMR; specific BYOB e-commerce applications are practitioner-inferred from the underlying complexity and choice-overload research)

### Finding 5: Hedonic vs. Utilitarian Bundle Framing
<!-- Audit Note (2026-04-22): NEW finding — Run B proposal accepted by reconciler. Verified via WebSearch 2026-04-21. Directly extends Finding 3 to the hedonic/utilitarian dimension. -->
- **Source**: Khan, U., & Dhar, R. (2010). "Price-Framing Effects on the Purchase of Hedonic and Utilitarian Bundles." *Journal of Marketing Research*, 47(6), 1090–1099. <https://doi.org/10.1509/jmkr.47.6.1090>
- **Methodology**: Series of experiments examining how price presentation framing (discount vs. bonus) interacts with bundle composition (hedonic vs. utilitarian items) to influence purchase intent. Controlled manipulations of bundle type and price framing across undergraduate and online samples.
- **Key Finding**: Consumers respond differently to bundle framing depending on whether the bundle is primarily hedonic (pleasure-oriented: luxury candles, wine, spa products) or utilitarian (functional: cleaning supplies, office essentials). Hedonic bundles benefit from "bonus" framing ("Get a free gift with purchase") rather than straight discount framing; the free-gift language reduces the guilt associated with hedonic indulgence. Utilitarian bundles respond more neutrally to discount vs. bonus framing — economic savings are socially acceptable to pursue directly. The psychological mechanism is self-licensing: a bonus framing for hedonic items allows customers to justify the purchase without feeling extravagant.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For hedonic product bundles (candles, bath products, premium food, luxury accessories), frame bundles as "gift with purchase" or "complimentary item" rather than leading with dollar savings. For utilitarian bundles (household essentials, office supplies, cleaning products), standard discount framing ("Save $15 when you buy both") works well. Mixed bundles (one hedonic + one utilitarian item) can use either framing — test both. This finding connects to Finding 3 (aggregate vs. itemized framing) and should be considered in concert: complementary + hedonic bundles benefit from aggregate bonus framing; non-complementary + utilitarian bundles benefit from itemized savings framing.
- **Replication Status**: Published in JMR (top-tier marketing journal). Consistent with hedonic guilt and self-licensing literature. No large-scale direct replication identified as of 2026, but mechanism is theoretically grounded.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires customers to perceive the bundle as primarily hedonic or utilitarian — mixed-composition bundles may dilute the effect. Cultural context may moderate guilt around hedonic consumption. Effect size may shrink at high price points where customers scrutinize total expenditure regardless of framing.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JMR; controlled experiments; theoretically grounded mechanism)

### Finding 6: Bundle Value from Reduced Search Costs
<!-- Audit Note (2026-04-22): NEW finding — Run B proposal accepted by reconciler. Verified via WebSearch 2026-04-21. Adds a non-price value-driver mechanism missing from the current file. -->
- **Source**: Harris, J., & Blair, E.A. (2006). "Consumer preference for product bundles: The role of reduced search costs." *Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science*, 34(4), 506–513. <https://doi.org/10.1177/0092070306288405>
- **Methodology**: Experimental study examining how the cognitive effort saved by a curated bundle (vs. self-assembling equivalent items) affects bundle preference and willingness to pay. Manipulated search cost salience across conditions to isolate the search-cost mechanism from price effects.
- **Key Finding**: Consumers value bundles not only for price savings but for the cognitive search cost they eliminate. When customers face high uncertainty about which complementary products to choose (which lens fits which camera, which skincare products work together), a pre-curated bundle reduces decision effort and is valued above and beyond its component sum. This "search-cost savings" effect is independent of and additive to price discount effects — bundles can command a price premium simply by solving the "what goes with what" problem. The effect is strongest when: (1) category expertise is low, (2) product compatibility is non-obvious, and (3) the curation source is trusted.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Position bundles explicitly as "expertly curated" or "guaranteed compatible" to activate the search-cost value. Language like "We've done the research — these work perfectly together" communicates curation value beyond the price savings. For technical product categories (electronics, skincare, supplements), the search-cost reduction can justify bundle pricing at or above the sum of parts — customers pay for certainty. Bundle description copy should lead with compatibility assurance before price savings for high-complexity categories. Staff-curated bundles ("Our aesthetician's morning routine") command more search-cost premium than algorithmic ("Frequently bought together") bundles.
- **Replication Status**: Peer-reviewed JAMS publication. Mechanism is consistent with information economics literature on search costs and with UX research on decision fatigue. Direct replications in e-commerce settings limited as of 2026.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires genuine perceived complexity in self-assembly. For simple, obviously-compatible product combinations (e.g., a notebook + a pen), search cost savings are low and the effect diminishes. Requires trust in the curator — unknown brands do not benefit as much as established specialists. Digital goods with clear standalone utility may also show weaker effects.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (peer-reviewed JAMS; controlled experiment; mechanism well-grounded; limited e-commerce-specific replication)

## Methodological Notes

1. **CRO Finding 14 Context**: This file extends but does not replace CRO Finding 14. The McKinsey/Deloitte/HBR revenue numbers in Finding 14 are from consulting industry research and should be cited as directional benchmarks. The academic findings here (Adams & Yellen, Stremersch & Tellis, Janiszewski & Cunha) provide the *mechanism* explaining why those revenue lifts occur.

2. **The Complementarity Requirement**: All bundle psychology research shows that perceived complementarity is the gating variable. Customers must believe the items "go together." Test your proposed bundles with a simple complementarity survey before building the feature.

3. **Discount Threshold Reality**: The 15–25% discount range in scaffolded reference material is supported by the general promotions literature but not specifically studied in bundling contexts. CRO Finding 14 cites McKinsey data showing 10–30% revenue lifts — this range encompasses the discount sweet spot, not the lift magnitude.
