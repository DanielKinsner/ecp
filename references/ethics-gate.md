### Ethics Finding Output Model — Three States, Not Two

The ethics gate has three output states. Auditors MUST choose the correct one — not default to BLOCK because a pattern "looks bad."

| State | Severity | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **BLOCK** | CRITICAL | Currently violating a specific regulation. Cite the exact clause. | Filled ★★★★★ stars displayed with zero reviews → FTC 16 CFR Part 465 § 465.2 |
| **ADJACENT** | MEDIUM | Not currently illegal, but one change away from a violation. Strongly recommend fixing. | Hollow ☆☆☆☆☆ stars with "(0 reviews)" — truthful now, but removing the count text would cross the line |
| **CLEAR** | (none) | No ethics concerns found in this cluster. | Standard product page with no dark patterns |

**BLOCK findings** are non-negotiable — they survive reconciliation dedup at CRITICAL and cannot be downgraded. They represent actual legal exposure.

**ADJACENT findings** are strong recommendations — they surface a pattern that is *legally adjacent* to a violation but not currently crossing the line. They are written as MEDIUM severity findings with the tag `ETHICS_STATE: ADJACENT`. The observation must explain:
1. What the auditor found
2. Why it is NOT currently a violation (what specific element keeps it legal)
3. What change would tip it INTO a violation
4. Why fixing it now is strongly recommended (both for conversion AND to eliminate legal adjacency)

**ADJACENT finding format:**
```
FINDING: FAIL
SECTION: [slug]
ELEMENT: [element]
ETHICS_STATE: ADJACENT
SOURCE: [VISUAL|CODE|BOTH]
OBSERVATION: [What you found. Why it's currently compliant. What would make it a violation.]
RECOMMENDATION: [Specific fix — framed as "strongly recommended" not "required by law"]
REFERENCE: ethics-gate.md — [section]
PRIORITY: MEDIUM
SOURCE_URL: [URL from Source Registry — MANDATORY on all ethics findings]
**Why this matters:** [Business + legal adjacency rationale]
↳ ethics-gate.md ([Regulation], [Year]) [Gold]
```

**CLEAR** means the auditor checked and found nothing. Output the standard clearance note.

### Mandatory Source URL on All Ethics Findings

**Every ethics finding — BLOCK or ADJACENT — MUST include a `SOURCE_URL:` line** linking to the official regulation text from the Source Registry above. No exceptions.

If the auditor cannot find the regulation's URL in the Source Registry, that is a signal the citation may be wrong. Stop and verify before writing the finding.

The user must be able to click through and read the actual law that the finding references. An ethics finding without a verifiable source URL is an unsubstantiated legal claim and MUST NOT be presented as fact.

### Applicability Self-Check — MANDATORY Before Firing BLOCK

Before an auditor writes `PRIORITY: CRITICAL` with `ETHICS_STATE: BLOCK` on an ethics finding, they MUST pass this three-question self-check. If any answer is "no" or "unsure," the finding is ADJACENT (MEDIUM), not BLOCK (CRITICAL).

1. **Does this specific regulation prohibit what I'm seeing on this specific page?** Not "does the regulation exist" — does it actually cover this scenario? Example: FTC 16 CFR Part 465 prohibits fake reviews. Empty star outlines with "(0 reviews)" are not fake reviews — they're a truthful empty state. The regulation is real but doesn't apply here. → ADJACENT, not BLOCK.

2. **Would the cited penalty actually be enforceable in this scenario?** Some regulations have specific scope limitations (FTC Junk Fees Rule applies to ticketing/lodging, not all e-commerce; CA SB-478 excludes shipping costs). Citing a $53,088 penalty that doesn't apply to the user's situation is more harmful than missing the finding entirely — it causes unnecessary legal panic.

3. **Can I point to a specific section or clause in the regulation that this page violates?** Not the regulation's general topic — the specific prohibition. "16 CFR Part 465 § 465.2(a) prohibits reviews by people who haven't used the product" is verifiable. "16 CFR Part 465 says this is bad" is not.

If the auditor cannot pass all three, the finding MUST be written as ADJACENT (MEDIUM), not BLOCK (CRITICAL). The auditor still gets credit for spotting the pattern — ADJACENT findings are valuable precisely because they catch things before they become violations.

**Why this matters:** A false BLOCK citing a specific law is worse than a missed finding. It tells the user they're breaking a law they're not breaking, potentially causing unnecessary legal consultations, development panic, or loss of trust in the audit. The ethics gate's credibility depends on every BLOCK being defensible. ADJACENT gives auditors a way to flag real risks without making false accusations.

**Note on FTC consent orders:** FTC consent orders are subject to rescission under current enforcement posture (see Vacated Rules Tracker above — Rytr order set aside Dec 22, 2025). Before citing a specific FTC consent order as live binding precedent, verify the order is still active at ftc.gov/legal-library. FTC statutory rules (16 CFR Part 465, 16 CFR Part 255, ROSCA, CAN-SPAM) are NOT consent orders and remain fully enforceable regardless of enforcement posture.

### Multi-Tier Standards — Severity Mapping Rubric (MANDATORY)

Many design-accessibility-UX standards have multiple tiers: a **legal/regulatory minimum**, a **stricter legal/regulatory standard** (sometimes not enforced for private businesses), **platform guidelines**, and **UX best practices**. Auditors MUST NOT collapse these tiers into a single CRITICAL/BLOCK finding. **Only the legally-enforceable tier maps to CRITICAL.** Missing a platform recommendation or a stricter AAA-level standard is NEVER a legal BLOCK on its own.

**General severity-mapping rule:**

| Finding condition | PRIORITY | ETHICS_STATE |
|---|---|---|
| Violates a currently-enforceable legal/regulatory minimum (WCAG 2.2 AA, CA SB-478 ambit, TCPA written-consent, FTC Fake Reviews Rule, etc.) | **CRITICAL** | BLOCK |
| Meets the legal minimum but fails a stricter legal standard that isn't enforced in this jurisdiction (e.g., WCAG 2.2 AAA in US private ecommerce) or a platform/industry recommendation (Apple HIG, Material Design) | **MEDIUM** | ADJACENT |
| Meets legal + platform recommendations but fails a broader UX best practice | **LOW** | — |
| Meets all | PASS | CLEAR |

**Worked example — tap-target size (the 2026-04-22 false-critical that triggered this rubric):**

| Element size | Standard failed | Auditor output |
|---|---|---|
| < 24×24 CSS px (no ≥24 px spacing exception) | WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 Level AA — ADA-enforceable for private ecommerce via case law, EAA-binding for EU | PRIORITY: CRITICAL, ETHICS_STATE: BLOCK |
| 24×24 ≤ size < 44×44 (AA-compliant, AAA-noncompliant) | WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.5 Level AAA (not legally required for US private ecommerce) + Apple HIG 44pt + Google Material 48dp recommendations | PRIORITY: MEDIUM, ETHICS_STATE: ADJACENT |
| 44×44 ≤ size < 48×48 | Apple HIG met; Material Design 48dp not met | PRIORITY: LOW |
| ≥ 48×48 | All standards met | PASS |

**Other multi-tier standards auditors must apply this rubric to** (not exhaustive):

- **Color contrast:** WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.3 AA (4.5:1 normal text, 3:1 large text) = CRITICAL floor; SC 1.4.6 AAA (7:1 normal text, 4.5:1 large text) = MEDIUM ADJACENT.
- **Heading structure:** WCAG 2.2 SC 1.3.1 AA (proper heading hierarchy) = CRITICAL if broken; stylistic heading-density recommendations = LOW.
- **Alt-text presence:** WCAG 2.2 SC 1.1.1 AA (non-decorative images must have alt text) = CRITICAL if missing; alt-text quality/descriptiveness recommendations = MEDIUM ADJACENT.
- **Form input labels:** WCAG 2.2 SC 3.3.2 AA (explicit labels on form fields) = CRITICAL if missing; placeholder-as-label or inline-validation-UX = MEDIUM/LOW.
- **Motion / autoplay:** WCAG 2.2 SC 2.2.2 AA (no auto-updating/moving content > 5s without pause) = CRITICAL; autoplay-video UX best-practice = MEDIUM ADJACENT.
- **Target-size AAA standard (2.5.5):** never mapped to CRITICAL for US private ecommerce — it's an AAA recommendation, not a legal floor.

**Rule of thumb:** if the finding's justification reads "this should be X per [Apple HIG / Google Material / WCAG AAA / industry best practice]," the finding is NOT a CRITICAL BLOCK. Downgrade to ADJACENT or LOW per the rubric above.

**Why this matters:** This rubric addresses the 2026-04-22 false-critical where a 40×40 CSS px hamburger button fired as ADA/EAA CRITICAL — 40×40 PASSES WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 AA (24×24 min) and fails only the AAA/platform 44×44 recommendation. The correct severity was MEDIUM ADJACENT. Without this rubric, auditors may re-create the same conflation across any standard with multiple tiers.

## PART 1: CRO / PAGE-LEVEL DARK PATTERNS

### 1.1 Urgency / Scarcity

- Countdown timers MUST be tied to real, verifiable deadlines — else **BLOCK**
- Scarcity claims ("Only X left") MUST reflect actual current inventory — else **BLOCK**
- "People viewing" counters MUST reflect real concurrent sessions, not fabricated numbers — else **BLOCK**
- Fake urgency backfires: 17.6% sales DECREASE observed from fabricated "Only X left" messages (Park, J., Kim, S., & Yoo, K. (2020). "The backfire effect of scarcity claims." *Journal of Consumer Psychology*, DOI: 10.1016/j.jcps.2020.01.002)
- **Legal basis:** FTC Section 5 (15 U.S.C. § 45) — deceptive acts or practices; EU DSA Art 25 (applies only to "online platforms" per DSA Art 3(i) — typical first-party ecommerce stores are NOT in Art 25 scope; use UCPD 2005/29 Art 5 + national UDAP for first-party retailers); UCPD 2005/29 Annex I point 7 for false-limited-availability (all EU traders, not just platforms)

### 1.2 Pricing Display (Page Level)

- First displayed price MUST include all mandatory non-government fees — else **BLOCK** (CA SB-478)
- Strikethrough "was" prices MUST be real previous selling prices, held for reasonable period — else **BLOCK**
- Shipping costs MUST be disclosed before payment information is collected — else **BLOCK**
- **Scope clarification — shipping is NOT a "mandatory fee" under SB-478:** CA SB-478 explicitly excludes "postage or carriage charges that will be reasonably and actually incurred to ship the physical good to the consumer" from its mandatory fee disclosure requirement. The FTC Junk Fees Rule (May 2025) applies only to live-event ticketing and short-term lodging, not general e-commerce. Shipping costs are variable (destination, speed, weight) and cannot be calculated before the customer provides an address. **Displaying shipping on product pages or homepage cards is a UX best practice (reduces cart abandonment) but is NOT a legal requirement.** A free-shipping threshold banner (e.g., "Free shipping over $75") satisfies transparency for homepage and catalog contexts.
- **Auditor guidance:** Before flagging shipping disclosure, check the announcement bar, site header, and page banners for existing free-shipping messaging. A visible free-shipping threshold banner is compliant and should not be flagged. Do NOT flag as CRITICAL — shipping visibility on product cards is at most a MEDIUM UX recommendation.
- **Penalty (SB-478 / CLRA drip pricing):** SB-478 is codified as Civil Code § 1770(a)(29) under the CLRA. **CA AG enforcement** flows through the UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 17200, 17204, 17206): up to **$2,500 per violation** civil penalty. **Private right (CLRA)**: actual damages (minimum $1,000 per consumer in class actions per Civ. Code § 1781) + restitution + punitive damages + attorneys' fees + injunctive relief (Civ. Code § 1780). Federal overlay for matters within FTC scope: up to $53,088 per violation under the 16 CFR § 1.98 schedule in effect Jan 17, 2025.
- 39–48% of cart abandonment is caused by hidden costs revealed at checkout

### 1.3 Reviews / Social Proof

- All reviews MUST be from real verified users of the product — else **BLOCK** (FTC Fake Reviews Rule, 16 CFR Part 465)
- Insider/affiliate/employee reviews MUST be clearly labeled with relationship — else **BLOCK**
- Negative reviews MUST NOT be suppressed, de-ranked, or hidden — else **BLOCK**
- AI-generated reviews or testimonials are explicitly prohibited by 16 CFR Part 465 — else **BLOCK**
- **Penalty:** Up to $53,088 per violation (2025 adjusted amount, FTC Federal Register, effective Jan 17, 2025)
- **Enforcement:** FTC issued 10 warning letters to businesses (Dec 22, 2025) — first Consumer Review Rule enforcement wave; follow-up civil penalties expected 2026

#### Phantom Social Proof

- **BLOCK (actual phantom social proof):** Displaying **filled** star ratings (e.g., ★★★★★), "Rated and Reviewed" claims, fabricated review counts, or aggregate rating badges when zero verified reviews exist. These imply a positive rating that does not exist — deceptive under FTC Section 5 and the Fake Reviews Rule (16 CFR Part 465).
- **NOT a BLOCK (truthful empty state):** Displaying **hollow/empty** star outlines (e.g., ☆☆☆☆☆) alongside an explicit "(0 reviews)" count is the widget's default empty state and is **truthful**. The FTC Fake Reviews Rule targets fabricated reviews, incentivized reviews, suppressed negative reviews, and AI-generated reviews — not an empty UI widget that accurately reports zero. Flag as **MEDIUM** UX recommendation (hiding the widget when count is zero is better for conversion, but displaying it with "(0 reviews)" is not deceptive or illegal).
- **Auditor guidance:** Before flagging star ratings as CRITICAL phantom social proof, check whether the stars are FILLED (implying a rating) or HOLLOW/EMPTY (displaying the widget's zero state). Also check whether the review count is explicitly shown — "(0 reviews)" alongside empty stars is a truthful disclosure, not a deceptive claim. Only flag as CRITICAL when the visual presentation would lead a reasonable consumer to believe the product has been positively reviewed when it has not.
- **Visibility test:** An element is "displayed" only if rendered with computed `display` != `none` AND `visibility` != `hidden` AND has non-zero bounding box dimensions. A hidden DOM node does NOT constitute "displaying."
- 'Trusted by thousands' or similar vague volume claims without verifiable evidence (specific count, named customers, or linked reviews) — flag as **CRITICAL** under `social-proof-placement`
- Applies to the page being audited, not site-wide shared elements (footer store-level badge is acceptable if store has reviews)

#### Schema Markup and Social Proof — FTC + Lanham Act Adjacency

- **ADJACENT (MEDIUM):** Review or rating claims surfaced via schema markup (e.g., `aggregateRating`, `Review`) that accurately reflect real reviews on the page are NOT violations. However, if schema-surfaced data would make a false claim in a search snippet that a competitor could substantiate as injurious (e.g., inflated star counts, falsely claimed "#1 rated"), this creates **Lanham Act § 43(a) (15 U.S.C. § 1125(a))** false-advertising exposure in addition to FTC § 5 exposure. Lanham Act claims are filed by competitors directly — no FTC enforcement required.
- **BLOCK:** `aggregateRating` schema declaring 5.0 stars or an inflated review count not backed by the number of on-page verified reviews — false structured data fires both FTC Fake Reviews Rule (16 CFR Part 465) AND Google Structured Data spam policies simultaneously.
- **ADJACENT:** Schema markup correctly reflecting real reviews, but title tag or meta description adds an unsubstantiated superlative ("Best rated 2026 — Expert reviewed") with no on-page substantiation. Truthful schema + false surrounding claim creates Lanham Act adjacency.
- **Source:** Lanham Act § 43(a), 15 U.S.C. § 1125(a) — https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/1125; FTC Fake Reviews Rule 16 CFR Part 465 — https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/rules/rulemaking-use-consumer-reviews-testimonials. Cross-reference: Part 7.4 (Lanham Act false advertising in metadata).

### 1.4 Choice Architecture

- Accept/decline options MUST be visually symmetrical (same size, weight, prominence) — else **BLOCK** (EU DSA Art 25 — **scope: "online platforms"** per DSA Art 3(i), i.e., hosting services that store + disseminate user information to the public. Typical first-party ecommerce stores are NOT "online platforms" and Art 25 does not directly apply; cite UCPD 2005/29 Art 5 + national UDAP + FTC § 5 for first-party retailers)
- No confirm-shaming language ("No thanks, I don't want to save money") — **BLOCK**
- No pre-checked boxes adding items, services, or subscriptions — **BLOCK**
- **Penalty:** Up to 6% of global annual turnover under **EU DSA Art 74(1)** (applies to Very Large Online Platforms and Very Large Online Search Engines designated by the European Commission under Art 33). Non-VLOP DSA enforcement is set by Member State authorities per Art 52 and varies by jurisdiction. X/Twitter fined €120M (Dec 2025) for DSA dark-pattern violations. Art 26 concerns advertising transparency, not penalties — do not cite Art 26 for the 6% figure.

### 1.5 Subscription Dark Patterns (Page Level)

- **Amazon Prime settlement (Sep 17, 2025):** $2.5B total ($1B civil penalty + $1.5B consumer refunds) — largest dark patterns enforcement action in US history
  - Judge John Chun (W.D. Wash.) ruled Amazon violated ROSCA (Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act, 15 U.S.C. §§ 8401–8405) by collecting billing information before disclosing Prime's $139 annual cost and auto-renewal terms
  - 35 million consumers affected by deceptive "Iliad" cancellation flow
  - Amazon's internal emails described practices as "a bit of a shady world" — personal executive liability included in consent order
- Settlement mandates: clear decline buttons, transparent pricing disclosure before billing info, simple cancellation matching signup method
- **FTC Click-to-Cancel Rule:** Finalized Oct 2024 (16 CFR Part 425), VACATED by Eighth Circuit (Jul 2025) — FTC restarting rulemaking. **ROSCA remains fully enforceable.** Rule's principles still apply as de facto standard under Section 5.
- Cancellation MUST provide a **simple mechanism to stop recurring charges** — ROSCA 15 U.S.C. § 8403(3) requires a "simple mechanism" but does NOT statutorily require identical-steps or identical-channel parity. **Do not cite ROSCA for a "same number of steps, same channel" BLOCK.** That stronger parity standard was in the FTC Click-to-Cancel rule (16 CFR Part 425), which was VACATED by the 8th Circuit on Jul 8, 2025. Under current law: (a) ROSCA requires a simple-mechanism cancellation path but leaves step/channel specifics to FTC § 5 case-by-case analysis; (b) a "same steps / same channel" standard remains a de facto practitioner best practice and is the core of the Amazon Prime settlement's behavioral remedy, but it is NOT a per-se ROSCA violation to have asymmetric paths if the cancellation is genuinely simple. (c) California's AB-2863 DOES impose a statutory online-cancel parity requirement within CA ARL scope (see §7.5) — that's an ADJACENT but state-specific authority.
- Free trial-to-paid conversions MUST disclose billing amount, date, and method before any payment info collected — else **BLOCK**

## PART 2: SEO ETHICS

### 2.1 Cloaking / Hidden Text

- **BLOCK:** Showing different content to Googlebot than to users (cloaking) — Google Spam Policies (updated Dec 10, 2025, developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials/spam-policies)
- **BLOCK:** Text hidden via CSS (`display:none`, white text on white background, font-size:0, off-screen positioning) to inflate keyword density
- **BLOCK:** Doorway redirects that serve crawlers one URL and users a different destination
- **Consequence:** Manual action penalty — site-wide or page-level deindexing from Google Search; removal can take weeks to months after reconsideration request
- Image lazy-loading and responsive image swaps are NOT cloaking (Google explicitly stated, Support article 9044175)
- **Google policy authority:** Google Search Essentials (formerly Webmaster Guidelines) — no monetary fine, but deindexing = permanent revenue destruction for organic-traffic-dependent sites

### 2.2 Fake / Misleading Structured Data (Schema Markup)

- **BLOCK:** Marking up content that doesn't exist on the visible page (e.g., reviews markup with no visible reviews)
- **BLOCK:** Fake aggregate rating schema (inflated star counts, fake review totals not reflected in actual reviews)
- **BLOCK:** Product schema claiming prices or availability that differ from actual page content
- **BLOCK:** FAQ/HowTo schema for content not genuinely helpful to users — Google explicitly targets "misleadingly formatted content" (Spam Policies, Dec 2025)
- **Consequence:** Rich result eligibility revoked; structured data flagged as spam; potential manual action on full domain
- Google's structured data quality guidelines: "structured data that misrepresents the actual content... will not be displayed as a rich result and may possibly be marked as spam" (developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/sd-policies)
- **Note:** Google retired 7 schema types (Nov 2025 announcement) — verify active supported types before implementation

### 2.3 Keyword Stuffing in Product Descriptions

- **BLOCK:** Inserting keywords at unnatural densities in product titles, descriptions, meta tags, or alt text solely for search manipulation
- **BLOCK:** Repeating the same phrase 5+ times in a paragraph where it provides no user value
- **BLOCK:** Hidden keyword lists in product pages (zero-opacity div, visually hidden span)
- **Consequence:** Google's content spam detection (2025 AI-powered) flags excessive repetition; manual action for egregious cases; algorithmic ranking suppression for subtler violations
- **Secondary risk:** Keyword-stuffed descriptions that make false product claims also trigger FTC Section 5 liability (deceptive product representations)

### 2.4 Fake Reviews for SEO Purposes

- **BLOCK:** Purchasing reviews to inflate product page review counts for SEO benefit (FTC Fake Reviews Rule, 16 CFR Part 465, effective Oct 21, 2024)
- **BLOCK:** Incentivizing positive reviews without clear disclosure (applies to review signals used in structured data markup)
- **BLOCK:** Using AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude) to generate product reviews for publication. **Legal basis:** FTC Fake Reviews Rule 16 CFR § 465.2(a)–(b) — disseminating reviews that materially misrepresent user experience is prohibited; the FTC's Aug 2024 final rule preamble explicitly reaches AI-generated fake reviews. **Do not cite the Rytr consent order as live precedent** — the Rytr final order was set aside by the FTC on Dec 22, 2025 under the White House AI Action Plan directive (vote 2-0; FTC finding that the original complaint failed to satisfy FTC Act legal requirements and unduly burdened AI innovation). The underlying Fake Reviews Rule remains fully enforceable and is the controlling authority.
- **BLOCK:** Suppressing or filtering negative reviews to improve aggregate star rating displayed in schema
- FTC Fake Reviews Rule covers reviews used across channels — including review schema that surfaces in Google Search results
- **Penalty:** Up to $53,088 per violation (FTC); $337.3M returned to consumers from FTC advertising enforcement actions in FY2024 (FTC press release Mar 2025: https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/03/new-report-shows-ftc-returned-3373-million-consumers-2024; FY2024 Agency Financial Report: https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/ftc_gov/pdf/fy2024ftcagencyfinancialreport11152024.pdf)
- **Enforcement:** Fashion Nova FTC refund order (Jan 28, 2025); Sitejabber final order (Jan 3, 2025) for misrepresenting ratings from consumers who hadn't received products

### 2.5 Doorway Pages / Thin Content Manipulation

- **BLOCK:** Creating pages with substantially similar content targeting slight keyword variations ("red running shoes size 10," "red running shoes size 11" with identical body copy)
- **BLOCK:** Auto-generated landing pages with templated/AI-spun content providing minimal user value
- **BLOCK:** Pages that exist solely to rank and redirect users to a different destination
- **Consequence:** Google Spam Policies explicitly prohibit "doorway abuse" — sites with large doorway page networks receive site-wide manual actions
- Google's October 2025 spam update explicitly targeted AI-generated guest post farms and thin machine-generated content embedding paid backlinks
- **Threshold:** Each page must provide unique, substantive value to a real user — not just a keyword variation

### 2.6 Link Scheme Penalties

- **BLOCK:** Purchasing links with keyword-rich anchor text for ranking manipulation
- **BLOCK:** Private Blog Network (PBN) link building — Google's October 2025 spam update explicitly targeted AI-generated guest post farms
- **BLOCK:** Link exchanges ("link to me, I'll link to you") at scale
- **BLOCK:** Embedding links in widgets, templates, or footer boilerplate distributed to other sites without `rel="nofollow"` or `rel="sponsored"`
- **BLOCK:** Paid links without `rel="sponsored"` attribute — violates Google's spam policies regardless of disclosure to readers
- **Consequence:** Manual action for "unnatural links" — site-wide ranking penalties; link disavow process (Google Search Console) required for recovery
- Google Search Console manual actions report: "Buying links or participating in link schemes in order to manipulate ranking in Google Search results is a violation of Google's spam policies"
- Google tightened spam enforcement in 2024–2025 across link-adjacent abuse; enforcement now includes algorithmic devaluation AND manual penalties

## PART 3: PRICING ETHICS

### 3.1 Drip Pricing / Hidden Fees

- **BLOCK:** Revealing mandatory fees (processing fees, service charges, resort fees, "convenience" fees) only at checkout — applies to ALL e-commerce (FTC Rule on Unfair or Deceptive Fees, 16 CFR Part 464, effective May 12, 2025)
- **BLOCK:** Displaying product price as "$X" when total mandatory cost is materially higher
- **BLOCK:** Mandatory fees buried in cart as a separate line item without disclosure on product page
- **CA SB-478 (Honest Pricing Law):** Effective July 1, 2024 — all advertised prices for goods/services sold in California must include all mandatory non-government fees. Codified as CLRA § 1770(a)(29). Enforcement: CA AG / DAs via UCL up to $2,500 per violation (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17206); private CLRA remedies under Civ. Code §§ 1780–1781 include actual damages (minimum $1,000 per consumer in class actions), restitution, punitive damages, attorneys' fees, and injunctive relief.
- FTC Junk Fees Rule (May 12, 2025): Explicitly prohibits bait-and-switch pricing and hidden fees in live-event ticketing and short-term lodging; FTC announced case-by-case enforcement for all other e-commerce industries
- States following CA: Several states enacted similar drip pricing bans in 2024–2025; trend toward national standard
- **Practical rule:** Price shown in ad/on product page = price user pays at checkout (before user-selected options and government taxes)

#### Shipping-costs distinction — fires vs. doesn't

Shipping costs displayed separately from the product price are **NOT** a drip-pricing or junk-fees violation. Auditors flagging "shipping not included in product price" as CRITICAL under SB-478 or 16 CFR 464 are wrong about the scope of both laws.

- **FIRES (BLOCK):** Mandatory processing fees, service charges, "convenience" fees, resort fees, or environmental-handling fees revealed only at checkout and not disclosed on the product page. Taxes that the seller knows in advance (e.g., a store selling only in CA collecting a fixed CA-rate) masked as "taxes calculated at checkout" when the rate is actually deterministic.
- **DOES NOT FIRE:** Shipping costs calculated at checkout based on destination/weight/method. Sales tax calculated at checkout based on destination. "Free shipping over $X" banners. "Shipping calculated at checkout" language on the cart or product page. Ticketed / lodging pricing surfaces (these are the narrow scope where the FTC Junk Fees Rule currently applies).
- **WHY:** CA SB-478 (Cal. Civ. Code § 1770(a)(29)(B)) explicitly carves shipping and government-imposed taxes out of the "all mandatory fees must be included in advertised price" requirement. The FTC Unfair or Deceptive Fees Rule (16 CFR Part 464, effective May 12, 2025) is narrowly scoped to live-event ticketing and short-term lodging by its own text; e-commerce shipping does not fall inside that scope. The FTC has stated it will enforce case-by-case against other industries under Section 5, but that is deception-by-materiality, not a blanket "shipping is a junk fee" rule.
- **Auditor rule:** Before flagging shipping as CRITICAL, verify (1) the cost is a product-seller-imposed mandatory fee (not carrier-calculated shipping or destination-based tax), AND (2) the fee is concealed from the product/cart surface until checkout. If either test fails, the correct classification is **MEDIUM** UX recommendation ("surface shipping estimate earlier for better conversion") — not CRITICAL.

### 3.2 Bait-and-Switch Pricing

- **BLOCK:** Advertising a product at one price with intent not to sell it at that price (FTC Penalty Offense — "Penalty Offenses Concerning Bait & Switch," FTC.gov)
- **BLOCK:** Out-of-stock items advertised at low prices to drive traffic, then substituting higher-priced alternatives
- **BLOCK:** Showing one price in Google Shopping/email/ad and a different (higher) price on the product page
- **BLOCK:** "Limited quantities" advertised at sale price that never existed in meaningful quantity
- FTC maintains published "penalty offenses" list for bait-and-switch — prior knowledge of this list means no prior-notice defense
- **Penalty:** Up to $53,088 per violation where penalty offense authority applies; injunctive relief + consumer redress in case-by-case enforcement
- FTC Dec 2024 Junk Fees Rule explicitly cites "bait-and-switch pricing tactics" as a named enforcement target across industries

### 3.3 Dynamic Pricing Discrimination

- **FLAG/WARN (not yet BLOCK in most jurisdictions):** Using consumer data (location, browsing history, device type, demographic signals) to show individually different prices without disclosure
- **BLOCK where applicable:** Price discrimination based on protected class characteristics (race, national origin, religion, sex, disability) violates Fair Housing Act, Equal Credit Opportunity Act, and state civil rights laws
- **BLOCK:** Dynamic pricing that exploits crisis conditions (surge pricing during declared emergencies) — violates state price-gouging laws (active in CA, NY, FL, TX, and 30+ other states)
- **FTC surveillance pricing study (Jan 2025):** FTC gathered information via administrative subpoenas to 8 dynamic pricing vendors; study found tools "relied on consumer data such as location, browsing history, and purchase patterns to set individualized prices, adjust prices in real-time, and manipulate product rankings"
- **State legislation trend (March 2026):** Multiple states (CO, IL, WA) advancing algorithmic pricing transparency bills; Reuters reported March 4, 2026 states "zeroing in on dynamic and algorithmic pricing" as FTC pulls back
- **DOJ position (2025):** DOJ argued in antitrust cases that using shared pricing algorithms to coordinate prices could violate Sherman Act Section 1
- **Current obligation:** Disclose when pricing is personalized; never use protected-class proxies; monitor state price-gouging trigger statutes

#### New York Algorithmic Pricing Disclosure Act (NY Gen. Bus. Law § 519-a)

- **Effective:** November 10, 2025. Applies to retailers using algorithmic personalized pricing that targets NY consumers.
- **BLOCK:** Using algorithmic pricing based on consumer personal data for NY-targeted stores WITHOUT displaying the mandatory disclosure: **"THIS PRICE WAS SET BY AN ALGORITHM USING YOUR PERSONAL DATA"** — verbatim statutory language required.
- **Penalty:** $1,000 per violation.
- **Scope:** Applies when pricing is personalized using consumer data (browsing history, location, device type, purchase history, demographics). Does NOT apply to standard location-based shipping tiers or publicly-stated promotional pricing that does not use individual consumer profiling.
- **Source:** NY Gen. Bus. Law § 519-a (enacted 2025) — https://legislation.nysenate.gov/

### 3.4 BNPL (Buy Now, Pay Later) Disclosure Requirements

- **BLOCK:** BNPL offers that do not clearly disclose: (1) total repayment amount, (2) number and timing of installments, (3) any fees for late payment or missed payments, (4) impact on credit score if applicable
- **BLOCK:** Presenting BNPL as "free" or "no interest" without disclosing late fees or penalties
- **Legal basis:** Truth in Lending Act (TILA, 15 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq.) and Regulation Z (12 CFR Part 1026). **Note:** The CFPB Interpretive Rule (Jul 30, 2024) that classified BNPL accounts as "credit cards" subject to TILA was **withdrawn by CFPB on May 12, 2025** (Fed. Reg. 2025-08286). TILA/Reg Z underlying disclosure obligations remain federal law independent of the interpretive rule; the withdrawal removes the CFPB's specific BNPL-as-credit-card classification but does not extinguish the disclosure requirements for BNPL products that do qualify as credit products.
- **CFPB enforcement status:** CFPB withdrew the BNPL Interpretive Rule on May 12, 2025 (Fed. Reg. 2025-08286, Trump administration). State attorneys general retain UDAP enforcement authority independent of CFPB. TILA/Regulation Z remain fully enforceable federal law.
- **State exposure:** CA, NY, CO, and others have independent UDAP (Unfair, Deceptive, or Abusive Acts or Practices) authority to pursue misleading BNPL disclosures
- **Best practice:** Treat BNPL like any credit product — full disclosure before checkout completion, not just at final confirmation

### 3.5 Subscription Trap Patterns

- **BLOCK:** Enrolling users in paid subscriptions during checkout without clear, affirmative consent separate from purchase completion — ROSCA (15 U.S.C. §§ 8401–8405)
- **BLOCK:** Free trial → paid conversion without (a) clearly disclosing the trial period end date, (b) the charge amount, (c) how to cancel before being charged
- **BLOCK:** Cancellation flows that require more steps than signup (Amazon "Iliad flow" pattern — specifically condemned in $2.5B settlement, Sep 2025)
- **BLOCK:** Offering to "pause" or "downgrade" as the ONLY visible option when user navigates to cancel — must show clear "Cancel Membership" path
- **BLOCK:** "Negative option" features where users are charged unless they affirmatively opt out, without clear pre-billing disclosure
- **BLOCK:** Sending cancellation confirmation to an email the user no longer monitors, then reinstating subscription
- **Amazon Prime enforcement (W.D. Wash., Judge John H. Chun):** Partial summary judgment **Sep 17, 2025** — court found Amazon Prime subject to ROSCA and held that Amazon obtained billing info before disclosing material terms (a ROSCA violation). Jury trial commenced Sep 22, 2025. Settlement entered **Sep 25, 2025** (4th day of trial): **$2.5B total** — $1B civil penalty + $1.5B consumer refunds. Individual executive liability (SVP Neil Lindsay, VP Jamil Ghani) included in the order. FTC press release Sep 25, 2025: https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/09/ftc-secures-historic-25-billion-settlement-against-amazon
- **ROSCA penalties:** Up to $16,000 per transaction (civil) + disgorgement + consumer refunds
- **FTC Click-to-Cancel rule vacated by 8th Circuit (Jul 2025)** — FTC restarting rulemaking; ROSCA + FTC Section 5 fully enforceable in interim
- **EU Digital Services Act Art 25 (scope: online platforms only per Art 3(i)):** For in-scope platforms, subscription interfaces must make cancellation "as easy as" subscription. For first-party ecommerce retailers not meeting the Art 3(i) "online platform" definition, cite UCPD 2005/29 Art 5 + national UDAP statutes instead.
- **Practical standard:** If your cancellation UX would embarrass you in front of a judge, fix it before launch

## PART 4: EMAIL / SMS ETHICS

### 4.1 CAN-SPAM Act Requirements

- **Authority:** CAN-SPAM Act of 2003 (15 U.S.C. §§ 7701–7713), enforced by FTC; $53,088 per email in violation (current 2025 adjusted amount)
- **BLOCK violations:**
  - False or misleading header information (From, To, Reply-To must accurately identify sender)
  - Deceptive subject lines (must not misrepresent email content)
  - Missing physical postal address (must include valid mailing address)
  - Failure to identify message as an advertisement where not obvious from context
  - No opt-out mechanism (every commercial email MUST include a clear, conspicuous unsubscribe mechanism)
  - Failing to honor opt-out requests within 10 business days
  - Charging a fee to unsubscribe or requiring more than sending one reply email to opt out
- **Record CAN-SPAM penalty:** Verkada — $2.95M (2024, FTC) — largest CAN-SPAM penalty in FTC history; violations included excessive email frequency, no effective opt-out, missing postal address
- **Recent enforcement:** Experian Consumer Services — $650,000 civil penalty (settled; FTC complaint filed 2023, finalized 2024) for sending marketing emails to account-holders who had no opt-out mechanism
- **Key rule:** Signing up for a product/service is NOT consent to receive unrelated marketing emails

### 4.2 GDPR Consent Requirements for Marketing Emails

- **Authority:** Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR), Articles 6, 7, 12, 13; enforced by EU member state Data Protection Authorities
- **BLOCK violations:**
  - Sending marketing emails to EU residents without prior, freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous affirmative consent (Article 6(1)(a) + Article 7)
  - Pre-ticked consent boxes — NOT valid consent under GDPR
  - Bundled consent (e.g., "I agree to terms and marketing") — must be separate checkboxes
  - "Legitimate interest" as basis for cold outreach marketing email — courts increasingly reject this; requires balancing test documentation
  - No ability to withdraw consent as easily as it was given (Article 7(3))
  - Failing to maintain records of consent (who consented, when, how, to what) — Article 7(1)
- **Penalties:** Up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever higher) — Article 83(5)
- **Recent enforcement:** France CNIL fined Google €325M (Sep 2025) for email tracking and consent violations; Ireland DPC fined Meta **€1.2B (May 22, 2023)** for EU–US data transfer violations under Schrems II (largest GDPR fine to date; the file had two inconsistent dates for this fine — 2023 is correct)
- **EU ePrivacy Directive** (2002/58/EC, implemented nationally): Adds additional requirement — prior opt-in for email marketing to individuals (stricter than GDPR in many member states)
- **UK GDPR:** Post-Brexit UK maintains equivalent requirements; ICO enforcement independent of EU

### 4.3 TCPA Requirements for SMS Marketing

- **Authority:** Telephone Consumer Protection Act (47 U.S.C. § 227); enforced by FCC + private right of action
- **BLOCK violations:**
  - Sending marketing SMS without "prior express written consent" — must be written consent, signed (electronic OK), specific to the sender, and obtained before first message
  - SMS to numbers on National Do Not Call Registry without consent
  - Automated SMS after recipient has opted out (replied STOP)
  - Using STOP/HELP keywords for any purpose other than their standard functions
- **FCC One-to-One Consent Rule — VACATED, never took effect.** The 11th Circuit vacated this rule on Jan 24, 2025 (*Insurance Marketing Coalition Ltd. v. FCC*, No. 24-10277) before its scheduled April 11, 2025 effective date. **Do not cite as a live BLOCK.** The underlying TCPA "prior express written consent" requirement remains unchanged — consent must still be written, signed, and obtained before the first message, and sellers must still honor STOP revocations. Shared-sender consent obtained through lead generators is valid again under pre-rule TCPA doctrine.
- **Penalties:** $500/message (negligent); $1,500/message (willful/knowing)
- **Class action exposure:** TCPA class-action filings surged ~95% year-over-year through mid-2025 (1,052 vs. 539 federal class actions, Jan–Jun 2024 → Jan–Jun 2025; per WebRecon mid-year 2025 report, Jul 25, 2025 — https://natlawreview.com/article/midyear-litigation-report-tcpa-class-actions-staggering-952-2024-previously-highest). TCPA's "each message is a separate violation at $500 statutory / $1,500 willful" architecture produces catastrophic aggregate exposure for mass-SMS senders. Representative SMS settlements in the ecommerce messaging context: Experian $650K CAN-SPAM (2023), Verkada $2.95M CAN-SPAM (2024). Do NOT cite the *Wakefield v. ViSalus* $925M verdict here — that case involved voice robocalls (not SMS) and the aggregate award was reversed by the Ninth Circuit on due-process grounds in Oct 2022 (21-35201).
- **Required elements for compliant SMS consent:**
  1. Clear disclosure that user will receive recurring automated marketing texts
  2. Disclosure of message frequency
  3. "Message and data rates may apply" disclosure
  4. Instructions to text STOP to cancel, HELP for help
  5. Clear identification of the brand sending messages
- **Post-opt-out obligations:** Honor STOP immediately; do not send any further marketing messages; only transactional/requested content after opt-out

### 4.4 Unsubscribe Dark Patterns

- **BLOCK:** Unsubscribe links that navigate to a page requiring login before processing the request
- **BLOCK:** Unsubscribe confirmation emails that themselves contain marketing offers
- **BLOCK:** "Manage preferences" as the ONLY option when user wants to fully unsubscribe
- **BLOCK:** Unsubscribe processes requiring more than one click/action to complete (CAN-SPAM: process may require user to visit ONE webpage for opt-out only)
- **BLOCK:** Re-subscribing users after they've unsubscribed (even if they later interact with a different signup form without clear re-opt-in)
- **BLOCK:** "We'll miss you" emotional manipulation screens with no prominent complete-unsubscribe option
- **BLOCK:** Email frequency "pause" offered as substitute for full unsubscribe when full unsubscribe is requested
- **Legal basis:** CAN-SPAM Section 5(a)(3); GDPR Article 7(3); FTC Section 5
- **Practical standard:** Unsubscribe must be one-click where possible; processing must be complete within 10 business days (CAN-SPAM), immediately for GDPR/TCPA

### 4.5 Misleading Subject Lines

- **BLOCK:** Subject lines that imply a personal relationship where none exists ("Re: Our conversation yesterday" cold outreach)
- **BLOCK:** Subject lines falsely implying urgency or scarcity not reflected in email content
- **BLOCK:** "FWD:" or "RE:" prefixes on non-reply cold outreach emails
- **BLOCK:** Subject lines referencing financial obligation the user doesn't have ("Your invoice," "Payment confirmation," "Action required on your account") for promotional emails
- **BLOCK:** Subject lines containing false news/urgency ("Breaking: Your order has a problem") for promotional content
- **Legal basis:** CAN-SPAM Act Section 5(a)(2) — "It is unlawful for any person to initiate the transmission of any commercial electronic mail message... if such person has actual knowledge, or knowledge fairly implied on the basis of objective circumstances, that a subject heading of the message would be likely to mislead a recipient..."
- **Penalty:** Each email with a deceptive subject line = separate CAN-SPAM violation = up to $53,088; Verkada $2.95M enforcement (2024) included subject line violations
- **FTC Section 5:** Independently prohibits deceptive acts — applies even if technical CAN-SPAM requirements are met

## PART 5: PRODUCT MEDIA ETHICS

### 5.1 Misleading Product Photos (FTC Truth-in-Advertising)

- **BLOCK:** Product photos that materially misrepresent the product being sold
- **BLOCK:** Using photos of a higher-tier/different model to advertise a lower-spec product
- **BLOCK:** Photos showing features, accessories, or components not included with the product without clear labeling ("sold separately" must be visible, not buried)
- **BLOCK:** Scale manipulation in photos that makes products appear significantly larger/smaller than actual dimensions without size reference
- **BLOCK:** Photos showing assembled products that require significant effort/expertise to assemble, where assembly difficulty is material to purchase decision
- **Legal basis:** FTC Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. § 45); FTC Policy Statement on Deception (1983, still binding); FTC Guides for the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials (16 CFR Part 255, updated 2023)
- Truth-in-advertising requires that all elements of an ad (including images) are truthful, not misleading, and substantiated
- **Penalty:** FTC civil penalties under Section 19 ($53,088/day for ongoing violations); restitution to consumers; injunctive relief
- FTC annual report FY2024: $337.3M returned to consumers from advertising enforcement actions (press release Mar 2025: https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/03/new-report-shows-ftc-returned-3373-million-consumers-2024)

### 5.2 Fake Before/After Images

- **BLOCK:** Before/after product images that are not representative of typical results for ordinary users
- **BLOCK:** Before/after images using lighting, makeup, professional staging, or Photoshop alterations that create material differences between conditions
- **BLOCK:** Before/after images where "after" results require additional products, professional treatments, or interventions not included with the product
- **BLOCK:** Time-compressed before/after (showing 6-month results as if achievable in 2 weeks)
- **BLOCK:** Using the same image manipulated in post-production as both "before" and "after"
- **Required disclosures:** If before/after images show above-average results, must include prominent disclaimer: "Results not typical. Individual results may vary." Per FTC Endorsement Guides, disclaimer must be clear and conspicuous — NOT in fine print
- **Legal basis:** FTC Act Section 5; FTC Guides for Advertising Claims Substantiation; FTC guidance on performance claims requires that claims in images, not just text, be truthful and substantiated
- Applies to: health/beauty products, fitness equipment, cleaning products, dietary supplements, skincare — any category where before/after implies transformation

### 5.3 AI-Generated Fake Lifestyle Photos Presented as Real

- **BLOCK:** Presenting AI-generated product lifestyle images as actual product photography without disclosure
- **BLOCK:** AI-generated images showing product use cases or environments materially different from product's actual capabilities
- **BLOCK:** Using AI-generated images to show products in scenarios (e.g., "waterproof" product in water, "load-bearing" product under stress) without real-world performance testing backing the implied claim
- **BLOCK:** AI-generated "customer lifestyle" photos implying real customer experiences that don't exist
- **FTC Operation AI Comply (Sep 2024):** FTC filed 5 enforcement actions against operations using AI to generate deceptive content; signals that AI-generated deceptive imagery is an active FTC priority
- **FTC guidance (2025):** Companies using AI in marketing materials must ensure claims (including visual claims) are truthful and substantiated — Section 5 applies regardless of whether image is AI-generated or photographed
- **EU AI Act (effective Feb 2, 2025):** AI systems used to generate commercial content with "subliminal techniques" that distort consumer behavior face penalties up to 7% of global annual turnover
- **Emerging standard:** Major platforms (Amazon, Shopify, Google Shopping) are developing AI-image disclosure requirements for product listings — anticipate mandatory disclosure requirements by 2026–2027
- **Practical rule:** AI-generated product images must: (1) accurately represent the actual product; (2) not imply real customer usage; (3) be disclosed as AI-generated if asked; (4) not show performance or capabilities not substantiated by testing

#### Materiality distinction — product imagery vs. decorative imagery

The BLOCK rules above apply to AI-generated **product imagery** — images that depict, represent, or imply properties of the product being sold. They do NOT apply to:

- **Decorative/atmospheric imagery** — hero banners, category card backgrounds, newsletter backgrounds, lifestyle mood images that are clearly navigational or atmospheric, not product representations
- **Abstract or generic imagery** — stock-photo-style backgrounds, gradient overlays, pattern fills
- **Images where actual products use real photography** — if every product listing has real photos and only the site chrome uses AI-generated backgrounds, the materiality threshold is not met

**Materiality test:** Would a reasonable consumer make a different purchasing decision about a specific product if they knew the hero banner (or category background, or newsletter image) was AI-generated? If no — the image is decorative, not a BLOCK.

**Auditor guidance:** When encountering AI-generated imagery (detected via filenames like `ChatGPT_Image_*`, `Gemini_Generated_Image_*`, or EXIF metadata), classify as:
- **BLOCK (CRITICAL)** if the image depicts or implies properties of a product being sold (e.g., showing a product installed on a vehicle when no such installation photo exists, implying customer use that never happened)
- **LOW (informational)** if the image is decorative/atmospheric and actual product photos use real photography. Recommend optional disclosure ("Some lifestyle imagery is AI-generated") as a best practice, not a compliance requirement.

**Legal basis for distinction:** FTC Operation AI Comply (Sep 2024) targeted deceptive AI capability claims and fake reviews — zero cases involved decorative imagery. FTC Section 5 materiality test requires the representation to affect purchasing decisions. EU AI Act Article 50 deployer obligations apply to deep fakes, biometrics, and public-interest text — not decorative website backgrounds.

### 5.4 Color Accuracy Misrepresentation

- **BLOCK:** Product images with heavily altered color profiles where color is material to the purchase (e.g., clothing, paint, furniture, consumer electronics)
- **BLOCK:** Using professional studio lighting, post-processing, or color grading that makes product color materially different from actual product color under normal conditions
- **BLOCK:** Color swatch options on product pages that do not accurately represent actual available colors
- **BLOCK:** Photos that show a "Midnight Blue" product as black or a "Coral" product as orange — color naming must correspond to accurate visual representation
- **Legal basis:** FTC Act Section 5; FTC's "reasonable consumer" standard — if a reasonable consumer would believe the product is a different color than what arrives, the image is deceptive
- **Return rate context:** Color misrepresentation is the #1 reason for apparel/home goods returns (studies show 22–30% of returns cite "color different than pictured") — misrepresentation has measurable business AND legal cost
- **Required practice:** At minimum, include a "colors may vary by display device" disclaimer; for color-critical products, provide color chip/swatch with actual Pantone/hex reference; photograph under neutral D65 lighting

## PART 6: CROSS-CUTTING REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

### 6.1 EU AI Act — Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (phased application; do not cite as single in-force date)

**Phased application timeline (Article 113) — cite the specific date that applies:**
- **Feb 2, 2025** — **Article 5 prohibited practices** in force. Covers: subliminal techniques materially distorting behavior *and causing significant harm* (Art 5(1)(a)); exploitation of vulnerabilities by age / disability / socio-economic status (Art 5(1)(b)); social scoring (Art 5(1)(c)); specific biometric/emotion-recognition restrictions. Also in force: Chapter I general provisions + Chapter II AI literacy obligations.
- **Aug 2, 2025** — General-purpose AI model obligations (Chapter V) + governance/notifying authorities (Chapter VII) + Commission rules on high-risk procedures in force.
- **Aug 2, 2026** — **General application** of the Regulation, including **most high-risk system obligations** (Annex III — employment, education, essential private services, law enforcement, etc.). **Pre-Aug-2026 BLOCKs citing "high-risk AI" obligations are premature.**
- **Aug 2, 2027** — Remaining high-risk system obligations (Annex I product safety categories).

**What applies RIGHT NOW (as of 2026-04-22):** Only the Art 5 prohibited-practice BLOCKs plus GPAI and literacy obligations. Standard CRO personalization, dynamic pricing, and recommendation ranking **do NOT automatically fall under Art 5** — the Art 5(1)(a) test requires BOTH subliminal/manipulative technique AND material distortion of behavior causing significant harm. Most ecommerce personalization does not meet that bar; it may fall under the high-risk framework from Aug 2, 2026 depending on use case.

**Penalties:**
- **Up to €35M or 7% of global annual turnover** for Art 5 prohibited practices (Art 99(3)).
- **Up to €15M or 3% of global annual turnover** for most other infringements.
- **Up to €7.5M or 1% of global annual turnover** for providing incorrect information to authorities.

**Auditor guidance:** Do NOT flag ordinary CRO personalization, recommendation engines, or dynamic-pricing algorithms as automatic AI Act BLOCKs. Only flag as Art 5 BLOCK when there is direct evidence of subliminal-technique deployment + material behavior distortion + significant harm. High-risk-system obligations are not live for private ecommerce until Aug 2, 2026 at earliest.

### 6.2 GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

- Requires explicit consent for behavioral tracking, personalization, and marketing
- Right to explanation for automated decision-making affecting consumers (Article 22)
- **Penalties:** Up to 4% of global annual turnover or €20M (whichever higher)
- Applies to any site with EU visitors — not limited to EU-based businesses

### 6.3 CCPA/CPRA Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT) Regulations (phased compliance)

**Approval:** Finalized by OAL Sept 22, 2025; regulations take effect Jan 1, 2026.

**Phased compliance — do not cite any single effective date:**

- **Jan 1, 2026** — regulations take effect. Risk-assessment compliance begins for processing activities initiated after this date. Insurance and cybersecurity-audit provisions apply to newly-initiated activities.
- **Jan 1, 2027** — ADMT consumer rights begin: opt-out, pre-use notice, and access rights apply when a business uses ADMT to make a "Significant Decision" (finance/credit, housing, education, employment, healthcare).
- **Dec 31, 2027** — risk-assessment completion deadline for processing activities that were initiated before 2026 and continue into 2026.
- **Apr 1, 2028** — first CPPA attestation deadline (summary of completed risk assessments).
- **Through 2030** — additional phased milestones.

Applies to: personalized pricing, algorithmic recommendations, behavioral targeting, automated CRO personalization when tied to a Significant Decision. Requires opt-out mechanisms for profiling (effective under the Jan 1, 2027 ADMT rights date).

### 6.4 US State Privacy Laws (20+ states active)

- Indiana, Kentucky, Rhode Island: effective January 1, 2026
- Trend: convergence toward CCPA-like requirements with ADMT provisions
- All include consumer rights for automated decision-making transparency

### 6.5 CA SB 243 (2025) — Companion Chatbots Only (narrow scope)

**Signed:** Governor Newsom, Oct 13, 2025. **Effective:** Jan 1, 2026. Annual reporting to the Office of Suicide Prevention begins Jul 1, 2027.

**Scope — read the definition carefully; the statute is narrower than the name suggests.** SB 243 regulates "companion chatbots" — AI chatbots that sustain multi-turn relationships with users and can generate outputs likely to elicit emotional responses. The statute **explicitly excludes** (statutory carve-outs):

- Bots used ONLY for customer service, business operational purposes, productivity, internal research, or technical assistance
- Bots limited to a video-game context that cannot discuss mental-health, self-harm, sexually-explicit, or other off-topic subjects
- Stand-alone voice-assistant devices (Alexa-style) that do not sustain a multi-interaction relationship or generate emotionally-elicitive output

**What SB 243 requires** (operators in scope):
- Clear/conspicuous notification that the chatbot is artificial when a reasonable person would be misled into thinking it's human
- Special disclosure + safety obligations for known-minor users
- Suicide/self-harm content prevention protocol, published on the operator's website
- Annual reporting to the Office of Suicide Prevention (beginning Jul 1, 2027)

**Penalties:** Private right of action — injury in fact claimant recovers the greater of actual damages or **$1,000 per violation** (no AG-specific penalty cap in the bill text).

**Auditor guidance — do NOT flag as SB 243 BLOCK:**
- Standard customer-service chatbots (explicit statutory carve-out)
- Sales-assistant bots focused on product/transaction help (carve-out)
- Voice assistants / Alexa-style devices (carve-out)

These may still require disclosure under **SB-1001 (bot disclosure, 2018)** for commercial-incentive contexts and election communications — cite SB-1001, not SB 243, for ordinary ecommerce bot-disclosure findings.

### 6.6 Enforcement Posture Note (2025–2026) — Read Before Assessing Enforcement Risk

**Federal enforcement** has shifted under the Trump AI Action Plan: FTC consent-order pipeline has slowed and the FTC has rescinded at least one order (Rytr, Dec 2025). However, FTC statutory authority (FTC Act § 5, ROSCA, CAN-SPAM, Fake Reviews Rule) remains fully enforceable — enforcement posture does not equal non-enforcement. Operation AI Comply (Sep 2024) produced 5 enforcement actions under both Biden and Trump; dark-patterns enforcement continues via ROSCA + § 5 even after Click-to-Cancel vacatur.

**Enforcement intensity is shifting to state AGs and private class actions.** CA AG, NY AG, TX AG, WA AG, and IL AG have all announced expanded consumer-protection enforcement priorities for 2026. Private class action exposure (TCPA, BIPA, CCPA) is accelerating independently of federal enforcement posture. The Lanham Act (§ 43(a)) is litigated by competitors — not regulators — and is entirely unaffected by federal enforcement posture changes.

**International enforcement remains active and unaffected.** CNIL (France), CMA (UK), and CA AG are actively enforcing. CNIL fined Google €325M in September 2025. UK DMCCA 2024 expands CMA dark-pattern enforcement authority.

**Bottom line:** Do not interpret federal enforcement slowdown as permission to reintroduce prohibited practices. State + private + international enforcement vectors remain robust. Statutes are laws regardless of current FTC prioritization. Verify any specific FTC consent order at ftc.gov before citing as live precedent — orders may be rescinded; statutory rules are not. (Quarterly review recommended.)

## PART 7: EXPANDED CLUSTER COVERAGE

Part 7 extends the ethics gate beyond the original CRO-centric scope to cover the full 10-cluster audit surface. Earlier Parts focused on conversion dark patterns, SEO manipulation, pricing, email/SMS, and product imagery. Part 7 adds authority-grade coverage for children's privacy, cookie consent timing, web accessibility, false advertising in metadata, state subscription laws, and cross-border data transfer — the regulatory surface that fires on the trust-credibility, product-media, performance-ux, content-seo, post-purchase, and audience clusters that lacked direct ethics-gate rules.

### 7.1 COPPA — Children's Online Privacy (audience cluster)

- **Authority:** 15 U.S.C. §§ 6501–6506; FTC rule at 16 CFR Part 312. 2025 Final Rule Amendments: **published April 22, 2025** (Fed. Reg. 2025-05904), **effective June 23, 2025**, with a **general compliance deadline of April 22, 2026** (earlier deadlines apply to § 312.11(d)(1), (d)(4), and (g) for FTC-approved COPPA safe-harbor programs). These are the first COPPA updates since 2013. As of 2026-04-22 the compliance window has closed for most regulated entities.
- **Scope:** Applies to any site directed at under-13 users OR any general-audience site with actual knowledge of under-13 users. "Directed at" includes child-themed imagery, age-inappropriate cartoons, or product categories aimed at minors.
- **Violation pattern:** Collecting persistent identifiers, precise geolocation, or behavioral-targeting cookies from under-13 users without verifiable parental consent. Personalization/retargeting features that build profiles on minors.
- **BLOCK:** Third-party ad pixel firing on a product page aimed at children (toys, kids' apparel) with no age gate.
- **ADJACENT:** General-audience store with a "kids" category that fires the same pixel site-wide but has a compliant age-screen at checkout.
- **Penalties:** FTC extracted $520M from Epic Games (2022) and $20M from Microsoft (2023); YouTube paid $170M. Active enforcement priority through 2026.
- Applies to clusters: **audience**, secondary impact on **trust-credibility** (age-gate trust signals) and **performance-ux** (pre-consent tracking)

### 7.2 EU ePrivacy Art 5(3) — Cookie Consent Timing (performance-ux, audience)

- **Authority:** Directive 2002/58/EC Article 5(3), as amended. Already in Source Registry.
- **Scope:** Any site with EU visitors, not only EU-based. Applies across member states; enforced by national DPAs (CNIL, ICO, Garante, etc.).
- **Violation pattern:** Analytics, advertising, or personalization scripts executing on initial page load BEFORE the visitor has given affirmative opt-in consent. "Reject all" hidden behind a second screen or styled as a weaker CTA than "Accept all."
- **BLOCK:** Google Analytics, Meta Pixel, or retargeting tag firing pre-consent. Consent banner labeled "By continuing to use this site you accept cookies" (not affirmative consent).
- **ADJACENT:** Banner present with Accept/Reject buttons but Reject routed through a second modal (CNIL has fined this pattern at €60M — Facebook, 2022 — and €150M — Google, 2022).
- **Penalties:** CNIL fines reach €150M under Art 5(3) specifically; ICO issued enforcement notices 2024–2026 to major e-commerce sites. GDPR tops up for the data-protection consequences.
- Applies to clusters: **performance-ux** (page-load script timing), **audience** (personalization/profiling)

### 7.3 ADA Title III + EAA — Web Accessibility (product-media, performance-ux)

- **US Authority:** Americans with Disabilities Act Title III, 42 U.S.C. § 12182. **No codified technical standard exists for private businesses** — Title III applies to "places of public accommodation," and its web application is interpreted through case law (e.g., *Robles v. Domino's Pizza*, 9th Cir. 2019; *Gil v. Winn-Dixie*, 11th Cir. 2021). Courts generally look to WCAG 2.0 / 2.1 AA as the industry benchmark when evaluating accessibility, but **do NOT frame WCAG 2.1 AA as a binding universal federal rule for private ecommerce** — it is the *de facto* litigation benchmark, not a statutory minimum. Private plaintiffs have a direct right of action; no administrative exhaustion required. The DOJ's March 2024 Web Rule (28 CFR Part 35) applies to **state and local governments only (ADA Title II)** and does NOT bind private ecommerce businesses — do not cite it as a Title III authority.
- **EU Authority:** European Accessibility Act, Directive (EU) 2019/882. Enforcement began **2025-06-28** for e-commerce services placed on the EU market. **EAA does require WCAG 2.1 AA** as the technical baseline for covered services; this is distinct from US Title III where the technical standard is not codified.
- **Technical standard:** WCAG 2.2 AA is the current W3C Recommendation (12 December 2024) and is backward-compatible with 2.1. Recommend targeting WCAG 2.2 AA as the operative standard; 2.1 AA remains the minimum legacy floor that courts and regulators have enforced.
  - **EAA (EU)**: WCAG 2.1 AA is the binding technical standard cited in the directive for covered e-commerce services. WCAG 2.2 AA conformance automatically satisfies 2.1 (backward-compatible).
  - **US ADA Title III**: WCAG 2.0 / 2.1 AA is the *de facto* litigation benchmark courts reference, not a codified federal rule. Do not cite as a binding universal federal rule.
- **Violation pattern:** Product images without alt text; decorative images with misleading alt text (e.g., the CDN filename); form fields without labels; color-only status indicators ("Red means error"); video without captions; icon-only buttons with no accessible name.
- **BLOCK:** "Add to Cart" rendered as an icon-only button (🛒) with no aria-label or visible text fallback — blocks assistive technology entirely.
- **ADJACENT:** Alt text present but describes the file ("thermal-raptor-roof-top-install-video.jpg") instead of the product. Technically non-empty, functionally useless.
- **Penalties:** Over 4,600 ADA web lawsuits filed in US in 2024 (Seyfarth tracker); settlement amounts typically $15K–$75K per site plus mandatory remediation. EAA adds an EU layer for any seller shipping to EU customers.
- Applies to clusters: **product-media** (alt text, gallery controls, video captions), **performance-ux** (keyboard navigation, focus management, layout shift as a cognitive accessibility factor), **visual-cta** (icon-only CTAs, color-only states)

### 7.4 Lanham Act § 43(a) — False Advertising in Metadata (content-seo cluster)

- **Authority:** 15 U.S.C. § 1125(a). Private right of action — competitors sue directly without waiting for FTC. Most-litigated false-advertising statute in US federal court (~500 suits/year).
- **Scope:** Meta titles, meta descriptions, schema-surfaced content, Open Graph tags, and any on-page claim that reaches search results. "Puffery" is protected; specific comparative claims are not.
- **Violation pattern:** Comparative claims ("#1 rated," "Better than [competitor]," "Voted best 2026") without substantiation. Unverified superlatives in schema markup that surface as rich snippets.
- **BLOCK:** Title tag "Best [product] 2026 — Top-Rated by Experts" with no on-page evidence of a ranking methodology or cited expert source. Schema `aggregateRating` declaring 5.0 stars with zero/few reviews backing it.
- **ADJACENT:** "Our most popular [product]" — puffery; genuinely popular doesn't invite Lanham liability. "Customer favorite" — subjective, generally safe.
- **Penalties:** Private plaintiffs recover lost profits + treble damages + attorney's fees. No vacatur risk like the FTC Click-to-Cancel rule — Lanham Act is statutory.
- Applies to clusters: **content-seo** (meta titles, descriptions, schema content), secondary impact on **trust-credibility** (substantiating review claims) and **pricing** (comparative "save vs. retail" framing)

### 7.5 California Automatic Renewal Law — CA ARL + AB-2863 (post-purchase cluster)

- **Authority:** California Business & Professions Code §§ 17600–17606, significantly strengthened by AB-2863 effective **2025-07-01**. Distinct from ROSCA (federal) and the vacated FTC Click-to-Cancel rule — ARL remains active and enforced.
- **Key 2025 changes:** AB-2863 added an online-cancel parity requirement: if signup is online, cancel MUST be available online (no phone-only cancel). Also annual renewal reminder for subs ≥ 1 year, and post-signup acknowledgment email with cancel instructions.
- **Violation pattern:** Online signup paired with phone-only or mail-only cancel. No annual renewal reminder for long subs. No post-signup email documenting cancel path. Auto-renew trial converting to paid without clear pre-renewal notice.
- **BLOCK:** Online signup; cancel requires calling a phone number during business hours. (Hulu, Adobe, and Sirius all settled ARL suits on this fact pattern.)
- **ADJACENT:** Online cancel exists but is buried two menus deep behind an FAQ link. (Likely survives a motion to dismiss but invites regulatory attention.)
- **Penalties:** Enforced by CA AG + private UCL (Business & Professions Code § 17200) plaintiffs. Typical settlements: $5M–$25M plus remediation. Class certification routinely granted.
- Applies to clusters: **post-purchase** (cancel-path design, renewal notices, acknowledgment email), **checkout-flows** (signup-flow disclosure)

### 7.6 EU–US Data Privacy Framework — Cross-Border Data Transfer (audience, checkout-flows)

- **Authority:** European Commission adequacy decision 2023-07-10 (replacing Privacy Shield post-Schrems II). Already in Source Registry. Supplements GDPR Articles 45–46 (international transfer mechanisms).
- **Scope:** Any US e-commerce site collecting EU customer data — account registrations, checkout data, email signups, analytics pixels pointing at US servers. Applies even if the business has no EU entity.
- **Violation pattern:** Two separate issues that must NOT be conflated — (a) the actual **transfer-mechanism gap** (using a US processor that lacks a valid DPF certification AND is not covered by Standard Contractual Clauses or another Art 46 mechanism — this is a substantive GDPR Chapter V violation) vs (b) a **notice gap** (the vendor has a valid DPF/SCC basis, but the privacy policy fails to disclose it). These carry different regulatory severity.
- **BLOCK:** Checkout embeds a US payment/analytics vendor that has **NO valid transfer basis** — no DPF certification, no SCCs, no BCRs, no other Art 46 mechanism. This is a substantive transfer violation under GDPR Chapter V.
- **ADJACENT:** Vendor has a valid transfer mechanism (DPF-certified OR covered by SCCs) but the privacy policy fails to name the vendor or identify the legal basis. **This is a notice/transparency compliance gap, NOT a transfer gap** — the data transfer is legally covered; the disclosure is what's weak. Do not flag as BLOCK on transfer-regime grounds; flag as ADJACENT with a GDPR Art 13-14 notice-adequacy framing.
- **Penalties:** Meta's €1.2B fine (May 2023) was under this transfer regime pre-DPF. Noyb (Max Schrems' org) filed 101 complaints across EU post-Schrems II specifically targeting US transfers.
- Applies to clusters: **audience** (personalization pixels, identity providers), **checkout-flows** (payment processors, fraud-detection services), **performance-ux** (analytics/monitoring vendors)

### 7.7 Washington My Health Data Act (WA MHMDA) — Inferred Health Data (audience)

- **Authority:** Washington My Health Data Act (SB 1155), RCW 70.372. Effective March 31, 2024; expanded scope July 1, 2024.
- **Scope:** Any entity that: (a) is organized for profit, (b) conducts business in Washington or targets WA residents, AND (c) determines the purpose and means of collecting, processing, sharing, or selling consumer health data. Covers health data **inferred** from behavioral data — browsing patterns that may reveal health conditions qualify.
- **BLOCK:** Advertising platforms or analytics tools that collect browsing behavioral patterns on a site with health-adjacent categories (supplements, fitness equipment, mental-health apps, sleep products, medical devices) may be collecting inferrable health data without the required WA MHMDA authorization. If no authorization mechanism is in place and the site targets WA residents, this is a BLOCK.
- **ADJACENT:** Site collects standard behavioral analytics (page views, click paths) but does not operate in a health-adjacent category and does not specifically target WA residents. Monitor for category creep.
- **Penalties:** WA AG enforcement; private right of action. Penalty structure mirrors WA Consumer Protection Act.
- **Primary source:** RCW 70.372 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=70.372
- Applies to clusters: **audience** (tracking/personalization), **performance-ux** (pre-consent analytics scripts)

### 7.8 UK Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (UK DMCCA) — Dark Patterns (post-purchase, checkout-flows)

- **Authority:** UK Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (Royal Assent May 24, 2024; c. 13). Consumer protection provisions phased in 2025–2026. CMA enforcement authority.
- **Scope:** Applies to businesses offering goods/services to UK consumers. CMA (Competition and Markets Authority) has direct enforcement authority for dark patterns; consumer protection provisions create private right of action.
- **Penalties:** Up to **10% of global annual turnover** for breaches of consumer protection provisions. CMA can impose remedies, fines, and behavioral orders without going to court.
- **ADJACENT:** Subscription dark patterns (pre-checked add-ons, no prominent cancel path, drip-pricing at checkout) for UK-facing stores. The DMCCA 2024 codifies existing CMA guidance on unfair commercial practices — patterns that were previously enforcement-risk are now explicitly statutory violations.
- **Primary source:** UK DMCCA 2024 — https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2024/13/contents
- Applies to clusters: **post-purchase** (subscription cancel paths), **checkout-flows** (drip pricing, add-on pre-checks)

### 7.9 EU Omnibus Directive — Price History Disclosure for Discounts (pricing cluster)

- **Authority:** EU Omnibus Directive (Directive 2019/2161, amending Directive 98/6/EC Art 6a), implemented across EU member states January 28, 2022.
- **Scope:** Any EU-facing e-commerce offering discounted prices. Applies to all sellers offering to EU consumers, including non-EU-based sellers.
- **BLOCK:** Flash sales, strikethrough pricing, or "sale" claims for EU-facing stores that do not display the **30-day lowest price** prior to the discount as the reference price. Using an inflated "was" price that was never the lowest price in the prior 30 days as the strikethrough reference is explicitly prohibited.
- **Required disclosure format:** "Prior price: €X" where €X = the lowest price charged by the seller in the 30 days preceding the promotional reduction. Cannot use MSRP or competitor pricing as the reference.
- **Penalty:** Varies by member state; EU-wide the maximum is not less than 4% of annual turnover in affected member states (per Directive) or €2M, whichever higher. National transpositions vary.
- **Primary source:** Directive 2019/2161/EU — https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2019/2161/oj
- Applies to clusters: **pricing** (flash sales, discount framing), **content-seo** (meta descriptions citing "X% off" claims)

### 7.10 Quebec Law 25 — Personal Information Protection (audience, checkout-flows)

- **Authority:** An Act to Modernize Legislative Provisions as Regards the Protection of Personal Information (Law 25 / Bill 64), SQ 2021, c. 25. Fully in force September 22, 2023.
- **Scope:** Any business collecting personal information from Quebec residents, regardless of where the business is located. Stricter than PIPEDA (federal Canadian privacy law) in several respects.
- **Key requirements:** (1) Privacy policy must disclose purposes of data collection with sufficient precision; (2) Explicit consent required for sensitive information and secondary uses; (3) Right to data portability and de-indexing; (4) Privacy impact assessments required before collecting personal information.
- **BLOCK:** Quebec-facing stores collecting personal data (checkout names/addresses, email addresses, behavioral tracking) without a Quebec-compliant privacy policy that clearly discloses the specific purposes of data collection and processing.
- **ADJACENT:** Privacy policy exists but uses vague, catch-all language ("we may use your data for marketing and business purposes") that fails Law 25's precision requirement — sufficient for PIPEDA but not Law 25.
- **Penalties:** CAD $25M or 4% of worldwide turnover (whichever higher) for intentional violations; CAD $10M or 2% for other violations.
- **Primary source:** SQ 2021, c. 25 — https://www.legisquebec.gouv.qc.ca/en/document/cs/p-39.1
- Applies to clusters: **audience** (data collection/personalization), **checkout-flows** (data collection at checkout)

## OVERRIDE RULE

**When in doubt, recommend the ethical alternative.**

Dark patterns are not just unethical — they are increasingly illegal across federal, state, and international jurisdictions. Measurable business data shows they are also counterproductive:
- Fake urgency: 17.6% sales decrease
- Hidden fees: 39–48% of cart abandonment
- Fake reviews: FTC fines + permanent brand damage when exposed
- Subscription traps: $2.5B settlement (Amazon), class action exposure for smaller operators

**The standard for every recommendation in this engine:**
> Would this practice be published in a national newspaper as deceptive?
> Would it withstand scrutiny from an FTC investigator, EU DPA, or class action plaintiff's attorney?
> Does it create genuine value for the user, or does it create value by deceiving them?

If the answer to any of these questions raises doubt, **recommend the ethical alternative**.

Ethics is not a compliance checkbox — it is the foundation of sustainable e-commerce. Companies built on manipulation get destroyed by regulators, class actions, and the users they exploited.
