## Executive Summary

### Top 3 Most Impactful Findings

1. **The 5-second test is the single most actionable evaluation tool for hero sections** (Finding 1) — If a visitor cannot answer "What does this company do? Why should I care?" within 5 seconds of seeing the hero, the headline needs rewriting. This simple heuristic catches the majority of hero section failures.

2. **Hero image direction cues redirect visitor attention — toward or away from the CTA** (Finding 6) — Eye direction in hero images is not decorative. A person looking toward the CTA directs visitor attention toward it; a person looking away steals attention from it. This is a free, zero-effort optimization that eyetracking research consistently validates.

3. **Above-fold CTA visibility on mobile is the primary hero failure mode** (Finding 8) — On mobile, hero images that are too large push the CTA below the fold, where it receives dramatically less attention. Reducing hero image height on mobile is the single fastest structural fix for mobile conversion.

## Findings

### Finding 1: The 5-Second Test — Clarity Is the Foundational Hero Metric
- **Source**: Nielsen Norman Group (NNGroup), "How Long Do Users Stay on Web Pages?" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/how-long-do-users-stay-on-web-pages/ ; Peep Laja / CXL Institute, "Does Your Website Pass The 5 Second Test?" — https://cxl.com/blog/5-second-test/ ; UsabilityHub (now Maze), five-second test methodology
- **Methodology**: NNGroup research analyzed visit duration across websites. Subsequent practitioner application formalized the "5-second test" as a usability methodology: show a page to an unfamiliar participant for 5 seconds, hide it, then ask key questions. The test is used by thousands of product teams and has been validated across multiple research contexts.
- **Key Finding**: The average visitor decides whether to stay or leave within the first 10-20 seconds of visiting a page. Within the first 5 seconds, they form a strong impression of relevance and credibility. If visitors cannot answer two questions after 5 seconds — "What does this company do?" and "Why should I care?" — the hero section is failing at its fundamental task. This is not a creativity or aesthetics standard; it is a clarity standard.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Run 5-second tests on every hero section before launch using Maze, UsabilityHub, or informal hallway testing. The test question protocol: (1) "What does this company sell or do?", (2) "What would you do next if you needed this?", (3) "What makes this different from competitors?" If 70%+ of participants cannot answer Question 1 correctly, the headline is failing. Failing Question 2 means the CTA is not prominent enough. Failing Question 3 means no differentiation has been communicated.
- **Replication Status**: NNGroup visit duration research is replicated across multiple studies. The 5-second test methodology is validated as a usability tool by Maze, UsabilityHub, and thousands of product teams. The specific causal link (5-second clarity → conversion) is extensively validated through CRO practice.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The 5-second test measures clarity, not conversion. A page can be clear and still fail to convert due to price, trust issues, or audience mismatch. High-consideration products may need more than 5-second clarity — the goal is initial comprehension, not complete persuasion in 5 seconds. Familiarity effects: repeat visitors who already understand the brand may not fail the 5-second test even with a poor hero section.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 2: The Value Proposition Must Pass the "In-Context" Test
- **Source**: Conversion Rate Experts (CRE), practitioner analysis and multiple case studies — https://conversion-rate-experts.com/ ; CXL Institute, "Unique Value Proposition: How to Create a UVP (With 7 Examples)" — https://cxl.com/blog/value-proposition-examples-how-to-create/ ; Steve Blank, Stanford entrepreneurship curriculum (Customer Development methodology)
- **Methodology**: Conversion Rate Experts synthesized findings from hundreds of landing page audits. CXL Institute synthesized practitioner A/B test results. The "in-context" test is a practitioner heuristic validated by CRO professionals.
- **Key Finding**: The single most common hero section failure is a value proposition that is either: (1) generic — could apply to any competitor ("Quality products, delivered fast"), or (2) internally-focused — describes what the company does rather than what the customer gets ("Award-winning manufacturer since 1987"). A passing value proposition answers: "What do I get? For me specifically? That I can't get elsewhere?" Format that consistently tests well: [What you do] for [specific audience] with [specific differentiator] — "Custom leather wallets for men who buy once and keep for decades / Handmade in USA / Lifetime warranty."
- **E-Commerce Application**: Test your hero headline by asking: could a competitor copy this headline verbatim? If yes, it lacks differentiation. Could you put a competitor's logo on your hero and have it still make sense? If yes, your hero is not specific enough. The most common fix: replace category claims ("Premium mattress") with outcome-specific differentiation ("The mattress that cold sleepers swear by — with temperature-regulating copper gel built in").
- **Replication Status**: Practitioner consensus across the major CRO firms (CRE, CXL, Conversion Sciences). No single peer-reviewed study — convergence of thousands of landing page audits.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Highly recognized brands (Apple, Nike, Patagonia) can use abstract hero statements because brand equity carries the meaning ("Think Different"). Unknown brands cannot — they need explicit value propositions. As brand recognition grows, the need for explicit differentiation in the hero decreases.
- **Citation Status**: CXL Institute URL (cxl.com/blog/value-proposition-examples-how-to-create/) was inaccessible (403 Cloudflare block) during the 2026-04-21 audit. This finding is retained as Bronze tier — practitioner consensus across CRE, CXL, and Steve Blank's Customer Development methodology. The directional claim (generic value propositions underperform specific ones) is convergent across thousands of landing page audits.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 3: Visual Hierarchy Squint Test — Five-Level Contrast Framework
- **Source**: Andy Budd, "Mastering CSS Design"; NNGroup, visual hierarchy research https://www.nngroup.com/articles/visual-hierarchy-ux-definition/; Luke Wroblewski, "Web Form Design" (2008); practitioner synthesis from multiple UX sources
- **Methodology**: The "squint test" is a practitioner heuristic derived from visual design theory and validated through usability testing. Squinting reduces the page to blurred shapes and forces evaluation of only the largest visual elements — revealing whether hierarchy is properly established.
- **Key Finding**: A properly structured hero section passes the "squint test": squinting until details blur should reveal exactly one dominant element (usually the headline or CTA), then secondary elements that are clearly smaller, then tertiary elements. If squinting reveals 2-3 elements at nearly equal visual weight, hierarchy is broken and users experience micro-paralysis at the moment of first impression. Hierarchy is created through five variables in priority order: (1) Size — largest element gets first attention; (2) Contrast — highest-contrast element attracts fixation; (3) Color — saturated vs. muted; (4) Whitespace — isolated elements appear more important; (5) Position — top-left bias (eye-tracking). All five variables must agree to create strong hierarchy; conflicting signals (e.g., large but low-contrast) dilute the effect.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Apply the squint test to every hero section during design review. The dominant element should be either the primary headline or the CTA button — not the hero image, not the navigation, not a promotional badge. If the CTA button fails to dominate visually in the squint test, increase its size, contrast, or surrounding whitespace. On mobile, run the squint test on the actual device — desktop mockups systematically underestimate mobile visual density problems.
- **Replication Status**: Squint test is a practitioner tool, not a peer-reviewed methodology. The underlying principles (size, contrast, position) are validated through NNGroup and Baymard eyetracking research. Visual hierarchy as a conversion driver is well-validated through CRO practice.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The squint test evaluates static hierarchy. Animated elements and video override the squint test — motion captures attention regardless of static visual hierarchy. On pages with hero video, the video is inherently the dominant element whether designers intend it or not.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver

### Finding 4: Required Above-Fold Elements — Minimum Viable Hero
- **Source**: Baymard Institute, "Homepage & Category UX Best Practices" — https://baymard.com/blog/current-state-of-ecommerce-category-ux and product page research; NNGroup, above-fold content research (https://www.nngroup.com/articles/scrolling-and-attention/); CXL Institute practitioner synthesis
- **Methodology**: Baymard usability testing across major e-commerce sites. NNGroup eyetracking across hundreds of pages. CXL synthesis of practitioner A/B tests.
- **Key Finding**: Based on eyetracking research and usability testing, five elements are required to be visible above the fold on landing pages for maximum first-impression conversion: (1) **Headline** — primary value proposition, left-aligned on desktop, left or center on mobile; (2) **Subheadline** — one additional persuasive statement (differentiator, risk reversal, or mechanism); (3) **Primary CTA** — single, visually distinct button; (4) **Hero Image or Video** — visual that supports (not contradicts) the headline; (5) **One Trust Signal** — review count, customer count, security badge, or media mention. All five elements visible above the fold has been correlated with significantly higher conversion rates in multiple A/B tests than pages missing any element. Baymard's benchmarking of major e-commerce sites has consistently found the trust-signal element to be the most commonly missing of the five — a large share of cold-traffic landing pages ship without any visible above-fold credibility indicator.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Audit every landing page: on the most common viewport size for your traffic (check analytics), are all five elements visible without scrolling? On desktop: typically 1024-1280px width, 768-900px height. On mobile: typically 375px width, 667-812px height. If any element is missing or requires scrolling to reach, that is the highest-priority fix. Note: "trust signal" can be as simple as "★★★★★ 4.8 (3,247 reviews)" displayed near the headline — it need not be a badge.
- **Replication Status**: Baymard methodology is well-validated. The five-element framework converges across multiple research sources.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: The previously-stated "63% of landing pages lack a visible trust signal above the fold" specific figure could not be anchored to the cited Baymard URL during the 2026-04-21 audit. The directional claim is retained; the specific numeric has been removed pending independent sourcing.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The "five elements above the fold" rule is for cold-traffic landing pages. Product detail pages (PDPs) on established e-commerce sites follow different conventions — the product image and price/CTA pairing may be sufficient if the navigation provides brand context. Single-product landing pages (for paid traffic) benefit most strictly from this framework.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 5: Secondary CTA — Reducing Commitment Creates an On-Ramp
- **Source**: Oli Gardner, Unbounce, "Secondary CTAs" — https://unbounce.com/; CXL Institute practitioner research https://cxl.com/blog/; multiple A/B tests on dual-CTA hero sections
- **Methodology**: Unbounce and CXL synthesis of A/B tests comparing single-CTA vs. dual-CTA hero sections across hundreds of landing pages.
- **Key Finding**: Adding a low-commitment secondary CTA alongside a high-commitment primary CTA can increase total conversions (combining both) while maintaining or increasing primary CTA clicks in some contexts. The secondary CTA serves users who are not ready for the primary commitment. Example: Primary = "Start my free trial" / Secondary = "See how it works" (watch video or view demo). The secondary CTA should be visually subordinate — ghost/outline style or text link — so it does not compete with the primary. Critical distinction: dual CTAs work when the two CTAs serve genuinely different commitment levels. Dual CTAs fail when they serve the same user at the same commitment level (e.g., "Buy Now" + "Add to Cart" creates confusion with no commitment reduction).
- **E-Commerce Application**: For landing pages with significant new-visitor traffic or complex/high-ticket products, consider a secondary CTA that reduces commitment: "Learn more," "See it in action," "Watch the demo," "Read the reviews." Keep the secondary CTA 30-40% smaller than the primary and use outline/ghost styling to prevent visual competition. Monitor both CTAs' conversion rates separately — if the secondary CTA dominates, the primary CTA may be asking for too much commitment too early.
- **Replication Status**: Dual-CTA effectiveness is context-dependent. Some A/B tests show dual CTAs increase total conversion; others show they reduce primary CTA clicks. Directional finding: dual CTAs help when the secondary CTA is genuinely lower-commitment and visually subordinate.
- **Boundary Conditions**: **Cross-reference:** See `cta-design-and-placement.md` Finding 9 — single CTA focus can increase clicks by up to 371% in email and landing page contexts. The single-vs-dual CTA debate depends on the audience's consideration level. For impulse products, single CTA wins. For considered purchases, dual CTAs may help.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 6: Hero Image Eye Direction Redirects Visitor Attention
- **Source**: James Breeze, "You look where they look" — 2009 eye-tracking study (106 participants, Tobii eye-tracker). Originally at usableworld.com.au; after the domain move, the study and related Breeze research are hosted at https://www.objectiveexperience.com/eye-tracking-ux-research/. Tobii eye-tracking research on human face direction effects. **Citation hygiene note (2026-04-22):** the NNGroup "Photos as Web Content" article (nngroup.com/articles/photos-as-web-content/) does NOT cover gaze direction — it covers stock-vs-authentic photo quality — and was removed from this finding's source list. It remains a correct citation for Finding 7 (hero image types).
- **Methodology**: James Breeze's study (106 participants, Tobii eye-tracker) showed participants landing pages with baby images looking directly at camera vs. looking toward text/CTA. NNGroup conducted parallel research on face direction effects on attention distribution.
- **Key Finding**: When a human face in a hero image looks directly at the camera (at the viewer), visitor attention focuses on the face and does not transfer to page content. When the face looks toward the headline, CTA, or key content, visitor eye gaze follows the direction of the gaze — attention is redirected toward the important content. The Breeze study showed that face-toward-content produced dramatically more fixations on the page text vs. face-at-camera which produced fixations concentrated on the face. The effect is driven by the strong human tendency to follow gaze direction (a deeply wired social attention mechanism).
- **E-Commerce Application**: When using human models or characters in hero images, orient them toward (not away from) the CTA or headline. This is zero-cost and requires only image selection or post-production cropping. Lifestyle images showing someone using the product while facing the CTA side of the layout are optimal. **Avoid**: Images of people staring at the camera (engagement photography style) when placed adjacent to a CTA — they steal attention. **Use**: Images of people looking down at a product, toward the CTA, or in profile toward the text content.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated across multiple eye-tracking studies. The gaze-following mechanism is well-established in social cognition research (Baron-Cohen et al., 1997). The application to web layouts is replicated by Tobii research.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The effect is strong for faces but weaker for non-human directional cues (arrows, pointing fingers). The hero image is competing with many other visual elements — gaze direction is one factor among several. On mobile, layout compression may reduce the spatial relationship between the face and the CTA, weakening the directional effect.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 7: Hero Image Types — Which Perform and Why
- **Source**: CXL Institute, multiple A/B test syntheses; Baymard Institute product image research; NNGroup, "Photos as Web Content" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/photos-as-web-content/; Nielsen, "Authentic Photos vs Stock Photos" (eyetracking) — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/photos-as-web-content/
- **Methodology**: NNGroup eyetracking comparing stock photos vs authentic photography. Baymard usability testing across product pages. CXL practitioner synthesis across hundreds of A/B tests.
- **Key Finding**: NNGroup's eyetracking research found that generic stock photos (people in suits shaking hands, diverse teams smiling at laptops) are treated as decorative noise — users' eyes skip over them entirely. Authentic product-in-use photography and real team/founder photos receive significantly more fixation time. Ranking of hero image types by conversion performance (from highest to lowest): (1) Product in-context/in-use — customer using the product, showing scale and real-world application; (2) Before/after — when honest and visually clear; (3) Face looking toward CTA — human connection with attention direction; (4) Abstract supporting mood — for service businesses where physical product doesn't exist; (5) Generic stock — near zero positive impact, some negative impact on perceived credibility.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Commission original product photography before optimizing hero layouts. Even a single high-quality in-context product photo outperforms well-designed hero layouts with generic stock images. For apparel: model wearing the product in a real environment outperforms studio/white-background. For software/SaaS: screenshot in realistic context outperforms abstract illustration. For physical products: product on a surface with real environment cues outperforms white-background product shots in hero positions.
- **Replication Status**: NNGroup stock photo finding is well-replicated. The hierarchy of hero image types is from practitioner meta-analysis, not a single study.
- **Boundary Conditions**: For technical products where the buyer needs to evaluate specifications, product-on-white-background may be appropriate in the hero to show the product clearly. The "authentic beats stock" finding is strongest for lifestyle, consumer, and service categories.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 8: Mobile Hero Optimization — Above-Fold CTA Is the Primary Failure Mode
- **Source**: Baymard Institute, mobile UX research — https://baymard.com/research/mcommerce-usability; Google, "Mobile Speed and Revenue" study — think.storage.googleapis.com; NNGroup, "Scrolling and Attention" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/scrolling-and-attention/
- **Methodology**: Baymard mobile usability testing. Google large-scale analysis of mobile page performance and conversion. NNGroup mobile scrolling behavior research.
- **Key Finding**: On mobile, the #1 hero section failure is the CTA being pushed below the fold by an oversized hero image. Mobile screens (375-430px wide, 667-926px tall for common iOS/Android) have significantly less vertical real estate than desktop. A hero image appropriate for desktop (600-800px tall) will push the CTA off-screen on mobile, where it receives only 44% of the viewing attention that above-fold content receives (vs 57% for above-fold content on desktop — NNGroup). The solution: use separate mobile hero image sizing (200-300px maximum hero image height on mobile), shorten mobile headlines (2 lines maximum at 320-375px widths), and make the CTA a full-width button (easier to tap, more visible). **Cross-reference**: See `eye-tracking-and-scan-patterns.md` Finding 4 for the 57%/44% above-fold viewing time distribution.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Test every hero section on real mobile devices (not just browser dev tools). Check: (1) Is the CTA visible without scrolling on iPhone SE (375x667)? (2) Is the headline readable at 16px minimum without zooming? (3) Is the CTA at least 48px tall for easy tapping? (4) Is the hero image using `srcset` to serve a smaller/cropped version on mobile? If the answer to any of these is no, fix before launch. Use Google PageSpeed Insights' mobile screenshot to quickly check above-fold rendering.
- **Replication Status**: Baymard mobile UX findings are extensively validated. NNGroup above/below-fold attention data is replicated. Google mobile conversion correlation studies are large-scale.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Mobile users scroll more readily than desktop users (NNGroup scrolling behavior research), so being below-fold on mobile is less catastrophic than being below-fold on desktop — but it still reduces conversion. The CTA should be above-fold AND sticky (following scroll) for maximum mobile performance. **Cross-reference**: See `cta-design-and-placement.md` Finding 11 for sticky CTA research (5-37% conversion lift).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 9: Minimal Navigation in Hero — Reducing Exit Points
- **Source**: Unbounce, "Landing Page Best Practices" — https://unbounce.com/; Oli Gardner research on navigation removal; multiple A/B tests compiled by CXL Institute https://cxl.com/blog/
- **Methodology**: Unbounce's research across thousands of hosted landing pages. CXL synthesis of navigation removal A/B tests.
- **Key Finding**: Navigation menus in hero sections of dedicated landing pages provide exit points that reduce conversion. A/B tests consistently show that removing or minimizing navigation on landing pages (particularly paid-traffic landing pages) increases conversion rate. The principle: every navigation link is a competing CTA that can draw the user away from the primary conversion goal. In one widely cited Unbounce analysis, conversion rates on pages with no navigation were significantly higher than on pages with full navigation.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For dedicated landing pages (paid social, paid search, email campaigns): remove navigation entirely or reduce to logo only + possibly a phone number. For site-wide product landing pages (part of the main site): minimize navigation — use a single-level nav without dropdown menus in the hero area. Never place a full mega-menu navigation in a landing page hero section. This principle applies to checkout pages also — reducing navigation at checkout is a proven conversion tactic. **Important distinction**: Homepage heroes on e-commerce sites benefit from some navigation because the user may need to browse categories; dedicated campaign landing pages benefit from navigation removal.
- **Replication Status**: Navigation removal on landing pages is one of the most consistent findings in CRO. Multiple large-scale A/B tests confirm. Directional variance: some tests show larger lifts (30%+) and some show smaller effects, but the direction is consistently positive for navigation removal.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Removing all navigation can hurt conversion when users have strong trust concerns and want to verify the site is real (e.g., ability to navigate to "About Us" or "Contact"). For unknown brands, leaving a minimal nav or footer with trust links may outperform complete navigation removal. A/B testing in context is recommended.
- **Citation Status**: This finding represents practitioner consensus across Unbounce, CXL, and major CRO practitioners. The specific Unbounce analysis is widely cited in CRO literature but a direct article URL anchor was not available during the 2026-04-21 audit. Bronze tier reflects the practitioner-consensus basis.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 10: Trust Signal in Hero — The Single Minimum Viable Trust Element
- **Source**: Baymard Institute, landing page and checkout research; Stanford Web Credibility Project (B.J. Fogg, 2002) — https://credibility.stanford.edu/; CXL practitioner synthesis
- **Methodology**: Baymard usability testing on trust signal placement. Stanford / Consumer WebWatch study of 4,500+ participants on website credibility (Fogg, Soohoo, Danielson, Marable, Stanford, and Tauber, 2002, "How Do People Evaluate a Web Site's Credibility?"). CXL A/B test synthesis.
- **Key Finding**: A single, prominent trust signal visible in the hero section (without scrolling) is associated with significantly higher conversion rates than hero sections with no trust signals. The minimum viable trust signal for cold traffic is a review aggregate: "★★★★★ 4.8 stars from 3,247 reviews" placed below the headline or subheadline. For unknown brands, this signal reduces the "is this real?" skepticism that causes immediate bounce. The Stanford Web Credibility research (Fogg et al. 2002, "How Do People Evaluate a Web Site's Credibility?", Stanford / Consumer WebWatch) found that users assess website credibility within seconds, primarily based on visual design cues (46.1% of credibility assessments) across 4,500+ participants. **Cross-reference**: Full trust signal research is in `trust-and-credibility.md`. Placement rules (proximity to CTA vs. general page placement) are also in that file.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Add one trust signal to the hero section of every landing page with cold traffic. Priority order: (1) Aggregate review rating with count (if 50+ reviews), (2) Customer count ("Used by 40,000+ professionals"), (3) Recognized media mention ("As seen in Forbes, TechCrunch"), (4) Security/satisfaction badge ("30-day money-back guarantee"). Keep it compact — trust signals should not dominate the visual hierarchy. A small star rating below the headline achieves maximum impact with minimal visual weight.
- **Replication Status**: Baymard trust signal placement research is well-validated. Stanford credibility research is foundational and widely replicated. The specific impact of hero trust signals on conversion is from practitioner research.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Trust signals only work if they are authentic. Fake review counts or fabricated media mentions create stronger negative reactions than no trust signal when users verify them. Low review counts (<50) or low ratings (<4.0) should not be displayed in the hero — they hurt more than help.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 11: Background Video in Hero — Performance vs. Engagement Trade-Off
- **Source**: Google, "Lighthouse Performance Scoring" methodology — developers.google.com/web/tools/lighthouse; Videowise 2026 benchmarks — vendor; Wistia "State of Video" 2025 — wistia.com; Baymard Institute, "UX Research on Product Page Videos: Where and How to Embed Them (35% Get it Wrong)" (2019) — https://baymard.com/blog/embedding-product-page-videos
- **Methodology**: Google Lighthouse performance scoring is a technical standard. Videowise vendor benchmarks from shoppable video platform data. Wistia engagement analysis from hosted video platform. Baymard usability testing.
- **Key Finding**: Hero background video creates an engagement/performance tension: it increases visual richness and brand perception but significantly increases page load time and Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) — the primary Core Web Vitals metric that Google uses for ranking. A 2-5MB autoplay background video can add 1-3 seconds to LCP on 4G connections. Google's CrUX data shows that each 1-second increase in load time reduces mobile conversion by approximately 20% (Google "Speed Matters" study). **Technical requirements for hero video**: always `muted` and `playsinline` attributes; poster image (fallback); maximum 2MB compressed file size for the hero loop; serve a static image fallback on mobile where autoplay may be restricted. **Cross-reference**: See `video-integration.md` for full video type, placement, and performance optimization guidance.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Use background hero video only when: (1) the video communicates something impossible to convey with a static image (motion is the product — e.g., apparel movement, food preparation), AND (2) the video is compressed below 2MB, AND (3) a poster image is provided, AND (4) the video does not affect LCP (test with Google PageSpeed Insights before launch). For most e-commerce products, a high-quality static hero image will outperform background video on combined conversion × performance metrics. Video belongs in the product gallery, not necessarily in the page hero.
- **Replication Status**: Google CrUX performance/conversion data is large-scale and well-validated. Autoplay video restrictions are platform standards (iOS/Safari, Chrome data-saver mode). Video engagement data is vendor-sourced.
- **Boundary Conditions**: High-internet-speed markets (South Korea, Japan, US urban) have lower load time sensitivity. Lifestyle brands targeting design-oriented demographics may see brand perception benefits from hero video that offset the performance cost. A/B testing video vs. static on your specific traffic is the only way to know for your context.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver

### Finding 12: LCP Optimization for Hero Images — Technical Requirement
- **Source**: Google, "Largest Contentful Paint (LCP)" — https://web.dev/articles/lcp (definition and thresholds, WebFetch-verified 2026-04-21); Google, "Optimize Largest Contentful Paint" — https://web.dev/articles/optimize-lcp (optimization techniques); Google CrUX (Chrome User Experience Report) — developer.chrome.com; Cloudflare research on image optimization impact
- **Methodology**: Google's Core Web Vitals program measures LCP across Chrome users via CrUX. LCP targets are: Good < 2.5s, Needs Improvement 2.5-4.0s, Poor > 4.0s. These targets are based on Google's analysis of the relationship between load time and user engagement/conversion.
- **Key Finding**: The hero image is the most common LCP element on landing pages — the largest content element visible in the initial viewport. LCP is a Google ranking signal (since 2021) and directly impacts organic traffic in addition to conversion. Key technical requirements for hero image LCP optimization: (1) Use WebP format (30-50% smaller than JPEG with equivalent quality); (2) Set `loading="eager"` and `fetchpriority="high"` on the hero image; (3) Never lazy-load the hero image (a common but destructive default in many frameworks); (4) Use `srcset` with mobile-optimized variants (target < 100KB for mobile hero images, < 300KB for desktop); (5) Preload the hero image: `<link rel="preload" as="image" href="hero.webp">`. WebP, fetchpriority, srcset, and preload optimization techniques are documented at the companion `web.dev/articles/optimize-lcp` guide.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Run Google PageSpeed Insights on every landing page. If LCP is > 2.5s, the hero image is the #1 suspect. Check: Is it lazy-loaded? (Remove lazy loading.) Is it too large? (Compress to target sizes.) Is it in JPEG instead of WebP? (Convert.) Is it the wrong dimensions? (Use srcset.) Hero image optimization alone can move sites from "Needs Improvement" to "Good" LCP category, improving both Google ranking and conversion. Target hero image file size: < 200KB desktop, < 100KB mobile.
- **Replication Status**: Google Core Web Vitals is an industry standard, not a single study. The performance-conversion relationship is documented across large-scale Google and industry studies.
- **Boundary Conditions**: LCP depends on the full page, not just the hero image. Font loading, JavaScript blocking, server response time, and CDN can all be LCP bottlenecks. Hero image optimization is the most common but not the only lever.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 13: Mobile Hero Text-Length Budget — Headline and Subheadline Character Limits

- **Source**: Baymard Institute, "Mobile E-Commerce UX" research — https://baymard.com/blog/mobile-ux-ecommerce; NNGroup, mobile content readability research — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mobile-ux/
- **Methodology**: Baymard mobile usability benchmark (4,400+ sessions including mobile rounds). NNGroup mobile content readability research synthesizing usability testing and cognitive load principles.
- **Key Finding**: On 375px-wide mobile screens, hero headlines exceeding 2 lines at 32-40px consume more than 200px of vertical real estate, pushing the CTA below the fold on small devices. Optimal mobile headline: ≤40 characters. Subheadlines: ≤60 characters / 1-2 lines maximum. The cause is vertical real estate consumption on constrained mobile viewports, not a character-count rule per se — the character limits are derived from standard mobile type scales and Baymard's mobile usability findings on above-fold CTA visibility.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When designing mobile-first hero sections: (1) Write hero headlines at ≤40 characters; (2) Constrain subheadlines to ≤60 characters / 2 lines max at standard body size; (3) Test on an actual 375×667 device (iPhone SE) — not desktop preview — to confirm the CTA remains above the fold. A headline like "Stay dry in any storm — GORE-TEX membrane" (46 chars) wraps to 3 lines on small mobile and displaces the CTA; trimming to "Stay dry in any storm" (21 chars) + subheadline for mechanism keeps CTA visible. Cross-reference: Finding 8 (mobile hero failure modes).
- **Replication Status**: Character limits derived from Baymard mobile UX benchmark findings and NNGroup mobile content research. No single A/B test isolates character count as the independent variable; the underlying cause (vertical real estate consumption) is well-grounded in Baymard's mobile usability benchmark methodology.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Character limits shift with type scale — at 28px, more characters fit; at 40px, fewer. The ≤40/≤60 guidance is calibrated for common mobile hero type scales (32-36px headlines, 16-18px subheadlines). Brands targeting tablets or large-screen mobile (428px+ widths) have more flexibility. The rule applies most strictly to landing-page-style hero sections; site-global hero sections with navigation context have different constraints.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (Baymard mobile research + NNGroup mobile content guidance; practitioner-synthesis framing)

### Finding 14: Hero Image Aspect-Ratio and CSS `aspect-ratio` — Preventing Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS)

- **Source**: Google, "Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS)" — https://web.dev/articles/cls (thresholds and causes, WebFetch-verified 2026-04-21); Google, "Optimize Cumulative Layout Shift" — https://web.dev/articles/optimize-cls (remediation including images without dimensions)
- **Methodology**: Google Core Web Vitals program. CLS thresholds: Good ≤0.1, Needs Improvement 0.1–0.25, Poor >0.25. Google's CrUX real-user data identifies images or videos without dimensions as a common source of CLS. The companion `optimize-cls` article documents explicit remediation guidance.
- **Key Finding**: Hero images without explicit `width`, `height`, or CSS `aspect-ratio` attributes cause Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS) — a Core Web Vitals ranking signal. As the browser loads the hero image and discovers its actual dimensions, it reflows the page, causing visible layout jumps. CLS >0.1 is "Needs Improvement"; >0.25 is "Poor." On mobile, a shifting hero causing the CTA to jump in position can trigger mis-taps and interrupt the conversion flow. The fix is mechanical: add explicit `width` and `height` attributes to hero `<img>` elements, or use CSS `aspect-ratio` to reserve the correct space before the image loads.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For every hero image: (1) Add explicit `width` and `height` HTML attributes matching the intrinsic image dimensions; (2) Or use CSS `aspect-ratio: 16 / 9` (or appropriate ratio) on the container; (3) Test CLS score using Chrome DevTools Performance panel or PageSpeed Insights before launch. A CLS score >0.1 on the hero section is almost always fixable in minutes by adding the missing `width`/`height`. This is especially critical for mobile where hero images are served via `srcset` at different aspect ratios for different viewports — each `<source>` in the `<picture>` element needs size declarations. Cross-reference: Finding 12 (LCP optimization — the hero image must be both fast AND stable).
- **Replication Status**: Google Core Web Vitals is an industry standard. CLS thresholds and image-without-dimensions as a CLS cause are documented in Google's official developer guidance (WebFetch-verified). The mis-tap framing is a reasonable practitioner inference from CLS behavior on mobile, not stated explicitly on Google's page.
- **Boundary Conditions**: CLS is one of several Core Web Vitals signals. If LCP (Finding 12) is already the primary performance bottleneck, fixing CLS provides incremental improvement. Images loaded via CSS background (not `<img>`) don't have the same CLS risk as `<img>` without dimensions. Server-side rendered images with known dimensions often avoid this issue automatically.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (Google Core Web Vitals official documentation; CrUX real-user data)

### Limitations
- Hero section research is heavily influenced by website vertical, traffic source, and audience intent. B2C e-commerce findings may not apply to B2B landing pages or SaaS sign-up pages.
- A/B tests on hero sections often change multiple variables simultaneously (image, headline, CTA), making it difficult to isolate the effect of individual elements. Effect attribution to specific findings requires caution.
- Gaze direction research (Finding 6) is primarily from 2009. Replication with modern web layouts and mobile viewports would strengthen this finding.
- Performance research (Finding 12) evolves with browser standards, CDN technology, and device capabilities. Specific file size targets should be re-evaluated annually as mobile hardware and connection speeds improve.
