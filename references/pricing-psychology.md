## Summary

### Top 3 Most Impactful Findings

1. **Finding 3 (Charm Pricing Field Test)**: Anderson & Simester demonstrated that a $39 price outsold both $34 and $44 for the same dress in a mail-order catalog, proving that 9-ending prices can increase demand even at higher absolute prices.
2. **Finding 39 (Compromise Effect)**: Simonson (1989) demonstrates that consumers systematically prefer the middle option in a three-tier set — 45-55% vs. chance expectation of 33% — driven by extremeness aversion. Gold-tier evidence, extensively replicated, and more reliable than the decoy effect. This is the primary mechanism explaining why Good-Better-Best pricing pages work in practice.
3. **Finding 15 (Free Shipping Threshold - NuFace)**: Adding a "$75 free shipping" threshold increased orders by 90% and AOV by 7.32%, demonstrating that free shipping thresholds are one of the highest-leverage conversion tools available.

**Note (first draft):** Finding 39 (Compromise Effect, Gold) replaces original #2 Finding 6 (Ariely's Economist Decoy, Bronze) in the Top 3 due to higher evidence tier and broader reliable replication. Human review should confirm this prioritization.

## Findings

### Finding 1: Anchoring Effects on Price Judgments — Foundational Evidence
- **Source**: Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." *Science*, 185(4157), 1124-1131. https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.185.4157.1124 Also: Ariely, D., Loewenstein, G., & Prelec, D. (2003). "Coherent Arbitrariness: Stable Demand Curves Without Stable Preferences." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 118(1), 73-106. https://academic.oup.com/qje
- **Methodology**: Tversky & Kahneman: participants estimated quantities after exposure to random anchor numbers (wheel of fortune). Ariely et al.: MIT MBA students used last two digits of SSN as anchor before bidding on consumer goods.
- **Key Finding**: Arbitrary numerical anchors systematically bias subsequent value judgments. In Ariely's study, participants with high SSN digits (80-99) bid 216-346% more than those with low digits (01-20) for identical products. The anchor effect persists even when the anchor is transparently irrelevant.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Display higher-priced options first (e.g., premium tier before basic tier). Show original MSRP before sale price. Use "compare at" pricing to set an anchor. The first number a shopper sees becomes the reference point for value assessment.
- **Replication Status**: Extensively replicated. Anchoring is one of the most robust findings in behavioral economics, confirmed across hundreds of studies and meta-analyses (Furnham & Boo, 2011).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is reduced (but not eliminated) when participants have domain expertise. Less effective when consumers have strong prior price knowledge for the specific product. Extreme anchors can trigger suspicion.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 2: Multi-Unit Pricing Framing Increases Purchase Quantity
- **Source**: Manning, K.C. & Sprott, D.E. (2007). "Multiple Unit Price Promotions and Their Effects on Quantity Purchase Intentions." *Journal of Retailing*, 83(4), 411-421. https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-retailing Also supported by broader anchoring literature (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974). https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.185.4157.1124
- **Methodology**: Multiple experimental studies testing multi-unit pricing formats ("3 for $5") vs. per-unit pricing ("$1.67 each") on purchase quantity intentions.
- **Key Finding**: Multi-unit pricing (e.g., "3 for $5") increases purchase quantities compared to equivalent per-unit pricing. The number in the multi-unit offer serves as a quantity anchor. Effect is consistent with broader anchoring research.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Present bundle quantities with a single combined price rather than per-unit pricing. "3 for $29.99" will outperform "$9.99 each" even when the math is identical. The number in the multi-unit offer serves as an implicit purchase quantity anchor.
- **Replication Status**: Supported by multiple studies on quantity anchoring. Consistent with broader anchoring literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Works best for consumable/replenishable goods. Less effective for unique or high-consideration purchases.
- **NOTE**: Earlier versions of this finding cited Wansink, Kent & Hoch (1998), "An Anchoring and Adjustment Model of Purchase Quantity Decisions," *Journal of Marketing Research*, 35(1), 71-81. <https://doi.org/10.1177/002224379803500108> — with specific lift percentages (112%, 32%). Wansink's work has been **discredited** — 18 papers retracted, resigned from Cornell in 2018 for data fabrication. The underlying anchoring principle is well-supported by independent research (Tversky & Kahneman, Ariely, Manning & Sprott), but Wansink's specific numbers should not be cited. DOI is provided for historical reference only.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 3: Charm Pricing ($39 vs. $34) Increases Demand in Mail-Order Catalog
- **Source**: Anderson, E.T. & Simester, D.I. (2003). "Effects of $9 Price Endings on Retail Sales: Evidence from Field Experiments." *Quantitative Marketing and Economics*, 1(1), 93-110. https://link.springer.com/journal/11129 (MIT Sloan / University of Chicago)
- **Methodology**: Controlled field experiment with a national mail-order catalog. Same women's clothing item was randomly assigned prices of $34, $39, or $44 across different catalog versions sent to comparable customer groups.
- **Key Finding**: The $39 price sold 21 units; $34 sold 16 units; $44 sold 17 units. The 9-ending price at $39 outsold the lower price at $34 by approximately 31%. This is not just a left-digit effect -- $39 is absolutely higher than $34, yet it generated more demand.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When setting prices, test 9-ending variants even if they are higher than the current price. The signaling effect of a 9-ending ("this is a deal") can outweigh the higher absolute cost.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated across multiple product categories within the same paper. Consistent with the broader charm pricing literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect was strongest for new items and items not previously purchased by the customer. Diminished for repeat purchases where customers had a reference price. Does NOT work for luxury/prestige goods (see Finding 7).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 4: Left-Digit Effect Mechanism in Price Perception
- **Source**: Thomas, M. & Morwitz, V. (2005). "Penny Wise and Pound Foolish: The Left-Digit Effect in Price Cognition." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 32(1), 54-64. https://academic.oup.com/jcr
- **Methodology**: Five controlled experiments testing how consumers encode multi-digit prices, comparing price pairs where left digits differ ($2.99 vs. $3.00) vs. where they don't ($3.59 vs. $3.60).
- **Key Finding**: The left-digit effect occurs *only* when the leftmost digits differ (e.g., $2.99 vs. $3.00). The encoded magnitude of $2.99 is anchored on "2" and perceived as significantly less than $3.00, but $3.59 vs. $3.60 shows no comparable effect. The closer two competing prices are numerically, the stronger the left-digit effect becomes.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Charm pricing is most powerful when it changes the left digit ($3.00 to $2.99, $50 to $49.99). A price of $3.79 vs. $3.80 will show minimal effect because the left digit doesn't change. Prioritize left-digit reduction over simply ending in .99.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated. One of the most-cited papers in pricing psychology.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is moderated by involvement level and numerical processing style. Consumers who engage in deliberate calculation are less susceptible. Effect diminishes when prices are presented side-by-side (easier comparison).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 5: Decoy Effect Replication Challenges
- **Source**: Comprehensive meta-review cited in multiple sources (Frederick, S., Lee, L., & Baskin, E., 2014, and subsequent reviews). Also: Wikipedia's synthesis of Huber, Payne, & Puto (1982) original and subsequent replication attempts. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoy_effect and <https://doi.org/10.1086/208899>
- **Methodology**: Review of 91 replication attempts of the attraction/decoy effect across 23 product categories.
- **Key Finding**: Of 91 attempts to reproduce the attraction effect, only 11 produced reliable results. The effect is real but far more context-dependent than popularly presented. It works most consistently in controlled lab settings with simple choice sets.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Do not assume the decoy effect will automatically work in your pricing page. Test rigorously. The effect is strongest when: (a) options are easy to compare on 2 clear dimensions, (b) the decoy is clearly dominated on all dimensions, (c) choice sets are small (3 options).
- **Replication Status**: Partially replicated. Robust in specific conditions, unreliable in others.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Fails when consumers have strong prior preferences, when choice sets are complex (>4 options), or when the decoy is not clearly dominated. Real-world noise reduces effect size compared to lab settings.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Quality Flag**: Meta-review of 91 replication attempts across 23 categories; methodology is review-grade despite Bronze primary source attribution

### Finding 6: Ariely's Economist Decoy Experiment
- **Source**: Ariely, D. (2008). *Predictably Irrational*. Chapter 1. Experiment conducted at MIT with student participants. https://www.harpercollins.com/products/predictably-irrational-dan-ariely
- **Methodology**: 100 MIT students chose between Economist subscription options. Condition A: Web-only ($59), Print-only ($125), Print+Web ($125). Condition B: Web-only ($59), Print+Web ($125). The print-only option at $125 serves as the decoy (dominated by print+web at the same price).
- **Key Finding**: With decoy present: 16% chose web-only, 0% chose print-only, 84% chose print+web. Without decoy: 68% chose web-only, 32% chose print+web. The decoy shifted preference for the target option from 32% to 84% -- a 163% relative increase.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When designing pricing tiers (especially SaaS/subscription), include a "decoy" tier that is clearly dominated by the target tier. The decoy should match the target on price but offer strictly less value. This makes the target option feel like an obvious bargain.
- **Replication Status**: Classroom demonstration, not a formal peer-reviewed experiment. Directionally consistent with Huber et al. (1982) lab findings. See Finding 5 for replication caveats.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Student sample, hypothetical choice (no real purchase). Effect size in real purchasing contexts is likely smaller. The decoy must be obviously dominated -- subtle differences may not trigger the effect.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Quality Flag**: Classroom demonstration from a popular book, not a peer-reviewed experiment; treat as illustrative, not definitive

### Finding 7: Charm Pricing Backfires for Luxury and Premium Brands
- **Source**: Ortega, A.M., & Tabares, F.A. (2023). "Psychological pricing: Myth or reality?" *Journal of Retailing and Consumer Services*, 71, 103206. <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jretconser.2022.103206> (SSCI peer-reviewed, Elsevier). Also consistent with Stiving, M. & Winer, R.S. (1997). "An Empirical Analysis of Price Endings with Scanner Data." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 24(1), 57-67. <https://doi.org/10.1086/209493> and broader prestige pricing literature.
- **Methodology**: Ortega & Tabares (2023): Peer-reviewed empirical study in the *Journal of Retailing and Consumer Services* examining psychological pricing mechanisms including charm pricing backfire effects for premium brands. Stiving & Winer (1997): Scanner data analysis of price endings across product categories.
- **Key Finding**: For brands associated with high quality or luxury (e.g., Apple, premium fashion), charm pricing (e.g., $999.99 vs. $1,000) reduces perceived quality. Consumers' brains treat charm pricing as a "cheap trick." Luxury brands that use 99-endings may inadvertently cheapen their brand image.
- **E-Commerce Application**: If selling premium or luxury products, use round prices ($200, $1,000, $50). Reserve charm pricing for value-oriented products, everyday purchases, and impulse buys. The decision between charm and round pricing should be driven by brand positioning, not applied uniformly.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across multiple studies in the prestige pricing literature. Ortega & Tabares (2023) provides peer-reviewed SSCI confirmation.
- **Boundary Conditions**: This is specifically about brand-quality perception. For unknown/new brands without established premium positioning, charm pricing may still increase conversion. The boundary is brand perception, not product price level.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: Evidence tier upgraded Bronze → Silver: Ortega & Tabares (2023) is an SSCI-indexed peer-reviewed paper in *Journal of Retailing and Consumer Services* (Elsevier). The previous source (newneuromarketing.com) has been replaced with the primary academic source.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver

### Finding 8: Loss Aversion Ratio (Prospect Theory Foundation)
- **Source**: Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." *Econometrica*, 47(2), 263-292. <https://doi.org/10.2307/1914185> Refined in: Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1992). "Advances in Prospect Theory: Cumulative Representation of Uncertainty." *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*, 5, 297-323. <https://doi.org/10.1007/BF00122574>
- **Methodology**: Series of choice experiments presenting participants with gambles involving gains and losses of varying magnitudes. The 1992 paper estimated the loss aversion coefficient empirically.
- **Key Finding**: Losses are felt approximately 1.5x to 2.5x more intensely than equivalent gains. The commonly cited ratio is approximately 2:1 (often referenced as 2.25:1 from the 1992 paper). This means a $10 loss creates roughly the same emotional magnitude as a $20-$25 gain.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Frame promotions as "avoiding a loss" rather than "gaining a benefit." "Don't miss out on $20 savings" is psychologically stronger than "Save $20." Countdown timers, expiring discounts, and "last chance" messaging leverage this asymmetry directly.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated extensively. One of the most robust findings in behavioral economics, though exact ratio varies by context (1.5x to 2.5x range).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Loss aversion diminishes for very small amounts (trivial losses don't trigger the effect) and in domains where the person has significant expertise/experience. Some recent papers have questioned the universality of the 2:1 ratio.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 9: Countdown Timers and Scarcity Framing Conversion Lifts
- **Source**: Multiple practitioner A/B tests. Obvi (DTC supplement brand) A/B test, confirmed at OptiMonk case study. https://www.optimonk.com/a-b-testing-ideas/ Compiled by various CRO platforms.
- **Methodology**: Obvi: A/B test on bundle promotion page with vs. without countdown timer. Practitioner case studies across multiple DTC platforms.
- **Key Finding**: Obvi saw approximately 7.97% increase in conversions with the countdown timer variant (confirmed at OptiMonk). The directional finding — countdown timers on genuine time-limited offers increase conversion — is consistent across practitioner tests, though specific effect sizes vary by context.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Add countdown timers to time-limited promotions, flash sales, and cart pages. Combine with specific end times ("Offer ends in 2h 14m") rather than vague urgency ("Limited time!"). The timer creates a concrete loss frame — the deal disappearing.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across multiple practitioner tests. Exact effect sizes vary widely by context. No peer-reviewed RCT.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Overuse causes "urgency fatigue" — if every page has a countdown timer, customers learn to ignore them. Fake urgency (timers that reset) destroys trust. Most effective for genuinely time-limited offers.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: Bose 24-hour sale statistics (34% CTR lift, 22% conversion lift) removed — source not locatable by either audit run. Obvi figure corrected to 7.97% (content-matched at OptiMonk); the 13.7% revenue per user figure did not content-match and has been removed.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 10: Mere Touch Increases Perceived Ownership and Valuation
- **Source**: Peck, J. & Shu, S.B. (2009). "The Effect of Mere Touch on Perceived Ownership." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 36(3), 434-447. https://academic.oup.com/jcr
- **Methodology**: Multiple lab experiments testing whether physical contact with an object increases perceived ownership and willingness-to-pay. Controlled for prior interest and product type.
- **Key Finding**: Merely touching an object significantly increases perceived ownership and, consequently, the amount participants were willing to pay for it. The effect is mediated by perceived ownership -- touch creates a sense of "this is mine" that increases valuation.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For online stores where physical touch is impossible, use "ownership imagery" -- language and visuals that encourage customers to imagine the product in their possession ("Imagine this on your desk," "Picture yourself wearing this"). Product customization tools, AR try-on features, and 360-degree views serve as touch surrogates.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated. Extended by subsequent research on haptic imagery and touchscreen interfaces.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is stronger for individuals with high "need for touch." Online context inherently lacks physical touch, so surrogates (imagery, interactivity) have smaller effect sizes than actual touch.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 11: Endowment Effect -- WTA/WTP Ratio of 2:1
- **Source**: Kahneman, D., Knetsch, J.L., & Thaler, R.H. (1990). "Experimental Tests of the Endowment Effect and the Coase Theorem." *Journal of Political Economy*, 98(6), 1325-1348. https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/journals/jpe
- **Methodology**: Cornell University undergraduates randomly assigned as "sellers" (given a coffee mug) or "buyers" (not given a mug). Willingness-to-accept (WTA) and willingness-to-pay (WTP) were elicited in market-like conditions with real transactions.
- **Key Finding**: Median selling prices (WTA) were approximately 2x median buying prices (WTP) for identical mugs. Sellers demanded roughly twice what buyers were willing to pay, purely as a function of ownership status. The effect persisted across multiple rounds, showing no learning/extinction.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Once a customer adds an item to their cart, they begin to psychologically "own" it. Cart abandonment emails should frame the items as something the customer *already has* that they're about to lose ("Your items are waiting" / "Don't lose what you picked out"), leveraging the endowment effect + loss aversion. Wishlists, saved carts, and "reserved for you" messaging all amplify this.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated extensively. One of the foundational findings in behavioral economics.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is reduced when participants are experienced traders, when goods are purchased for resale (not personal use), and for goods the person doesn't value. Some recent meta-analyses suggest the ratio may be closer to 1.5:1 in real-world markets.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 12: Rule of 100 -- Percentage vs. Dollar Discount Framing
- **Source**: Berger, J. (2013). *Contagious: Why Things Catch On*. Simon & Schuster. Originally presented in Berger's marketing research and teaching. https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Contagious/Jonah-Berger/9781451686586
- **Methodology**: Conceptual framework based on perceived magnitude of discounts, supported by consumer behavior observations and classroom demonstrations.
- **Key Finding**: For products priced under $100, percentage discounts appear larger (20% off a $50 item = $10, but "20% off" sounds bigger than "$10 off"). For products over $100, absolute dollar discounts appear larger ($500 off a $2,000 laptop sounds bigger than "25% off," even though they're identical). The $100 threshold is the crossover point.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Dynamically display discount format based on product price. Under $100: show "Save 25%." Over $100: show "Save $50." This simple conditional formatting maximizes the perceived size of every discount without changing the actual discount amount.
- **Replication Status**: Not formally tested in a controlled experiment as published. Widely adopted as a practitioner heuristic. Directionally consistent with numerical cognition research.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The $100 threshold is a heuristic, not a precise crossover point. The actual crossover depends on the specific discount percentage and absolute amount. For amounts near $100, test both formats.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 13: Dollar-Off Coupons Can Generate 170% More Revenue Than Percentage-Off
- **Source**: MarketingSherpa case study. A/B test comparing coupon types for Evo (outdoor/ski retailer). https://www.marketingsherpa.com/
- **Methodology**: A/B test comparing a $50-off coupon vs. a 15%-off coupon (not equivalent discounts -- the $50 off was more generous for most order values).
- **Key Finding**: The $50-off coupon generated 170% more revenue and had a 72% higher conversion rate than the 15%-off coupon. Note: these are not equivalent discounts, so the result conflates discount generosity with framing format.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When offering coupons, the perceived concreteness of a dollar amount ("$50 off") may feel more tangible and actionable than a percentage ("15% off"), especially when the dollar amount is generous. However, always ensure you're comparing equivalent discount values when testing format.
- **Replication Status**: Single case study. The confound (non-equivalent discount amounts) limits generalizability of the framing effect specifically.
- **Boundary Conditions**: This specific result is confounded by different discount magnitudes. The 170% figure should NOT be cited as evidence that dollar-off framing is inherently superior -- only that $50 off outperformed 15% off in this specific context (which is unsurprising given the different actual values).
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze
- **Quality Flag**: Single confounded case study; discount amounts were not equivalent, so the framing effect cannot be isolated from discount generosity

### Finding 14: Bundle Pricing Consistently Increases Revenue — Specific Magnitudes Unverifiable
- **Source**: McKinsey & Company case study (online retailer, cross-aisle marketing +30%): https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/growth-marketing-and-sales/how-we-help-clients/clm-online-retailer. Deloitte personalization research (frequently misattributed to bundling specifically — actual Deloitte findings cover personalization broadly: 48% more likely to exceed revenue goals, 71% improved loyalty). Derdenger, T. & Kumar, V. "The Dynamic Effects of Bundling as a Product Strategy." Harvard Business School working paper (2013): https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/download.aspx?name=dynamicbundling_2013_06_20.pdf. Vendor secondary sources: Swell (https://www.swell.is/content/physical-product-bundling-statistics), Monetizely (https://www.getmonetizely.com/articles/how-does-price-bundling-affect-customer-psychology-and-revenue-optimization).
- **Methodology**: McKinsey: single-client case study (not generalizable). Deloitte: personalization maturity survey (not bundling-specific — the "40% retention / 22% transaction value" figures widely cited in vendor blogs do not appear in Deloitte's published reports, which use different metrics). Derdenger & Kumar: formal economic model with empirical analysis of the handheld video game market — rigorous but domain-specific.
- **Key Finding**: Bundling consistently increases revenue across all sources that examine it. Mixed bundling (buy individually or as bundle) dominates pure bundling and unbundled components (Derdenger & Kumar, confirmed empirically). However, the specific percentage ranges previously cited (10–30% McKinsey, 40%/22% Deloitte, 25–35% HBR) are either single-client case results, vendor blog mutations of broader personalization research, or secondary citation simplifications of dense academic work. The direction is robust; the specific magnitudes should not be cited numerically.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Offer bundles as an option alongside individual products (mixed bundling). Show the individual prices summed up next to the bundle price to make savings salient. Personalize bundle recommendations based on browsing/purchase history for maximum impact.
- **Replication Status**: The mechanism (bundling increases revenue via valuation homogenization and dynamic consumer segmentation) is confirmed by Derdenger & Kumar and separately by Gold-tier Findings 27–30 (Adams & Yellen, Stremersch, Janiszewski & Cunha). The specific consulting-report percentages are not independently replicable.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Bundling can reduce revenue if the products bundled have very different willingness-to-pay distributions (forcing a single price on heterogeneous valuations). Too many bundle options create choice overload (can reduce conversion by up to 10%).
- **Citation Status**: McKinsey source is a single-client case study. Deloitte "40%/22%" bundling figures are a vendor blog telephone game — actual Deloitte personalization reports use different metrics (48%/71%). The Derdenger & Kumar HBS paper is genuine academic work confirming mixed bundling dominance but does not contain the "25–35%" figure cited in secondary sources. Human-verified 2026-04-22.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze — the specific consulting-report percentages are misattributed, mutated through secondary citations, or non-generalizable case studies. The bundling *mechanism* is Gold-supported by Findings 27–30 and the Derdenger & Kumar paper.

### Finding 15: Free Shipping Threshold Increases Orders 90% (NuFace Case Study)
- **Source**: Red Door Interactive / VWO. NuFace A/B test case study. https://www.reddoor.biz/work/projects/nuface-finding-the-right-incentive-for-customers-to-convert/
- **Methodology**: A/B test on NuFace (anti-aging skincare) e-commerce site. Control: original site. Variant: "Free shipping over $75!" messaging added prominently above the Shop button. Traffic split 50/50.
- **Key Finding**: Orders increased by 90% (96% statistical confidence). Average order value increased by 7.32%. The $75 threshold was set above the existing AOV to encourage basket-building behavior.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Set free shipping thresholds 15-25% above current AOV. Place the messaging prominently and early in the shopping flow (global header, product pages) -- not buried in checkout. Use dynamic progress bars showing how close the customer is to qualifying.
- **Replication Status**: Single case study, but directionally consistent with broad e-commerce shipping research. Specific 90% lift is unusually high and may reflect the fact that NuFace had previously hidden their free shipping offer.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The 90% lift was partly because free shipping was already offered but not visible -- the test was as much about *communicating* the offer as *adding* it. Results will be smaller for sites that already prominently display shipping information. Threshold set too high creates friction and reduces conversion.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 16: Free Shipping Threshold Optimization Data
- **Source**: Multiple industry sources: FedEx consumer survey data (2025) https://www.fedex.com/, Cartly Pro analysis https://cartly-pro.com/, Intelligems merchant data https://www.intelligems.io/
- **Methodology**: Survey data and cross-merchant analysis of shipping threshold performance.
- **Key Finding**: 81% of shoppers are willing to spend more to qualify for free shipping (FedEx 2024 survey). A substantial share of shoppers add items to their cart specifically to reach a free shipping threshold. Dynamic progress indicators ("You're $12 away from free shipping!") increase threshold conversion by 15–25% compared to static messaging. Most e-commerce thresholds fall between $35–$100. Best implementations see 15–30% AOV increases.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: FedEx 2024 survey page-anchors 81% as the primary figure; the previously cited "58% add items" figure did not content-match to the cited FedEx survey URL and has been reframed as a directional practitioner range. The ~$64 industry average and 5–10% conversion decrease figures are practitioner-synthesis ranges not page-anchored to cited URLs and have been removed as specific numbers.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Implement a dynamic free shipping progress bar in the cart. Link it to recommended add-on products that would push the order over the threshold. A/B test the threshold amount -- too high reduces conversion; too low leaves AOV gains on the table.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across multiple merchant datasets and surveys.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effectiveness varies by product category and baseline AOV. Categories with low-cost add-ons (accessories, consumables) benefit most. Threshold is less effective for single-SKU stores or very high-ticket items.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 17: "Pennies-a-Day" Temporal Reframing Increases Compliance
- **Source**: Gourville, J.T. (1998). "Pennies-a-Day: The Effect of Temporal Reframing on Transaction Evaluation." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 24(4), 395-408. https://academic.oup.com/jcr
- **Methodology**: Series of laboratory studies testing how reframing an aggregate cost into a smaller per-day/per-week equivalent changes purchase likelihood. Tested across multiple product categories.
- **Key Finding**: Reframing a large one-time cost as a small daily cost ("less than $1/day" instead of "$350/year") systematically increases transaction compliance. The mechanism: daily framing causes consumers to retrieve small everyday expenses (a cup of coffee) as the comparison set, making the product seem trivially cheap. Aggregate framing triggers comparison to large purchases (a TV, a vacation), making the same product seem expensive.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For subscriptions and high-ticket items, always show the per-day or per-month equivalent alongside the total price. "$2.49/day" feels trivial even though it's $75/month or $900/year. SaaS pricing pages that show "per day" equivalents leverage this directly.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated across product categories within the original paper. Consistent with mental accounting theory (Thaler, 1985).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Most effective when the per-day amount can be compared to a trivially small daily expense. Less effective when the per-day amount is still high (e.g., "$15/day" doesn't feel trivial). The actual payment structure must still be clearly disclosed -- deceptive framing erodes trust.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 18: Subscription Commitment Anxiety and 70% Forgotten Subscriptions
- **Source**: ResearchGate publication: "Unsubscribe From Anxiety: The Psychological Costs of Subscription Service Overload" (2024). https://www.researchgate.net/publication/381547600 Multiple consumer surveys on subscription fatigue.

- **Boundary Conditions**: Subscription fatigue is primarily a concern in markets with many competing subscriptions (media, SaaS, meal kits). For unique/essential services with few alternatives, commitment anxiety is lower.

### Finding 19: Price-Quality Inference -- Higher Prices Increase Quality Expectations but Not Post-Experience Ratings
- **Source**: Kurz, P. et al. (2023). "Pricey therefore good? Price affects expectations, but not quality perceptions and liking." *Psychology & Marketing* (Wiley). https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/15206793 Also: Scitovszky (1944-45) foundational work on price-quality heuristics.
- **Methodology**: Experimental studies measuring both pre-consumption expectations and post-consumption quality ratings for products at different price points.
- **Key Finding**: Higher prices led to higher quality and liking *expectations* as predicted by the price-quality heuristic. However, after actually experiencing the product, there was no consistent effect of price on perceived quality or liking. The price-quality inference operates primarily at the pre-purchase evaluation stage.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Higher prices can increase *pre-purchase* conversion for products that cannot be easily evaluated before buying (experience goods, new products, unfamiliar brands). But post-purchase satisfaction depends on actual quality. Pricing above market for a mediocre product will increase returns and negative reviews. Price-quality inference is most useful for products where quality is genuinely ambiguous before purchase.
- **Replication Status**: The specific finding (expectations yes, experience no) is from a 2023 study. The broader price-quality heuristic has mixed replication results -- it's stronger in some categories than others.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Price-quality inference is strongest when: (a) consumers have limited product knowledge, (b) there is high variance in actual quality across brands, (c) other quality cues (reviews, brand reputation) are absent. It is weakest for familiar categories where consumers have strong prior preferences.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 20: Price-Quality Inference Increases With Brand Differentiation
- **Source**: Verma, D.P.S. & Sen Gupta, S. (2004). "Does Higher Price Signal Better Quality?" *Vikalpa: The Journal for Decision Makers*, 29(2). https://journals.sagepub.com/home/vik Also: Simon-Kucher & Partners (consulting research on price-quality relationships). https://www.simon-kucher.com/
- **Methodology**: Consumer perception studies examining when higher prices successfully signal quality across different product categories and brand environments.

In categories where brands are perceived as interchangeable, price has less influence on quality perception. Marketers who invest in brand differentiation amplify the effectiveness of premium pricing.
- **E-Commerce Application**: If selling premium products, invest in differentiating your brand (unique design, exclusive materials, superior customer service) before raising prices. Price increases without differentiation will not trigger the price-quality inference effectively. Show comparison tables, unique feature callouts, and quality certifications alongside premium prices.
- **Replication Status**: Consistent across the price-quality literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: In commodity categories with low perceived brand variance, higher prices simply reduce demand without quality inference benefits. The effect also weakens when abundant reviews and ratings provide direct quality signals, reducing reliance on price as a proxy.
- **Evidence Tier**: Bronze

### Finding 21: Meta-Analytic Evidence for Just-Below Pricing Effects
- **Source**: Troll, E.S., & Loschelder, D.D. (2024). "A meta‐analysis on the effects of just‐below versus round prices." *Journal of Consumer Psychology*, 34(2), 299–325. <https://doi.org/10.1002/jcpy.1353>
- **Methodology**: Pre-registered meta-analysis of **362** effect sizes drawn from **69** published articles (N = 40,541). Examined purchase intentions, perceived quality, and price underestimation as outcome variables. Effect sizes were synthesized using random-effects models; moderator analyses tested boundary conditions including product type, price level, left-digit change, and consumer processing style.
- **Key Finding**: Just-below (vs. round) prices produce a small but statistically reliable positive effect on purchase intentions (g = 0.13, 95% CI [0.01, 0.25] for purchase decisions). Critically, just-below prices have **no statistically significant effect on perceived quality** (g = 0.00, 95% CI [−0.17, 0.18], p = 0.96) — the common practitioner concern that .99 endings "cheapen" non-luxury products is not supported by the aggregate evidence. Effect is strongest when the left digit changes (consistent with Finding 4). Effect does not appear to degrade for non-luxury goods.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For value and mid-market products, .99 endings are safe — they lift purchase intentions without hurting quality perception. Reserve round pricing for genuinely luxury/prestige segments (see Finding 7). The effect size is small (g = 0.13), so don't expect double-digit conversion lifts from price endings alone. Charm pricing is a low-cost micro-optimization, not a growth lever.
- **Replication Status**: This IS the replication synthesis. 362 effect sizes across 69 studies (N = 40,541) is the highest-quality evidence available in this domain. Pre-registration reduces publication bias concerns. Supersedes single-study findings.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is moderated by left-digit change (strongest when left digit differs), consumer numerical literacy, and processing mode (deliberate calculators are less affected). Does not apply to luxury/prestige goods (see Finding 7). Effect size is small — not a replacement for price strategy, just an optimization.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: Author corrected: Troll & **Loschelder** (not Wieseke); co-author verified via Leuphana University preprint and PsycNet. Effect size counts corrected: 362 effect sizes from 69 studies (N = 40,541); g = 0.13 [0.01, 0.25] for purchase decisions; g = 0.00 for perceived quality (null result, p = 0.96).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (pre-registered meta-analysis in peer-reviewed journal)

### Finding 22: Round Number Fluency for Hedonic and Emotional Purchases
- **Source**: Wadhwa, M., & Zhang, K. (2015). "This Number Just Feels Right: The Impact of Roundedness of Price Numbers on Product Evaluations." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 41(5), 1172–1185. <https://doi.org/10.1086/678484>
- **Methodology**: Five laboratory studies using between-subjects designs. Participants evaluated products (hedonic: perfume, flowers; utilitarian: calculator, USB drive) at round vs. non-round prices. Studies manipulated processing mode, product type, and purchase context. Sample sizes ranged from 60–200 per study. Studies 4 and 5 tested purchase decisions with real monetary incentives.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: Study count corrected to five (per JCR abstract confirmation); "Six" in the prior version was incorrect.
- **Key Finding**: Round prices (e.g., $100) are processed with greater *cognitive fluency* for hedonic (emotional, pleasure-oriented) products, leading to higher product evaluations and purchase intent relative to non-round prices. The mechanism is fluency: round prices "feel right" for emotional purchases because they are processed without cognitive friction. For utilitarian products, the advantage disappears — charm pricing performs equivalently to round pricing on utilitarian goods. The effect holds for gift purchases (hedonic by nature).
- **E-Commerce Application**: Segment product catalog by hedonic vs. utilitarian nature. Jewelry, perfume, home décor, candles, art, gift items → use round prices ($50, $100, $200). Office supplies, electronics accessories, tools, cleaning products → .99 endings are fine or neutral. This extends beyond luxury goods (Finding 7) to ALL hedonic product categories, including mid-market emotional purchases. A $30 candle benefits from round pricing even though it's not "luxury."
- **Replication Status**: Replicated internally across 6 studies within the paper. Conceptually consistent with price-quality literature and fluency research. No independent direct replication found as of 2026, though the fluency mechanism is broadly replicated in adjacent literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires hedonic/emotional product context. Utilitarian purchases show no round-number advantage. Effect does not apply when consumers are in explicit calculation mode. Tested at moderate price points ($50–$500); generalizability to very low (<$10) or very high (>$2,000) prices is untested.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JCR, 6 internal studies, real-incentive conditions)

### Finding 23: Industry Prevalence Baseline — Round Prices as Differentiation Signal
- **Source**: Schindler, R.M., & Kirby, P.N. (1997). "Patterns of Rightmost Digits Used in Advertised Prices: Implications for Nine-Ending Effects." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 24(2), 192–201. <https://doi.org/10.1086/209504>. Supplementary: Rutgers Business School research by Schindler.
- **Methodology**: Content analysis of advertised prices in retail catalogs and newspaper advertisements. Analyzed rightmost digit distributions across thousands of retail price points. Documented disproportionate use of 9-ending prices relative to chance expectations.
- **Key Finding**: The majority of US retail advertised prices end in 9; practitioner summaries commonly cite approximately ~60%, though the exact share varies by category and this is not a single figure reported in Schindler & Kirby (1997) itself. In mass-market retail contexts, 9-endings are the statistical norm. This creates an asymmetric signaling environment: in categories saturated with .99 endings, round prices function as a differentiation signal of quality and intentionality. The signal value of round pricing increases when competitors uniformly use charm pricing.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Know your competitive pricing landscape before choosing endings. If your category is saturated with .99 prices (consumer electronics accessories, apparel discounters, mass-market beauty), a round price can stand out as a premium signal. If your category uses mostly round prices (fine jewelry, premium skincare, B2B software), charm pricing will feel out of place. Use competitor pricing analysis as input to price format decisions, not just price level decisions.
- **Replication Status**: Prevalence data is observational and well-established. The signaling implication is a practitioner inference, not directly tested in a controlled study. Consistent with differentiation theory in marketing.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Differentiation signal only works if the category context is saturated with .99 endings. Online price comparison tools reduce the ability to signal through format when prices are listed side-by-side.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (peer-reviewed academic study for the 1997 paper; signaling inference is practitioner-grade)

### Finding 24: Comparative Price Advertising — Acquisition Value vs. Transaction Value
- **Source**: Della Bitta, A.J., Monroe, K.B., & McGinnis, J.M. (1981). "Consumer Perceptions of Comparative Price Advertisements." *Journal of Marketing Research*, 18(4), 416–427. Extended by: Grewal, D., Monroe, K.B., & Krishnan, R. (1998). "The Effects of Price-Comparison Advertising on Buyers' Perceptions of Acquisition Value, Transaction Value, and Behavioral Intentions." *Journal of Marketing*, 62(2), 46–59. <https://doi.org/10.1177/002224299806200204>
- **Methodology**: Della Bitta et al.: Laboratory experiment with consumer panels evaluating comparative price ads (N ≈ 200). Grewal et al.: Extended theoretical framework with empirical tests across multiple product categories; measured acquisition value (overall worth) and transaction value (deal quality) as independent constructs.
- **Key Finding**: Comparative price ads (e.g., "Was $100, Now $75") work through two independent psychological channels: (1) **Acquisition value** — the product's perceived overall worth relative to needs, increased by showing a lower sale price; (2) **Transaction value** — the perceived quality of the deal itself, driven by the magnitude of savings. Both must be positive for maximum purchase intent. Strikethrough pricing without a credible reference price delivers acquisition value but undermines transaction value. A huge savings claim on a mediocre product boosts transaction value but not acquisition value.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Design product pages to communicate both value types. For acquisition value: show the sale price prominently with strong product imagery and benefit copy. For transaction value: make the savings magnitude unmissable — "You save $25 (33%)" as a separate, visually distinct element. The strikethrough alone is not enough; the savings amount should be stated explicitly in dollars AND percentage.
- **Replication Status**: Grewal et al. (1998) is among the most-cited papers in reference price research (1,500+ Google Scholar citations). The two-value framework is widely adopted in pricing literature. Multiple replications across product categories.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Transaction value effect requires the reference price to be *credible*. Inflated MSRPs that consumers recognize as artificial reduce transaction value. Both effects diminish when consumers have strong prior price knowledge.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 25: Advertised Reference Price Effects on Internal Reference Price Formation
- **Source**: Grewal, D., Monroe, K.B., & Krishnan, R. (1998). *Journal of Marketing*, 62(2), 46–59. (Additional findings from the same study as Finding 24.) <https://doi.org/10.1177/002224299806200204>
- **Methodology**: Structural equation modeling of the relationships between advertised selling price, advertised reference price, internal reference price, quality perception, and purchase intentions. Multiple studies across electronics and apparel product categories.
- **Key Finding**: The *advertised reference price* (e.g., "MSRP $200") directly elevates consumers' *internal reference price* — the price they personally consider "normal" for the product — even before they calculate savings. This elevated internal reference price then independently increases perceived quality AND perceived transaction value. The mechanism: high advertised reference → higher internal reference → product seems more valuable → deal seems better. The reference price does three things at once: anchors, signals quality, and increases deal attractiveness.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Set and display reference prices (MSRP, compare-at, "regular price") prominently — visually above and stylistically distinct from the sale price. Use MSRP when available; where there is no manufacturer MSRP, use a documented "regular price" based on actual prior pricing (FTC compliance requirement). The reference price should be the first number the customer sees on the page, establishing the baseline before the sale price is revealed.
- **Replication Status**: Highly replicated. The internal reference price construct has been studied extensively since Winer (1986). Grewal et al.'s specific modeling is widely cited and extended by dozens of follow-on studies.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires credible reference prices. FTC guidelines (16 CFR Part 233) require that reference prices reflect actual prior sales at that price. Inflated reference prices destroy the effect and create legal liability. Effect weakens when customers have strong external price knowledge.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold

### Finding 26: Multiple Reference Price Cues and Internal Reference Price Formation
- **Source**: Monroe, K.B., & Lee, A.Y. (1999). "Remembering Versus Knowing: Issues in Buyers' Processing of Price Information." *Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science*, 27(2), 207–225. <https://doi.org/10.1177/0092070399272006>. Also: Mazumdar, T., Raj, S.P., & Sinha, I. (2005). "Reference Price Research: Review and Propositions." *Journal of Marketing*, 69(4), 84–102. <https://doi.org/10.1509/jmkg.2005.69.4.84>
- **Methodology**: Monroe & Lee: Conceptual paper synthesizing memory and price processing research. Mazumdar et al.: Systematic review of 50+ reference price papers, developing a typology of reference price types and their antecedents.
- **Key Finding**: Consumers form internal reference prices from multiple simultaneous sources: (1) **Temporal reference prices** — remembered past prices for the same product; (2) **Contextual reference prices** — prices of competing products seen during the same session; (3) **Social/normative reference prices** — what they believe is a "fair" price based on general market knowledge. When an advertised reference price conflicts strongly with any of these internal anchors, the advertised price loses credibility. The effective anchor is determined by whichever is most accessible in memory at the point of purchase decision.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Anchor strategy must account for the customer's pre-existing price knowledge. For categories where customers research heavily (electronics, appliances), the advertised reference price must be defensible against Google searches — because the customer will check. For impulse or niche products where price knowledge is low, advertised reference prices have more power. In high-consideration categories, complement strikethrough pricing with trusted third-party price comparisons ("Seen elsewhere for $X — our price: $Y").
- **Replication Status**: The multi-source reference price model is foundational in pricing literature. Mazumdar et al. (2005) is a landmark review with 1,200+ citations. Well-established framework.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Internal reference prices are more resistant to anchoring when the category is price-transparent. Online shopping has increased price transparency significantly since the original reference price research — effect sizes for advertised reference prices in commodity categories are likely smaller today than in 1980s–90s studies.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (both primary sources are peer-reviewed, well-cited)

### Finding 27: The Economics of Bundle Pricing — Consumer Surplus Extraction
- **Source**: Adams, W.J., & Yellen, J.L. (1976). "Commodity Bundling and the Burden of Monopoly." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 90(3), 475–498. <https://doi.org/10.2307/1886045>. Extended by: Schmalensee, R. (1984). "Gaussian Demand and Commodity Bundling." *Journal of Business*, 57(1), S211–S230. <https://doi.org/10.1086/296229>
- **Methodology**: Adams & Yellen: Theoretical economics analysis using utility functions and reservation price distributions to show conditions under which bundling outperforms separate selling. Schmalensee: Extended the framework to show that bundling becomes most profitable when customers have *negatively correlated* valuations across items.
- **Key Finding**: Bundling is profitable when customers have heterogeneous willingness-to-pay for individual items AND when the bundle can serve as a "price fence." Schmalensee's critical insight: bundling is most valuable when different customers value different items highly — the customer who values A but not B, and the customer who values B but not A, both end up buying the bundle when the bundle price is set optimally. Negatively correlated item valuations = ideal bundling conditions. Positively correlated item valuations = bundling adds little.
- **E-Commerce Application**: The best candidates for bundling are products where different customer segments value them differently. Classic examples: camera + camera bag (photographers value camera highly, casual buyers value bag — values are negatively correlated). Avoid bundling items that ALL customers value equally — there's no surplus to extract. Use customer segment analysis to identify which product combinations have diverse valuation distributions.
- **Replication Status**: Foundational economic theory with 1,000+ citations. Extended by numerous economists and marketing researchers. The conditions for profitable bundling are well-established in theory; empirical validation in specific retail contexts varies.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Model assumes market power sufficient to set bundle prices. In highly competitive markets with easy unbundling alternatives, bundle pricing power diminishes. Digital goods are strong bundling candidates because marginal cost of adding items is near zero and customers can't easily source substitutes.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (foundational peer-reviewed economics; extensively cited and built upon)

### Finding 28: Price Bundling vs. Product Bundling — Strategic Distinction
- **Source**: Stremersch, S., & Tellis, G.J. (2002). "Strategic Bundling of Products and Prices: A New Synthesis for Marketing." *Journal of Marketing*, 66(1), 55–72. <https://doi.org/10.1509/jmkg.66.1.55.18455>
- **Methodology**: Conceptual synthesis paper reviewing 40+ years of bundling research across economics, operations research, and marketing. Developed a typology of bundling forms with empirical examples from multiple industries. Framework paper synthesizing the existing literature into actionable strategic guidance.
- **Key Finding**: Two fundamentally different strategies: (1) **Price bundling** — selling two or more separate products at a combined discount ("buy both for $79, save $21"); (2) **Product bundling** — integrating products so they become a new combined entity (a "starter kit" that functions as a unit). Price bundling is primarily a pricing tool; product bundling is a product strategy. Mixed bundling (offering the bundle AND the items individually) outperforms pure bundling for most e-commerce contexts because it captures both bundle-preferring and single-item-preferring customers.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Most e-commerce "bundles" are price bundles — discount for buying together. This is strategically appropriate but leaves value on the table vs. true product bundling (e.g., a curated skincare routine packaged as a "Complete Morning Routine"). True product bundles command price premiums, reduce comparison shopping, and create barriers to substitution. Invest in product bundling (curated sets, starter kits) for high-margin product lines; use price bundling for accessory cross-sell. Always offer mixed bundling (individual products still available) unless the product bundle genuinely cannot be decomposed.
- **Replication Status**: Framework paper with 2,000+ Google Scholar citations. Widely adopted in marketing strategy literature. The mixed > pure bundling finding is supported by multiple empirical studies.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Product bundling requires genuine functional integration or strong curation value — consumers resist bundles where the integration feels arbitrary. Price bundling is only effective when the discount is perceptible (minimum 15%, ideally 20–25%; see Finding 14). Pure bundling works for services with high switchover costs (software suites) but typically backfires in e-commerce where customers can order individual items from competitors.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (high-impact synthesis paper; mixed bundling > pure bundling finding has empirical support; primary framework is conceptual)

### Finding 29: Bundle Discount Presentation — Aggregate vs. Itemized Savings
- **Source**: Janiszewski, C., & Cunha, M., Jr. (2004). "The Influence of Price Discount Framing on the Evaluation of a Product Bundle." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 30(4), 534–546. <https://doi.org/10.1086/380287>
- **Methodology**: Six studies (grouped into four primary experiments in the article body) testing how the framing of bundle discounts affects perceived value. Compared aggregate savings presentation ("Save $20 on the bundle") vs. itemized savings presentation ("Save $8 on Item A, $12 on Item B") across complementary and non-complementary product sets. Sample: undergraduate students; N = 80–140 per experiment. Controlled for actual discount magnitude.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: Experiment count corrected to six studies (per Oxford Academic JCR abstract: "Six studies are used to provide evidence..."), grouped into four primary experiments in the article body. Prior version said "Four laboratory experiments."
- **Key Finding**: For **complementary product bundles** (items that go together naturally), aggregate savings framing ("Bundle saves $20") yields higher perceived value and purchase intent than itemized savings framing. Customers perceive the complementary bundle as a unified entity — breaking it into parts reduces the gestalt value. For **non-complementary bundles** (disparate items that don't obviously belong together), itemized savings framing outperforms aggregate, because customers evaluate each item individually and need to see savings on items they actually want.
- **E-Commerce Application**: For thematically coherent bundles (skincare routines, camera kits, outfit coordinates), use aggregate savings: "Complete Kit — $79 (Save $21!)". Do NOT show item-level discounts. For cross-category promotions combining unrelated items, show per-item savings so customers can evaluate each piece. Test which framing applies to your specific bundle composition.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated within the paper across four experiments. Cited 300+ times. Consistent with gestalt processing and mental accounting theory (Thaler, 1999). No recent large-scale direct replication found, but the theoretical mechanism is robust.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires clear product complementarity perception — if customers don't perceive the items as going together, the "treat as a unit" processing doesn't activate. Category knowledge moderates: experts may process bundles more analytically regardless of framing.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JCR, four experiments, controlled for discount magnitude)

### Finding 30: Build-Your-Own Bundle (BYOB) and Customization Value
- **Source**: Dellaert, B.G.C., & Stremersch, S. (2005). "Marketing Mass-Customized Products: Striking a Balance Between Utility and Complexity." *Journal of Marketing Research*, 42(2), 219–227. <https://doi.org/10.1509/jmkr.42.2.219.62293>
- **Methodology**: Empirical study of consumer responses to product configuration complexity in mass-customization PC-configuration research. Measured purchase intent, satisfaction, and decision difficulty across different levels of configuration complexity. The 3–5 slot number is a practitioner extrapolation from this mass-customization research, not a directly measured BYOB-bundle result.
- **Key Finding**: Build-your-own bundle systems — "Pick any 3 for $X" — generate higher engagement and customization-driven satisfaction, but only up to a moderate level of complexity. Step-by-step selection UX (choose item 1 → choose item 2 → choose item 3) reduces cognitive load. Show running savings total updating in real-time. Cap at 5 items max. Present BYOB as a constrained curation exercise ("Pick 3 bestsellers") rather than open customization. Works best for replenishment categories (supplements, coffee, skincare).

- **Boundary Conditions**: BYOB systems add UX complexity and development cost. They work best when products are reasonably interchangeable (all the same category) and customers have prior category knowledge. BYOB fails when products require complex comparison, option set is poorly curated, or the UX is buggy/confusing.

### Finding 31: Dollar vs. Percentage Discount — Empirical Evidence for Framing Effects
- **Source**: Hardesty, D.M., & Bearden, W.O. (2003). "Consumer Evaluations of Different Promotion Types and Price Presentations: The Moderating Role of Promotional Benefit Level." *Journal of Retailing*, 79(1), 17–25. <https://doi.org/10.1016/S0022-4359(03)00003-4>
- **Methodology**: Laboratory experiment with adult consumer panel (N = 225). Tested promotion frames (dollar-off vs. percent-off) across products at varying price levels ($10 to $200). Measured perceived value of the promotion, purchase likelihood, and perceived discount magnitude. Between-subjects design with multiple price levels.
- **Key Finding**: Dollar-off frames generated higher perceived value than equivalent percent-off frames for *high-priced* products (above approximately $100); percent-off frames generated higher perceived value for *lower-priced* products. The mechanism: consumers evaluate discount magnitude relative to the *larger* of the two numbers — for cheap products, a percentage number (e.g., 20%) is larger than a dollar amount (e.g., $3); for expensive products, a dollar amount (e.g., $200 off) is larger than a percentage (e.g., 10% off). This empirically validates the Rule of 100 heuristic (Finding 12) at the mechanism level.
- **E-Commerce Application**: The Rule of 100 (Finding 12) now has peer-reviewed support for *why* it works. Implement it: display discount as "% off" for products under ~$100; display as "Save $X" for products over ~$100. For a $150 jacket with a $30 discount: "Save $30" will outperform "20% off" in perceived deal quality. At exactly $100, test both — the crossover is not a sharp threshold.
- **Replication Status**: The direction of the finding has been replicated across multiple studies (Darke & Freedman 1995; Grewal et al. 1998; Chandrashekaran 2004). Robust finding in the pricing literature.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Crossover point is not exactly $100 — depends on the actual discount percentage and absolute amount. The threshold is a heuristic, not a precise inflection. Effect is reduced for consumers who mentally calculate both — they'll arrive at the same dollar amount regardless of framing.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed Journal of Retailing, controlled experiment)

### Finding 32: "Free" as a Special Category — BOGO vs. Percent-Off Frames
- **Source**: Sinha, I., & Smith, M.F. (2000). "Consumers' Perceptions of Promotional Framing of Price." *Psychology & Marketing*, 17(3), 257–275. <https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1520-6793(200003)17:3<257::AID-MAR4>3.0.CO;2-P>. Extended by: Shampanier, K., Mazar, N., & Ariely, D. (2007). "Zero as a Special Price: The True Value of Free Products." *Marketing Science*, 26(6), 742–757. <https://doi.org/10.1287/mksc.1060.0254>
- **Methodology**: Sinha & Smith: Experimental design comparing three promotion frames for identical discount: (1) "50% off" when buying two, (2) "Buy One Get One Free" (BOGO), (3) "Buy Two, Get 50% Off." Measured transaction value perception and store preference. Shampanier et al.: Series of field experiments at MIT involving real purchase choices between paid and free items.
- **Key Finding**: BOGO outperforms "50% off when you buy 2" and "Buy Two, Get 50% Off" in transaction value perception and store preference, even though all three are mathematically equivalent at 25% per-unit discount. The word "free" creates a distinct psychological category: Shampanier et al. demonstrate that reducing price to zero triggers a response beyond rational economic calculation — "free" items are chosen even when the paid alternative offers objectively better value. The zero-price effect is not merely anchoring; it is a qualitatively different emotional state.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When running buy-multiple promotions, always use BOGO framing when possible. "Buy one, get one free" will outperform "50% off when you buy 2" even at identical economics. The word "free" must be accurate. "Free gift with purchase" works for the same reason. "Free shipping" (over a threshold) leverages the zero-price effect — the shipping becomes psychologically free, not just discounted.
- **Replication Status**: Sinha & Smith: replicated in multiple follow-on studies in promotional framing literature. Shampanier et al. zero-price effect: replicated in field and lab settings; cited 1,000+ times; among the most robust findings in behavioral pricing.
- **Boundary Conditions**: BOGO requires customers to want two units — it fails for products where customers only need one. The zero-price effect is strongest when choosing between free and near-free alternatives; less strong when comparing free against much better quality paid alternatives.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (Sinha & Smith: peer-reviewed; Shampanier et al.: field experiments with real purchases, *Marketing Science*)

### Finding 33: Stacked Discount Arithmetic Errors — The Numeracy Gap
- **Source**: Chen, H., & Rao, A.R. (2007). "When Two Plus Two Is Not Equal to Four: Errors in Processing Multiple Percentage Changes." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 34(3), 327–340. <https://doi.org/10.1086/518531>
- **Methodology**: Multiple laboratory studies (N ≈ 60–150 per study) testing consumer comprehension of sequential vs. combined percentage discounts. Participants evaluated promotions involving multiple percentage changes. Measured perceived discount magnitude, perceived value, and purchase intent. Controlled for product category and price level.
- **Key Finding**: Consumers systematically add sequential percentage changes instead of compounding them. For "20% off + additional 10% off," the mathematically correct combined discount is 28% (not 30%), but most consumers perceive it as 30%. This systematic overestimation means stacked discount framing ("20% off + extra 10% for members") generates *higher perceived value* than the mathematically equivalent single discount ("28% off"), even though the financial outcome is identical. The error is consistent across education levels.
- **E-Commerce Application**: When layering promotions (sale + coupon code, sale + loyalty discount), present them as separate stacked savings rather than a combined total. "20% off + extra 10% for subscribers" will be perceived as more valuable than "28% off for subscribers." Display each component as a separate line in the savings summary: "Sale Discount: -20% / Loyalty Bonus: -10%" outperforms "Total discount: -28." Note: this should only be used when the discounts are genuinely separate tiers — fabricating stacked discounts from a single discount is deceptive.
- **Replication Status**: Replicated within the paper across multiple experiments and product categories. Consistent with broader numeracy and arithmetic error literature. No retraction.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect is reduced (but not eliminated) for consumers with high numeracy or explicit calculation prompts. In competitive price-matching contexts where consumers compare total savings across retailers, the error is less persistent. Works best in environments where shoppers are not actively computing final prices.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JCR, multiple studies)

### Finding 34: Discount Depth Thresholds — Perceptual Breakpoints for Promotion Effectiveness
- **Source**: Chandrashekaran, R. (2004). "The Influence of Redundant Comparison Prices and Other Price Presentation Formats on Consumers' Evaluations and Purchase Intentions." *Journal of Retailing*, 80(1), 53–66. <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jretai.2004.01.003>. Supplemented by: Grewal, D., Marmorstein, H., & Sharma, A. (1996). "Communicating Price Information Through Semantic Cues." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 23(2), 148–155. <https://doi.org/10.1086/209472>
- **Methodology**: Chandrashekaran: Laboratory experiment testing price presentation formats across discount levels. Grewal et al.: Experiments varying discount size (5%, 10%, 20%, 30%, 50%) and communication format, measuring perceived value and purchase intent (N ≈ 120 per study, multiple studies).
- **Key Finding** (practitioner synthesis across Chandrashekaran 2004 and Grewal et al. 1996): Discount effectiveness follows a non-linear pattern with four approximate breakpoints: (1) **Sub-threshold discounts (~5–10%)** produce minimal change in purchase intent — consumers perceive them as not worth changing behavior; (2) **Mid-range discounts (~15–25%)** generate meaningful perceived value and behavior change — the "believable deal" zone; (3) **Large discounts (~30–50%)** generate strong urgency but trigger quality skepticism, especially for unfamiliar brands; (4) **Extreme discounts (~60%+)** trigger strong skepticism — "What's wrong with it?" responses dominate outside genuine clearance contexts. Note: these four bands are a practitioner synthesis across both studies; neither paper states these exact breakpoints as a unified four-tier framework.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Set promotional discount floors at 15% for general promotions (below this, the promotion may cost margin without meaningfully moving conversion). Target 20–30% for major promotions. Reserve 40–50% for genuine clearance/end-of-season contexts where customers expect inventory liquidation framing. For promotions below 15%, consider framing them as bonus value-adds (free gift, free shipping, extended warranty) rather than price cuts, since small percentage discounts underperform added-value framing.
- **Replication Status**: The directional breakpoints are supported across multiple studies; specific thresholds vary by category. Electronics shoppers have lower thresholds than fashion shoppers; luxury has even tighter tolerance for deep discounts. The direction of finding is robust even if exact numbers vary by category.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Discount depth perception is highly category-specific. A 20% off in fine dining feels huge; 20% off in budget clothing is barely noticed. Familiar brands with known regular prices require deeper discounts to motivate behavior change.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: Breakpoints reframed as "practitioner synthesis" with tilde notation (~) to reflect that the four-band framework is derived across both sources, not stated in either paper's abstract as a single unified finding.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (Grewal et al.: peer-reviewed JCR; Chandrashekaran: peer-reviewed Journal of Retailing)

### Finding 35: Goal-Gradient Effect — Scientific Foundation for Free Shipping Progress Bars
- **Source**: Kivetz, R., Urminsky, O., & Zheng, Y. (2006). "The Goal-Gradient Hypothesis Resurrected: Purchase Acceleration, Illusionary Goal Progress, and Customer Retention." *Journal of Marketing Research*, 43(1), 39–58. <https://doi.org/10.1509/jmkr.43.1.39>
- **Methodology**: Multi-study field experiment and laboratory design. Field experiment: real coffee stamp loyalty card program (N = 900+ customers). Researchers measured inter-purchase time as customers approached the 10th stamp for a free coffee reward. Study 2: manipulated "illusionary head start" — cards starting with 2 stamps already on a 12-stamp card vs. a fresh 10-stamp card (both required 10 additional stamps for reward).
- **Key Finding**: Purchase frequency accelerated substantially as customers approached the reward threshold. The Columbia Business School study summary notes median completion times of approximately 10 days (illusionary head-start group) vs. approximately 15 days (control), driven by perceived proximity to the goal rather than any difference in actual remaining effort. The illusionary head-start effect is substantial and robust across the study's field and laboratory components. The effect is driven by perceived proximity to the goal, not the absolute number of actions remaining.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Free shipping progress bars work because of the goal-gradient effect — showing "You're $18 away from free shipping!" creates a mental goal state that accelerates purchase behavior as the customer approaches the threshold. The "head start" finding suggests pre-loading perceived progress: "You already qualify for standard shipping — add $18 more for FREE shipping" outperforms a blank progress bar starting from zero. Use specific dollar amounts ("$18 away") not round numbers ("about $20 away") — specificity increases goal salience. Update the progress bar in real-time on item add.
- **Replication Status**: One of the most-cited goal-gradient papers in marketing (1,800+ Google Scholar citations). Replicated in loyalty program, e-commerce, and gamification contexts. The foundational Hull (1932) animal learning result has been replicated hundreds of times. Robust finding.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires customers to be actively engaged in goal pursuit — passive browsers don't exhibit goal-gradient acceleration. Goal must be perceived as achievable: if the threshold is too far away, the gradient disappears. Illusionary progress is ethically acceptable when based on real baseline activity; fake pre-loaded progress bars unrelated to actual behavior are manipulative.
- **Audit Note (2026-04-21)**: The prior version cited "82% faster purchase acceleration" and "82% completion rate lift." These specific percentages could not be content-matched against any accessible paper summary. The Columbia Business School study summary (median 10 vs. 15 days) is the best accessible concrete datapoint. The directional finding — substantial acceleration and significant head-start effect — is fully supported.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JMR, field experiment with real purchases, extensively replicated)

### Finding 36: Unexpected Costs as the #1 Controllable Cart Abandonment Cause
- **Source**: Baymard Institute. (2024). "Cart Abandonment Rate Statistics." Ongoing research program. https://baymard.com/lists/cart-abandonment-rate. Primary data: proprietary large-scale consumer survey; current live page verified April 2026.
- **Methodology**: Baymard's abandonment reason data comes from their large-scale consumer survey. Results are from the subset who abandoned for reasons other than "just browsing." Survey waves are updated periodically; specific percentages are wave-dependent.
- **Key Finding** (current wave, 2024): **39%** of US online shoppers who abandon carts cite "extra costs too high" (shipping, tax, fees) as their primary reason — the #1 controllable reason. This is directly addressable by free shipping threshold implementation, early cost disclosure, and transparent pricing. Additional abandonment reasons from current wave: 21% delivery too slow; 19% didn't trust site with credit card; 19% site required account creation; 18% checkout too long/complicated; 14% couldn't see total order cost up-front. Baymard estimates $260 billion in potentially recoverable revenue in US+EU annually.
- **E-Commerce Application**: The business case for a free shipping threshold is direct: a substantial share of motivated shoppers are abandoning because of cost surprise. Implement: (1) free shipping threshold clearly communicated in site-wide header, (2) progress bar in cart showing distance to threshold, (3) product recommendations in cart for items that would qualify the order. The account creation reason is a CRO concern — consider guest checkout to address it simultaneously.
- **Replication Status**: Baymard is the authoritative source on e-commerce checkout usability. Their survey data has been published consistently since 2010 with stable findings. The #1-controllable-reason status of "extra costs too high" is stable across waves; specific percentages vary between waves. Consistent with similar surveys from Statista, SaleCycle, and Klaviyo.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Percentages are from US shoppers; EU and international data varies. B2B shoppers with expense accounts show less shipping cost sensitivity. For products where shipping cost is inherently high (oversized furniture, heavy equipment), threshold strategies are less effective than flat-rate or included shipping.
- **Citation Status**: Current Baymard live page (verified April 2026) shows 39% for "extra costs too high." An earlier survey wave (February 2024) reported 48% per multiple secondary sources (eMarketer, Shopify, Contentsquare). ### Finding 37: Free vs. Paid Shipping Demand Elasticity — The Cost-Revenue Tradeoff
- **Source**: Lewis, M. (2006). "The Effect of Shipping Fees on Customer Acquisition, Customer Retention, and Purchase Quantities." *Journal of Retailing*, 82(1), 13–23. <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jretai.2005.11.005>
- **Methodology**: Field experiment using natural variation in shipping fee structures across a major online grocery retailer's customer base. Analyzed 38,000+ transactions over multiple periods with different shipping fee schedules ($0 free shipping vs. flat fee vs. variable fee). Controlled for customer tenure, purchase frequency, basket size, and product category.
- **Key Finding**: Free shipping increased order incidence by 14.8% compared to flat-fee shipping. However, free shipping *reduced* revenue per order because customers who previously paid shipping fees no longer needed to justify those fees with larger baskets. The net effect on revenue was positive only when combined with a minimum order threshold — threshold-based free shipping captured the demand increase while preserving or growing basket size. Flat free shipping (no threshold) is a value transfer, not a revenue strategy.
- **E-Commerce Application**: This study directly establishes *why* thresholds matter. Free shipping with no threshold is a customer acquisition tool that trades margin for volume. Free shipping with a well-set threshold converts the cost into an AOV driver. The 15–25% above AOV rule (Finding 16) reflects this economics: set the threshold high enough that reaching it requires basket-building, but low enough that the goal is achievable. Lewis's finding on demand elasticity (+14.8%) is the demand-side estimate; the AOV lift from threshold-reaching behavior (15–30% per Finding 16) is the supply-side offset.
- **Replication Status**: Single field experiment with a large real-transaction dataset. The direction of findings (free shipping increases orders, threshold preserves margin) has been replicated in subsequent e-commerce research and is consistent with economic theory. Specific elasticity numbers (14.8%) are context-specific.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Study was conducted on grocery/subscription-type purchases with high repurchase frequency. Demand elasticity for free shipping varies substantially by product category: commodity goods show higher elasticity; high-consideration goods show lower elasticity because customers have already decided to buy and are less influenced by shipping terms at the margin.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed Journal of Retailing, large real-transaction field experiment)

### Finding 38: Asymmetric Dominance (Decoy Effect) — Original Evidence and Conditions
- **Source**: Huber, J., Payne, J.W., & Puto, C. (1982). "Adding Asymmetrically Dominated Alternatives: Violations of Regularity and the Similarity Hypothesis." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 9(1), 90–98. <https://doi.org/10.1086/208899>
- **Methodology**: Multiple experiments at Duke University. Participants chose between sets of consumer products (beer, cars, restaurants, lotteries). A third "decoy" option was added that was dominated by one target option on at least one attribute but not clearly dominated by the other option. Choice shares were measured across conditions with and without the decoy. N = approximately 153 participants across experiments.
- **Key Finding**: Adding an asymmetrically dominated decoy reliably increased choice share for the target option by 13% to 22% absolute choice share shift across product categories. This violates the regularity condition of standard rational choice theory (adding a new option should never increase the relative share of an existing option). However, see Finding 5 for the replication context: of 91 replication attempts reviewed by Frederick et al. (2014), only 11 were reliable. The effect is condition-dependent.
- **E-Commerce Application**: The decoy must be *asymmetrically* dominated — clearly worse than the target but not obviously dominated by all options. The classic SaaS application: if you want customers to choose the "Pro" ($99/mo, 15 users) tier, add a "Pro Lite" decoy ($89/mo, 10 users) that is clearly worse than Pro on price efficiency but makes Pro's value obvious. However, the compromise effect (Finding 39) is a more reliable mechanism for achieving similar outcomes. See Finding 5 for full replication caveats.
- **Replication Status**: The original paper has 1,500+ citations and has been replicated across dozens of studies in lab settings. In real-world e-commerce, the effect is condition-dependent and unreliable. See Finding 5 for the definitive replication context.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Requires: simple comparable options (2–3 dimensions); clearly dominated decoy; small choice sets (≤3 options). In complex real-world purchase decisions, the effect is unreliable.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JCR foundational paper); effect reliability in field settings is Silver — see Finding 5.

### Finding 39: Compromise Effect — The Reliable Middle-Choice Mechanism
- **Source**: Simonson, I. (1989). "Choice Based on Reasons: The Case of Attraction and Compromise Effects." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 16(2), 158–174. <https://doi.org/10.1086/209205>. Extended by: Simonson, I., & Tversky, A. (1992). "Choice in Context: Tradeoff Contrast and Extremeness Aversion." *Journal of Marketing Research*, 29(3), 281–295. <https://doi.org/10.1177/002224379202900301>
- **Methodology**: Simonson (1989): Multiple lab experiments (N = 80–180 per study). Participants chose among product sets where a middle option was added or removed. Tested cameras, microwave ovens, and other consumer durables. Simonson & Tversky (1992): Extended framework with field data from supermarket choices and lab studies, testing tradeoff contrast and extremeness aversion as separate mechanisms.
- **Key Finding**: When consumers face uncertainty about the best choice, they systematically favor compromise options — those in the middle of the range on price and attributes. This is driven by **extremeness aversion**: the psychological cost of choosing the cheapest option (fear of low quality) and the most expensive (fear of overpaying) both push consumers toward the middle. This is more robust and reliable than the decoy effect: it does not require an asymmetrically dominated option — any three-option set where one option is the middle generates preference for that middle. Simonson (1989) shows middle option selection rates of 45–55% vs. chance expectation of 33%.
- **E-Commerce Application**: The compromise effect is the primary reason three-tier pricing works in practice — more so than the decoy effect. For pricing pages: (1) Make your target tier the middle tier in *both* price and features; (2) Use the lowest tier to make the target look like a meaningful upgrade; (3) Use the highest tier to make the target look like reasonable value; (4) The "Most Popular" badge on the middle tier reinforces the compromise effect by providing social proof that the middle choice is the modal choice. For SaaS: if your highest-margin tier is "Pro" at $79/mo, add a genuine "Enterprise" at $199/mo to make Pro feel like the sensible middle ground.
- **Replication Status**: Extensively replicated. Simonson (1989) has 1,900+ citations; Simonson & Tversky (1992) has 2,100+. Confirmed in cross-cultural studies (US and Israeli samples), in supermarket field data, and in online choice experiments. More robust than the decoy effect (Finding 38) across real-world conditions.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect requires genuine uncertainty — expert consumers who have a strong prior preference are less susceptible. Weaker when consumers have strong brand loyalty to a specific tier. If different tiers serve genuinely different use cases, compromise logic doesn't apply (customers should choose the right tier, not the middle tier).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed JCR + JMR, multiple studies, cross-cultural replication, field data)

### Finding 40: Good-Better-Best Architecture — Strategic Revenue Capture
- **Source**: Mohammed, R. (2018). "The Good-Better-Best Approach to Pricing." *Harvard Business Review*, September–October 2018. https://hbr.org/2018/09/the-good-better-best-approach-to-pricing. Supplemented by economic modeling: Mussa, M., & Rosen, S. (1978). "Monopoly and Product Quality." *Journal of Economic Theory*, 18(2), 301–317. <https://doi.org/10.1016/0022-0531(78)90085-6>
- **Methodology**: Mohammed (2018): HBR practitioner article synthesizing case studies from companies that implemented Good-Better-Best (GBB) pricing, with revenue outcome data. Mussa & Rosen (1978): Foundational economic theory paper on second-degree price discrimination / vertical product differentiation — the theoretical basis for why tiered quality-price menus maximize revenue under heterogeneous willingness-to-pay.
- **Key Finding**: Mohammed (2018) reports that companies implementing Good-Better-Best tiered structures achieve revenue increases in roughly the 25–40% range compared to single-price or two-price models. **Caveat**: this range is aggregated from practitioner case studies without full disclosure of methodology; treat as directional, not as a precise effect estimate. The strategic logic: single pricing captures only customers whose WTP exactly matches the price point; three tiers capture three bands of WTP. The "Good" tier prevents customer defection to competitors; the "Better" tier is the primary profit driver; the "Best" tier captures high-WTP customers who would have been under-charged at the "Better" price. Mussa & Rosen: Economic proof that a menu of quality tiers is always at least as profitable as a single quality level under heterogeneous WTP.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Structure product offerings as explicit tiers (individual item, multi-pack, subscription; OR standard, plus, pro). Avoid naming the entry tier in ways that create stigma — "Essentials," "Standard," or "Starter" perform better than "Basic" or "Limited" for paid entry tiers. Price gaps: the "Good → Better" gap should feel like a meaningful upgrade for reasonable incremental cost; the "Better → Best" gap can be larger to justify the premium segment. Do not set "Good" price so low it cannibalizes "Better" — the "Good" tier exists to prevent competitor defection, not to serve as the primary revenue driver.
- **Replication Status**: Mohammed's HBR framework has been widely adopted in SaaS, DTC, and enterprise software pricing. The 25–40% revenue lift range comes from practitioner case studies synthesized by Mohammed (2018) without peer review. Mussa & Rosen's economic theory is foundational (500+ citations in economics). The conceptual case for tiered pricing is well-established; specific revenue lift numbers are practitioner-grade estimates.
- **Boundary Conditions**: GBB architecture fails when tiers are not genuinely differentiated; when "Good" is too generous and cannibalizes "Better"; when "Best" is priced so high it doesn't serve as a credible anchor (>4× the "Better" price typically fails as an anchor); or when product complexity prevents clear tier comparison.
- **Citation Status**: The Mohammed HBR article is behind a paywall; the specific 25–40% revenue lift range is widely repeated in practitioner/CRO literature attributing it to this article but could not be content-matched against the accessible portion at audit time. Treat the range as a practitioner synthesis figure, not a peer-validated number.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (Mohammed 2018: HBR practitioner research; Mussa & Rosen 1978: Gold for theoretical foundation)

### Finding 41: California SB-478 — State-Level Ban on Hidden Fees
- **Source**: California Civil Code §§ 1770(a)(29), 1771.1 (added by SB-478, 2023; effective July 1, 2024). https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240SB478
- **Methodology**: California statute; enforceable by Cal. AG under UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200) and via private right of action under the Consumers Legal Remedies Act (CLRA).
- **Key Finding**: California prohibits advertising, displaying, or offering a price that excludes mandatory fees, with two exceptions: (a) government-imposed taxes and (b) reasonable shipping costs actually charged. Drip pricing is a per se CLRA violation for sales into California. Civil penalty under UCL: $2,500 per violation (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17206).
- **E-Commerce Application**: US e-commerce serving California customers must include all mandatory non-government-tax, non-shipping fees in the advertised price. Service fees, convenience fees, processing fees, handling fees must be in the stated price — not added at checkout. Optional fees (e.g., expedited shipping upgrades, gift wrapping) are not mandatory and thus not covered.
- **Replication Status**: Regulatory requirement. Enforceable since July 1, 2024. Multiple private suits filed 2024–2025.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Consumer-facing sales into California. B2B contracts are generally exempt from CLRA. Government taxes and reasonable shipping remain permissible separate line items. Genuinely optional fees are outside scope.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (primary regulatory source)
- **Added**: Vera reconciled audit, 2026-04-22

### Finding 43: Charm Pricing Robustness Caution — Preregistered Online Null Result
- **Source**: Macé, S., & Le Lec, F. (2022). "Psychological price perception may exert a weaker effect on purchasing decisions than previously suggested: Results from a large online experiment fail to reproduce either a left-digit or perceptual-fluency effect." *PLOS ONE*, 17(8), e0270850. <https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0270850>
- **Methodology**: Large preregistered online experiment (>3,000 participants) testing left-digit effects and round-number fluency effects in a modern online shopping task. Open-access; full data and preregistration publicly available.
- **Key Finding**: Preregistered analysis failed to find a left-digit effect or a perceptual-fluency effect on purchasing decisions. Authors conclude that effect sizes in clean modern online conditions may be smaller than classical estimates, and that many prior published effects may reflect publication bias or specific in-store / catalog conditions not replicable online.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Temper expectations about charm pricing as a general online-conversion lever. Read alongside Finding 3 (Anderson & Simester catalog field evidence still positive), Finding 21 (Troll & Loschelder: small meta-analytic effect g=0.13), and Finding 4 (left-digit mechanism). Charm pricing is a low-cost micro-optimization, not a growth strategy. Test in your specific context rather than assuming the effect will apply.
- **Replication Status**: Single large preregistered study; itself an informative replication failure. Adds to the boundary-condition evidence alongside Finding 5 (Frederick et al. 2014's 11/91 decoy replication rate is a parallel caution in a different domain).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Online-only (does not test in-store or catalog). Modern price-comparison context (side-by-side competitor prices readily visible) may be a specific boundary condition absent from older lab/catalog studies. Single null study should be weighted alongside the positive meta-analytic evidence in Finding 21.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (preregistered, large sample, peer-reviewed open-access; single null study against well-established positive meta-analytic finding)
- **Added**: Vera reconciled audit, 2026-04-22

## Methodological Notes and Caveats

1. **Lab vs. Field**: Many of the foundational studies (Ariely's decoy, Kahneman's endowment, Thomas & Morwitz's left-digit) were conducted in laboratory settings. Real-world effect sizes are typically smaller due to noise, competing cues, and consumer heterogeneity.

2. **Publication Bias**: The pricing psychology literature has a significant publication bias toward positive findings. The decoy effect replication data (Finding 5: only 11/91 reliable replications) is a cautionary example.

3. **Practitioner Case Studies**: Findings from VWO, MarketingSherpa, and similar sources (Findings 9, 13, 15) are not peer-reviewed. Sample sizes, test durations, and confounds are often not fully disclosed. Treat specific numbers as directional rather than definitive.

4. **Context Dependence**: Nearly every finding has boundary conditions. Charm pricing backfires for luxury brands. Decoy effects fail with complex choice sets. Loss aversion framing loses power with overuse. Always test in your specific context.

5. **Insufficient Data Found**: For the endowment effect specifically in digital cart/wishlist contexts (Question 5), no controlled experiment measuring the effect of cart-adding on purchase probability was found. The application is inferred from the broader endowment effect literature (Findings 10, 11) rather than directly measured in an e-commerce cart setting.
