## ⚠️ Ethics-Gate Cross-Reference

This file's subject matter sits directly on the ethics-gate.md Part 1.1 **BLOCK (CRITICAL)** line for false urgency/scarcity. ## Summary

### Top 3 Most Impactful Findings

1. **Finding 2 (Hmurovic, Lamberton & Goldsmith 2023)**: Landmark peer-reviewed finding: time scarcity promotions in **online retail now reliably activate persuasion knowledge** — consumers recognize the tactic, feel manipulated, and resist. Confirmed verbatim in AMA summary: "online time scarcity promotions tend to be less effective than promotions without time limits, largely due to the activation of increased persuasion knowledge." Effect reverses when urgency is justified by exogenous factors (birthdays, seasonality). This fundamentally challenges the assumption that countdown timers always help.
2. **Finding 1 (Barton, Zlatevska & Oppewal 2022)**: Pre-registered meta-analysis of 131 studies (416 effect sizes) showing overall positive effect on purchase intentions (r = 0.27, medium effect), but *quantity* scarcity consistently outperforms *time* scarcity for physical products in low-involvement contexts; both effects erode with consumer awareness of the tactic.
3. **Finding 5 (Park, Rabinovich, Tang & Yin — Bon-Ton study)**: Real (not fake) "Five or less left" inventory messages increased purchase frequency but decreased daily purchase quantities by **17.6%** on slow-moving products — the primary source of a widely-misattributed statistic. Correct attribution matters for ethics framing.

## Findings

### Finding 1: Meta-Analytic Evidence for Scarcity Effects — Quantity vs. Time Distinction

- **Source**: Barton, B.L., Zlatevska, N., & Oppewal, H. (2022). "Scarcity Tactics in Marketing: A Meta-Analysis of Product Scarcity Effects on Consumer Purchase Intentions." *Journal of Retailing*, 98(4), 741–758. <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jretai.2022.06.003> (ScienceDirect abstract: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022435922000434)
- **Methodology**: Pre-registered meta-analysis of **131 studies (416 effect sizes)** examining scarcity effects on consumer purchase intentions. Studies covered quantity scarcity (limited stock), time scarcity (limited time offers), and access scarcity (exclusive/membership). Moderator analyses tested product type, involvement level, message framing, and consumer awareness of the tactic. Effect sizes computed as Pearson's r.
- **Key Finding**: Overall scarcity effect on purchase intentions: r = 0.27 (medium effect; directionally confirmed from secondary literature; specific r= values are from the file's original cite, not independently verified from the paywalled full text). Key moderator findings: (1) **Quantity scarcity** (r ≈ 0.31) significantly outperforms **time scarcity** (r ≈ 0.22) for physical products in low-involvement contexts; (2) Scarcity effects are stronger for **hedonic** products than **utilitarian** products; (3) Effects are stronger when **consumers are not aware** the tactic is being used (r ≈ 0.34) vs. when they are aware (r ≈ 0.18); (4) Per the published abstract, **time-based scarcity has the greatest effect on high-involvement products** — the effect inverts for expensive considered purchases, where urgency cues operate on information-scarce consumers rather than persuasion-knowledge-activated ones. This high-involvement finding is qualified, however, by Hmurovic et al. (2023, Finding 2), which shows that in pure online retail, persuasion-knowledge activation undermines time scarcity even for high-involvement categories.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Prefer quantity scarcity messaging ("Only 3 left in stock") over time scarcity ("Sale ends tonight") for everyday physical products. Quantity scarcity is more credible, less easily identified as a tactic, and more effective for low-involvement purchases. For high-involvement purchases (expensive, considered), the meta-analysis finds time-based scarcity can work — but the Hmurovic et al. counter-finding suggests this advantage largely disappears in online retail due to persuasion knowledge activation. Display stock levels only when genuine and only at thresholds that are actually rare (not "low stock" on items always in abundant supply). See also Finding 5 on quantity-signal backfire for slow-moving SKUs.
- **Replication Status**: Pre-registered meta-analytic synthesis of 131 studies / 416 effect sizes — this IS the replication synthesis. Methodology reduces researcher degrees of freedom. Consistent with Lynn 1991 (Finding 3).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Effect sizes are from lab/survey studies; many involve hypothetical purchase scenarios — real-world conversion effects are smaller. Effect varies dramatically by implementation: genuine scarcity from real inventory systems outperforms manufactured scarcity in both effectiveness and regulatory standing. Highly comparison-shopping consumers (using price-alert apps) have lower scarcity susceptibility.
- **Citation Status**: ScienceDirect abstract accessible via linkinghub redirect; full text behind Elsevier paywall. DOI verified 2026-04-21.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed *Journal of Retailing*, Elsevier; pre-registered meta-analysis; 131 studies / 416 effect sizes)
- **Reconciler Note**: Prior version cited "106 studies (N=18,413 participants total)" — corrected to 131 studies / 416 effect sizes per ScienceDirect abstract and `social-proof-patterns.md` Finding 11. High-involvement direction clarified to match the published abstract.

### Finding 2: Time Scarcity Activates Persuasion Knowledge in Online Retail

- **Source**: Hmurovic, J., Lamberton, C., & Goldsmith, K. (2023). "Examining the Efficacy of Time Scarcity Marketing Promotions in Online Retail." *Journal of Marketing Research*, 60(2), 299–328. <https://doi.org/10.1177/00222437221118856> (accessible summary: https://www.ama.org/marketing-news/new-research-about-time-based-urgency-in-online-retail/)
- **Methodology**: Multi-study field experiment and laboratory design. Study 1: Analysis of real online retail purchase data examining conversion rates for time-limited vs. non-time-limited promotions across 1.2 million+ customer-promotion interactions (N not independently verified from public abstracts; figure from original research file). Studies 2–5: Controlled laboratory experiments (N = 150–400 per study) testing the persuasion knowledge activation mechanism. Persuasion knowledge measured via scale items assessing awareness of marketing intent and perceived manipulation. Compared online vs. in-store contexts.
- **Key Finding**: In online retail contexts, time scarcity promotions reliably activate consumers' **persuasion knowledge** — their awareness that they are being marketed to — which triggers psychological reactance and reduces promotion effectiveness compared to non-scarcity promotions. Confirmed verbatim in AMA (2025) summary: "online time scarcity promotions tend to be less effective than promotions without time limits, largely due to the activation of increased persuasion knowledge." This effect is specific to online retail; in-store time scarcity promotions (flash sales visible in-person) do not show the same persuasion knowledge activation. **Important nuance from the paper**: when retailer-exogenous justifications are given for online time scarcity (e.g., a customer's birthday, a seasonal event, a supply-chain change), persuasion knowledge activation is decreased — improving time scarcity's effectiveness. The mechanism: online countdown timers and "limited time" messaging have become so ubiquitous that consumers have learned to recognize and distrust them.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Countdown timers are NOT reliably effective for all e-commerce contexts. Effectiveness depends on: (1) Audience exposure — highly experienced online shoppers are more susceptible to persuasion-knowledge activation; (2) Verifiability — genuine flash sales with real, non-repeating deadlines produce less reactance; (3) Context — new product launches, truly limited editions, and seasonal events with credible external reasons still benefit from time messaging; (4) **Exogenous justification** — tying urgency to a reason outside the retailer's control (a holiday, a birthday, a supplier deadline) measurably reduces persuasion-knowledge activation and improves effectiveness. Reserve countdown timers for authentically time-limited events. For general "sale" urgency, quantity scarcity (real inventory depletion) is more credible and activates less persuasion knowledge.
- **Replication Status**: Large-scale field data (1.2M+ interactions) combined with five experimental studies. Published in a top-3 marketing journal (JMR). Recent (2023); independent replication not yet published as of 2026. The persuasion-knowledge mechanism is a well-established theoretical construct (Friestad & Wright, 1994 — one of the most-cited papers in advertising research).
- **Boundary Conditions**: The persuasion-knowledge effect is strongest among high-frequency online shoppers with extensive exposure to urgency tactics. Weaker for: first-time customers unfamiliar with the retailer's promotion patterns; niche or specialty retailers with less e-commerce-saturated customer bases; genuinely unusual scarcity events (natural disasters, celebrity demand spikes). In-store promotions, physical pop-ups, and direct-mail urgency are less affected because the channel is associated with less systematic urgency manipulation.
- **Citation Status**: Primary paywalled (SAGE); DOI resolves to live SAGE page. AMA accessible summary added as reader-access secondary. DOI verified 2026-04-21.
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (peer-reviewed *Journal of Marketing Research*; multi-study design combining large-scale field data and controlled experiments)

### Finding 3: Commodity Theory Meta-Analysis — Why Scarcity Increases Value

- **Source**: Lynn, M. (1991). "Scarcity Effects on Value: A Quantitative Review of the Commodity Theory Literature." *Psychology & Marketing*, 8(1), 43–57. <https://doi.org/10.1002/mar.4220080105> — Foundation: Brock, T.C. (1968). "Implications of Commodity Theory for Value Change." In Greenwald, Brock & Ostrom (Eds.), *Psychological Foundations of Attitudes* (pp. 243–275). Academic Press.
- **Methodology**: Lynn (1991): Quantitative review of 21 published studies testing commodity theory predictions. Computed effect sizes and tested moderating conditions. Studies varied manipulation (quantity limits, access restrictions, explicit rarity claims) and outcomes (perceived value, desire, purchase intent). Brock (1968): Theoretical paper establishing commodity theory — the principle that scarce items are more valued because possession conveys personal distinctiveness and social status.
- **Key Finding**: Lynn's meta-analysis confirmed that scarcity reliably increases perceived value across 21 studies, with stronger effects for: (1) **Supply-restricted scarcity** (limited quantity) than demand-restricted scarcity (high demand); (2) **Exclusive access restrictions** (invitation only, membership required) over time-based restrictions; (3) Products associated with **self-expression and status signaling** rather than functional utility. Three mechanisms account for the effect: (a) quality inference ("scarce = good, must be popular"); (b) uniqueness value ("I'll have something others don't"); (c) social value ("owning this signals status"). These three pathways operate independently and additively.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Design scarcity messaging to activate the most relevant mechanism for your product type. For **luxury/premium products**: activate uniqueness and status pathways — "Exclusive to members," "Limited edition of 200," "Not available in stores." For **popular/mainstream products**: activate quality inference — "Selling fast," "Back in stock — limited quantity," "Most popular item in this category." For **utilitarian products**: quantity-based scarcity ("Only 2 left") is more credible than access-restriction framing, which feels incongruous on commodity items.
- **Replication Status**: Lynn (1991) is a 35-year-old meta-analysis — its findings have been extensively replicated and extended by Barton et al. (2022, Finding 1). The three-mechanism model is well-established. Commodity theory (Brock 1968) has 700+ citations. Robust theoretical and empirical foundation.
- **Boundary Conditions**: Scarcity value increases are reduced or reversed when: consumers believe scarcity is artificial or manufactured (triggers reactance); the product category has no plausible reason to be scarce (commodity goods); consumers have already purchased or rejected the item (scarcity doesn't restart consideration). The effect reverses for products consumers are *trying to avoid* — scarcity of an undesirable option increases relief, not desire.
- **Citation Status**: Lynn 1991 behind Wiley paywall; DOI verified to resolve. Brock 1968 is a seminal book chapter with no online URL (citation format consistent with evidence-tiers.md guidance).
- **Evidence Tier**: Gold (Lynn 1991: peer-reviewed *Psychology & Marketing*, Wiley, quantitative review; Brock 1968: foundational theoretical work in attitude research)

### Finding 4: Fake Scarcity Dark Patterns — Backfire Effects and Regulatory Risk

- **Source**: Bongard-Blanchy, K., Rossi, A., Rivas, S., Doublet, S., Koenig, V., & Lenzini, G. (2021). "'I Am Definitely Manipulated, Even When I am Aware of it. It's Ridiculous!' — Dark Patterns from the End-User Perspective." *Designing Interactive Systems (DIS) 2021*. https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3461778.3462086 (open-access PDF via King's College London: https://kclpure.kcl.ac.uk/portal/files/164075875/3463676.3485611.pdf) — Also: Gray, C.M., Kou, Y., Battles, B., Hoggatt, J., & Toombs, A. (2018). "The Dark (Patterns) Side of UX Design." *CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems*. <https://doi.org/10.1145/3173574.3174108> — Regulatory: see Ethics-Gate Cross-Reference section above for full primary-source URLs.
- **Methodology**: Bongard-Blanchy et al. (2021): Survey of 413 online users (N = 406 after excluding 7 gibberish responses) asking about awareness and ethical acceptability of 17 dark-pattern/nudge categories, including urgency and scarcity manipulation. Gray et al. (2018): Systematic analysis of dark pattern typologies in major e-commerce sites (N = 11 sites), including false scarcity classification.
- **Key Finding**: Bongard-Blanchy et al. (N = 406): Users "were generally aware of the influence that manipulative designs can exert on their online behaviour, but being aware does not equip users with the ability to oppose such influence" — 77% of respondents were aware of urgency/scarcity nudges; 63% rated false scarcity as ethically unacceptable (highest unacceptability rate of all 17 nudge categories tested); 44% reported acting on these nudges despite awareness — short-term lift is possible with long-term trust destruction. Gray et al.: False scarcity (timers that reset, stock counts that don't change) is documented at scale across major e-commerce sites. **Legal regime (corrected)**: The per-se prohibition on false scarcity is **UCPD 2005/29/EC Annex I, point 7** — no case-by-case assessment required. Directive (EU) 2019/2161 raised maximum penalties for widespread UCPD violations to at least 4% of annual EU turnover. EU DSA Art 25 further prohibits dark patterns on online intermediary platforms. US federal authority: FTC Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. § 45) — the FTC's 2022 staff report "Bringing Dark Patterns to Light" explicitly classifies false urgency/scarcity as deceptive (https://www.ftc.gov/reports/bringing-dark-patterns-light). **Penalties**: FTC Section 5 — $53,088 per violation (2025 adjusted rate); DSA Art 52 — up to 6% of worldwide annual turnover; UCPD — up to 4% of annual EU turnover for systematic cross-border violations.
- **E-Commerce Application**: The "fake scarcity detection" problem is now systematic: (1) Browser extensions like Honey and Capital One Shopping track historical prices and scarcity claims, alerting users to timers that have run before; (2) Review sites (Reddit, Trustpilot) specifically call out retailers using perpetual "sale ends tonight" banners; (3) EU UCPD Annex I point 7 creates per-se liability — no balancing test; (4) FTC Section 5 authority applies to false timers as deceptive practice, with state AGs independently empowered to act. The risk calculus: short-term conversion lift (real, documented) vs. long-term trust erosion + regulatory exposure + negative earned media. The only responsible path: genuine scarcity only. Real stock levels, real deadlines, real limits.
- **Replication Status**: The awareness data is from a 2021 survey (N = 406); the dark-pattern taxonomy is from 2018 field research. Consumer awareness of urgency tactics is increasing over time (consistent with Hmurovic et al. 2023 in Finding 2). Regulatory action is escalating and is the most objective measure of enforcement trajectory.
- **Boundary Conditions**: The ethical and regulatory concern applies specifically to *false* scarcity. Genuine scarcity messaging (real stock counts, real deadlines) is legally protected and ethically sound. The backfire risk applies to fake urgency, not to authentic urgency. Some categories (luxury goods, limited editions) have higher cultural tolerance for genuine scarcity marketing.
- **Citation Status**: Bongard-Blanchy ACM primary URL 403 (paywall); King's College London open-access PDF added. Gray et al. CHI paywalled at ACM; citation metadata verified via DOI. Regulatory URLs verified against Eur-Lex, Cornell LII, FTC.gov.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (Bongard-Blanchy et al.: peer-reviewed ACM DIS; regulatory sources: Gold for compliance reference; Gray et al.: peer-reviewed ACM CHI — top HCI venue; overall Silver due to dependence on survey self-report for behavioral claim percentages)
- **Reconciler Note**: Prior version cited "EU Omnibus Directive (2022/2/EU) Art. 6a" as the legal basis — wrong on both directive number and article. Directive number corrected to (EU) 2019/2161 (Modernisation/Omnibus). Article corrected to UCPD Annex I point 7. Note: Art. 6a *does* exist in the amended UCPD, but it governs **online marketplace transparency** (ranking disclosure, trader status) — not scarcity. "FTC enforcement actions against Cure.fit (2022)" removed — no substantiated enforcement record found for that case; replaced with the verifiable FTC 2022 "Bringing Dark Patterns to Light" staff report.

### Finding 5: Real Inventory-Scarcity Messages Can Reduce Daily Purchase Quantities — The Bon-Ton Study

- **Source**: Park, S., Rabinovich, E., Tang, C.S., & Yin, R. "The Impact of Disclosing Inventory Scarcity Information on Demand." Working paper (SSRN). Accessible summary: UCLA Anderson Review — https://anderson-review.ucla.edu/inventory-scarcity. Related technical note (same authors + Yu): "Should an Online Seller Post Inventory Scarcity Messages?" *Decision Sciences* (2020). <https://doi.org/10.1111/deci.12402> (the 17.6% figure traces to the JOM-adjacent working paper; the Decision Sciences paper is an extension). Final peer-reviewed publication: Park, S., Rabinovich, E., Tang, C.S., & Yin, R. (2020). *Journal of Operations Management*, 66(5), 534–552. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/joom.1082
- **Methodology**: Analysis of sales data from Bon-Ton, a large US department-store retailer, covering 199 products sold online between August 1, 2014 and January 31, 2016. The retailer displayed a "Five or less left in stock" message when inventory fell below six units. Researchers compared prices, sales amounts, transaction times, and inventory levels before and after the messages appeared, isolating the effect of the scarcity message on daily purchase frequency and quantity.
- **Key Finding**: The low-stock messages increased purchase *frequency*, but **daily purchase *quantities* fell 17.6% (from 0.77 units per day to 0.63 units per day)**. The quantity decline offset the frequency increase, leading to an overall drop in daily sales for slow-moving products. Effect was product-dependent: fast-moving items (above-average sales velocity) were largely unchanged; slow movers bore the full quantity-decline effect. **Mechanism interpretation**: customers shown "Five or less left" on a slow-moving product may have read the signal as "nobody wants this, it's being cleared out" (negative quality/popularity inference) rather than "popular and about to sell out" (positive urgency inference) — the commodity theory quality-inference mechanism running in reverse.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Important correction of a commonly-misattributed statistic. The "17.6% sales decrease from fabricated 'Only X left'" number that circulates in e-commerce writing comes from THIS paper — and it is NOT about fabricated scarcity. It is about REAL inventory-scarcity messages backfiring on slow-moving products. Operationally: (1) Audit which products generate the scarcity signal — if it's disproportionately slow-movers, disable the message for those SKUs; (2) Threshold calibration matters — "5 or less" may fire too early on products where that quantity will sit for weeks, reinforcing the clearance interpretation; (3) Pair scarcity signals with positive demand signals ("Selling fast" + "Only 3 left") to make the urgency-not-clearance interpretation dominant; (4) Reserve "X left in stock" messaging for products with demonstrated sales velocity where the scarcity is visibly credible.
- **Replication Status**: Single large-dataset field study on one retailer. Direction (quantity decline for slow movers) is consistent with commodity-theory boundary conditions in Lynn 1991 and Barton et al. 2022. Not independently replicated. Peer-reviewed in *Journal of Operations Management* (2020 — Gold-tier Wiley journal).
- **Boundary Conditions**: Single retailer (department store); single scarcity-message format ("Five or less left"); single 18-month date window; single retail category mix. Effect may not generalize to: fashion/luxury/perishable categories; other message formats ("Going fast," "Only 3 left," "Just restocked"); mobile vs. desktop presentations; retailers with different brand positioning. The key generalizable insight is mechanism-level: whether the scarcity signal reads as "urgency" or "clearance" depends on the product's perceived velocity and the message framing.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (peer-reviewed *Journal of Operations Management*, Wiley; single-retailer field data; findings not yet independently replicated)

### Finding 6: Multi-Jurisdictional Convergence on False-Scarcity Enforcement

- **Source**: Multi-jurisdictional primary/regulatory source set, including FTC Act § 5: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/45. Full source list:
  - FTC Act § 5: 15 U.S.C. § 45 — https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/45
  - FTC (2022). "Bringing Dark Patterns to Light" (staff report) — https://www.ftc.gov/reports/bringing-dark-patterns-light
  - UCPD Annex I point 7 (false-limited-availability per-se prohibition) — https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2005/29/oj (Annex I)
  - Directive (EU) 2019/2161 (Omnibus/Modernisation — raised UCPD penalties) — https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2019/2161/oj
  - EU DSA Regulation 2022/2065 Art 25 (platform dark patterns) — https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2065/oj
  - California UCL/FAL (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 17200, 17500) — https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayText.xhtml?division=7.&chapter=5.&lawCode=BPC
- **Methodology**: Regulatory-document cross-reference. Primary sources: US FTC enforcement record, Eur-Lex official journal texts, California legislature.
- **Key Finding**: False scarcity and false urgency claims are independently actionable under **at least four regulatory regimes**, providing no jurisdictional safe harbor: (a) **US federal — FTC Act § 5** prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices"; the FTC's 2022 Bringing Dark Patterns to Light report explicitly classifies false urgency/scarcity as deceptive practice; (b) **EU — UCPD Annex I point 7** per-se prohibits falsely stating limited-time availability to elicit immediate decision; Directive 2019/2161 raised maximum penalties to at least 4% of annual EU turnover for widespread violations; (c) **EU platforms — DSA Art 25** specifically bans dark patterns on online intermediary platforms (effective 2024-02-17); penalty cap under DSA Art 52: up to 6% of worldwide annual turnover; (d) **California — UCL/FAL** creates a private right of action for unfair/deceptive business practices, including false scarcity, independent of federal enforcement posture. Notable public enforcement example: Netherlands ACM (2025) required Epic Games to change Fortnite Item Shop countdown timers that "created the false impression that an item or offer may soon run out or disappear" under UCPD Annex I analysis.
- **E-Commerce Application**: Treat false scarcity as a multi-jurisdictional risk that cannot be neutralized by monitoring a single regulatory body: (1) FTC de-prioritization does not remove state UCL or EU UCPD liability; (2) Penalties scale with company size under Directive 2019/2161 (up to 4% EU turnover) and DSA Art 52 (up to 6% worldwide turnover); (3) California UCL private litigation is independent of regulatory enforcement; (4) Platform-intermediated sellers (Amazon, Shopify apps, game stores) face DSA Art 25 exposure as platform operators separately from their own merchant liability. Compliance rule: **all scarcity signals must be verifiable from backend data**. Countdown timers must reference real, non-repeating end-times; stock counts must reflect real inventory. Audit logs of which scarcity signal was shown to which user at which time are recommended as evidence of good-faith practice.
- **Replication Status**: Regulatory-source finding. Enforcement patterns evolve; the primary sources (FTC, Eur-Lex, state AG) are the authoritative current reference.
- **Boundary Conditions**: This finding covers *false/deceptive* scarcity. Truthful scarcity with accurate data is legally protected. State AG enforcement priorities vary by administration. EU Commission enforcement is currently escalating across dark-pattern categories; US federal enforcement intensity in retail dark patterns is lower than in 2021–2023 but remains available to state AGs under parens patriae provisions. California UCL private-plaintiff exposure is independent of any regulatory posture.
- **Evidence Tier**: Silver (primary regulatory sources directly referenceable; FTC, EU Commission, and CA AG enforcement archives constitute authoritative documentation; Silver rather than Gold because enforcement patterns are evolving and jurisdiction-specific outcomes remain pending)

## Methodological Notes

1. **CRO Finding 9 Update**: The Bose (34% CTR lift) and Obvi (7–8% conversion lift) case studies in CRO Finding 9 were from Bronze-tier practitioner sources. Finding 2 here (Hmurovic et al. 2023, Gold tier) provides the first rigorous evidence that time scarcity in online retail now *underperforms* for experienced shoppers. The practitioner lift numbers likely reflect early implementations before consumer persuasion knowledge fully developed in the market. Treat the 7–34% lift range as an upper bound for naive/new customer segments; expect diminishing returns as customer sophistication increases.

2. **Quantity vs. Time (Updated)**: Both meta-analyses (Barton et al. 2022 and Lynn 1991) support prioritizing **quantity** scarcity over **time** scarcity for physical product e-commerce. Quantity limits are perceived as more credible (inventory is finite), harder to fake at scale, and trigger less persuasion-knowledge activation. However: Finding 5 (Park et al.) shows that even real quantity messages can backfire on slow-moving products — treat quantity scarcity as powerful but conditional on product velocity and message framing that signals demand rather than clearance.

3. **The Ethical Framework**: The scaffolded file's "Ethical Urgency Framework" is correct in principle. This file adds empirical grounding: the N = 406 awareness and unacceptability data (63% rate false scarcity highest-unacceptability), the correctly identified UCPD Annex I point 7 legal regime, the multi-jurisdictional convergence in Finding 6, and the consumer detection infrastructure (browser extensions, review sites) that makes fake scarcity increasingly self-defeating.

4. **Attribution Hygiene (Reconciler 2026-04-22)**: The widely-circulated "17.6% fake scarcity decline" statistic originates with Park, Rabinovich, Tang & Yin (Bon-Ton data, Finding 5) and is NOT about fake/fabricated scarcity. It is about REAL inventory-scarcity messages producing a quantity decline on slow-moving products. When using this statistic in audits, cite the Park et al. JOM paper (2020) and characterize it correctly: the finding is about message-framing backfire on slow movers, not fake-scarcity harm.

5. **Regulatory Citation Correction (Reconciler 2026-04-22)**: The prior live version of this file cited "EU Omnibus Directive (2022/2/EU) Art. 6a" as the legal basis for prohibiting false scarcity — wrong on both the directive identifier and the article. The correct primary legal basis is **UCPD 2005/29/EC Annex I, point 7** (per-se blacklist; no balancing required). The Omnibus/Modernisation Directive is **(EU) 2019/2161**, which amended the UCPD and raised penalties; it is not a standalone instrument with its own "Art. 6a" on scarcity. Article 6a *does* exist in the amended UCPD, but it governs **online marketplace transparency obligations** (ranking disclosure, trader vs. private-seller status) — not false scarcity messaging.
